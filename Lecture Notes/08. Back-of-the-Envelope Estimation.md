# Back-of-the-Envelope Estimation for System Design

> Beginner-first HLD study notes for turning users and product behavior into approximate QPS, latency, concurrency, CPU cores, RAM, storage, network throughput, server count, and load-balancer requirements. The goal is not precise prediction. The goal is to discover which constraints can change the design and to make every assumption inspectable.

## Scope, terminology, and evidence labels

The supplied rough bullets were:

- RAM;
- core;
- memory;
- server count;
- whether a load balancer is needed, and at what throughput;
- latency; and
- queries per second.

### Terminology assumption

`RAM` and `memory` are often synonyms. To avoid covering the same bullet twice:

- **RAM** means volatile working memory used by processes, requests, connections, caches, and the operating system.
- **Persistent storage** is how this chapter interprets the separate `memory` bullet: database/object bytes retained on SSD, HDD, or a managed storage service.

If a future interviewer says "memory," clarify which one they mean.

### Evidence labels used in this chapter

- **Verified technical guidance**: an original paper, official documentation, or first-party engineering material linked nearby.
- **Current product-specific example**: a provider's documented unit or quota checked on **2026-07-16**. It may change and is not a universal hardware limit.
- **Self-reported interview experience**: a candidate publicly described a round. It shows question style, but company attribution, exact wording, and evaluation cannot be independently verified.
- **Illustrative estimate/design**: invented round numbers used to teach the method. It is not a claim about a named company's private architecture or present traffic.

Every throughput or capacity number in a worked example is either derived from stated assumptions or labeled hypothetical. There is deliberately no table claiming "PostgreSQL handles X QPS" or "one server handles Y users." Code, queries, payloads, hardware, data shape, connection behavior, and latency targets can change those values by orders of magnitude.

## 1. Plain-language foundation

### 1.1 What is a back-of-the-envelope estimate?

Imagine planning a wedding dinner. Before choosing the kitchen, you do not need to know whether 347 or 351 plates will be used. You do need to know whether the event has:

- 10 guests;
- 300 guests; or
- 30,000 guests arriving in one hour.

Those scales require different plans.

A **back-of-the-envelope estimate**, also called a **BOTE**, **napkin estimate**, **Fermi estimate**, or rough **capacity estimate**, is quick arithmetic that places a system in the right order of magnitude:

> Is this tens, thousands, millions, or billions of operations/bytes/connections, and which resource is likely to become the constraint?

It is not a purchase order and not a performance guarantee.

### 1.2 The core reasoning chain

Most useful estimates follow the same chain:

```text
product behavior
    |
    v
operations and bytes per user/event
    |
    v
average rate -> peak rate -> failure/retry rate
    |
    v
resource demand: CPU, RAM, storage, network, connections
    |
    v
architecture decisions and risks
    |
    v
benchmark, load test, observe, revise
```

For example:

```text
10 million users each read 20 feeds/day
        |
        v
200 million reads/day
        |
        v
~2,300 average reads/s; perhaps ~12,000 at an assumed peak
        |
        v
CPU + cache + database-query + response-byte demand
        |
        v
number/type of app instances, cache strategy, LB and DB plan
```

The arithmetic matters only if the last two arrows affect the design.

### 1.3 Estimate, benchmark, load test, and capacity plan are different

| Activity | Question it answers | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Back-of-envelope estimate | "Roughly what scale and bottleneck should I expect?" | Assumptions and simple arithmetic |
| Microbenchmark | "How fast is this operation/component under controlled conditions?" | Measured experiment |
| Load test | "How does the production-like system behave under a realistic workload and beyond it?" | End-to-end measurement |
| Capacity plan | "What should we provision over time, including growth, failures, deploys, quotas, and cost?" | Forecast + tests + production history |

An estimate chooses what to test. A test replaces guessed per-node capacity with evidence. Production measurements update the plan.

AWS Well-Architected guidance recommends benchmarking and data-driven architecture choices, and specifically recommends production-like load tests at expected load **and beyond it** to find bottlenecks ([AWS Performance Efficiency](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/wellarchitected/latest/framework/performance-efficiency.html); [AWS load-testing guidance](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/wellarchitected/2024-06-27/framework/perf_process_culture_load_test.html)). Google SRE similarly says to establish resource-to-capacity ratios by load testing rather than tradition ([Google SRE, *Service Best Practices*](https://sre.google/sre-book/service-best-practices/)).

### 1.4 Inline prerequisite: orders of magnitude

An order of magnitude is a factor of about ten:

| Value | Scientific form | Spoken form |
|---:|---:|---|
| 1,000 | `10^3` | thousand |
| 1,000,000 | `10^6` | million |
| 1,000,000,000 | `10^9` | billion |
| 1,000,000,000,000 | `10^12` | trillion |

For BOTE work, `8.6 x 10^4` seconds/day can be rounded to `10^5` if only the order of magnitude matters. Use `86,400` when a cleaner accurate result is easy.

Useful mental anchors:

```text
1 minute = 60 seconds
1 hour   = 3,600 seconds
1 day    = 86,400 seconds ~= 100,000 seconds
30 days  ~= 2.6 million seconds
1 year   ~= 31.5 million seconds
```

### 1.5 Inline prerequisite: bits, bytes, and rate units

- One **byte (B)** is 8 **bits (b)**.
- Network rates are commonly written in bits/second: Mbps, Gbps.
- Payload and storage sizes are commonly written in bytes: KB, MB, GB, TB.
- Therefore, `240 MB/s x 8 = 1,920 Mb/s = 1.92 Gb/s`, before protocol overhead.

Decimal SI units are convenient for BOTE:

```text
1 KB = 1,000 bytes
1 MB = 1,000 KB
1 GB = 1,000 MB
1 TB = 1,000 GB
1 PB = 1,000 TB
```

Binary units use different names:

```text
1 KiB = 1,024 bytes
1 MiB = 1,024 KiB
1 GiB = 1,024 MiB
```

Do not silently mix the two. NIST explicitly reserves the SI prefixes kilo/mega/giga for powers of ten rather than powers of 1,024 ([NIST SI-prefix guidance](https://www.nist.gov/pml/special-publication-811/nist-guide-si-chapter-4-two-classes-si-units-and-si-prefixes)).

### 1.6 Inline prerequisite: rate, latency, throughput, and concurrency

These terms describe different axes:

| Term | Plain meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Arrival/offered rate | Work clients attempt to send per time | 10,000 requests/s arrive |
| Throughput | Work completed per time | 9,800 responses/s complete |
| Goodput | Useful, correct work completed within its goal | 9,500 non-timeout successful responses/s |
| Latency | Time one operation spends end to end | p95 = 220 ms |
| Concurrency | Work active/in flight at once | 1,500 open requests |
| Utilization | Fraction of a finite resource in use | CPU = 65% |
| Saturation | Demand exceeds prompt service capacity; a queue forms | DB pool wait grows |

The average number of in-flight operations can be estimated with **Little's Law**:

```text
concurrency ~= throughput x average time in system

At 10,000 requests/s and 150 ms average latency:
concurrency ~= 10,000 x 0.150 = 1,500 requests
```

The relationship is about long-run averages in a stable system; it does not say that every instant has exactly 1,500 requests. The original result is John Little's `L = lambda W` ([Little, 1961](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/opre.9.3.383)).

### 1.7 Inline prerequisite: CPU, core, vCPU, thread, and CPU time

- A **CPU/socket** is a physical processor package.
- A **physical core** can execute instructions independently.
- A hardware **thread** is a logical execution context on a core; two hardware threads do not necessarily provide twice the performance.
- A **vCPU** is a cloud/provider abstraction. Its mapping is provider- and instance-family-specific.
- A software **thread** is scheduled onto the CPU by the operating system.
- **CPU time** is time actually executing on a CPU, not time waiting for a network or database.

For example, AWS documents that many EC2 types use simultaneous multithreading and expose each hardware thread as a vCPU; one documented `m5.xlarge` mapping is two physical cores with two threads each, producing four vCPUs ([AWS EC2 CPU options](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/AWSEC2/latest/UserGuide/instance-optimize-cpu.html)). Treat this as AWS/product-specific documentation, not a definition for every cloud.

A request can take 200 ms wall-clock time while consuming only 3 ms of CPU because it spends most of its life waiting:

```text
request wall time: | CPU 1ms |--- wait for DB 190ms ---| CPU 2ms |
CPU demand:          3ms
latency:           193ms
```

That distinction is central to estimating cores versus concurrency/RAM.

### 1.8 Inline prerequisite: RAM versus persistent storage

RAM:

- is fast, byte-addressable working memory;
- holds program code, heap, stacks, buffers, caches, connection state, and OS page cache;
- is normally lost when the machine/process disappears; and
- is constrained by live working state, not only stored business data.

Persistent storage:

- holds data expected to survive restarts;
- is sized by records/objects, retention, indexes, replication, and backups;
- may have independent capacity, throughput, and IOPS limits; and
- is not made "large enough" merely because total bytes fit.

A 10 TB database does not require 10 TB of application RAM. It may, however, benefit from a RAM working set that holds hot indexes/pages.

## 2. Why estimation is needed in system design

### 2.1 It turns vague scale into design pressure

"Design Twitter" is too vague. These produce very different pressures:

- 1,000 users occasionally reading text;
- 10 million users refreshing feeds;
- one celebrity broadcasting to 50 million followers;
- images/videos dominating bytes;
- users keeping persistent notification connections; or
- a traffic spike concentrated in 30 seconds.

Estimation tells you whether the dominant problem is:

- CPU per operation;
- network bytes;
- storage growth;
- live connections/RAM;
- database read or write work;
- hot-key/fan-out skew;
- latency and queuing; or
- failure headroom.

### 2.2 It justifies architecture, including simplicity

Good estimation can justify **not** adding technology:

> "Peak traffic is about 80 RPS and the data is 30 GB after a year. One properly tested application instance plus a standby and a managed relational database meet the target. I do not need sharding."

It can also reveal a specialized path:

> "API traffic is only 5,000 RPS, but video egress is 200 Gb/s. App servers are not the media-delivery plane; use object storage and a CDN."

### 2.3 It exposes impossible or incomplete requirements early

Examples:

- p99 end-to-end latency is 100 ms, but a mandatory third-party call already takes p99 250 ms;
- one region must survive another region's loss, but each normally runs at 70% of maximum;
- all 5 PB of retained data must fit in one node's RAM;
- a fan-out operation creates 10 million synchronous writes inside one user request.

The estimate is valuable even before it produces a server count.

### 2.4 It is not prophecy

An estimate cannot predict:

- an unmeasured query plan;
- lock contention;
- garbage-collection pauses;
- a bad deploy doubling CPU;
- hot partitions;
- retry storms;
- provider throttles;
- code-path changes; or
- the exact p99 latency at a particular utilization.

Google SRE warns that requests with the same "QPS" label can have radically different resource costs, and describes direct resource consumption such as CPU time as a better signal when request mixes vary ([Google SRE, *Handling Overload*](https://sre.google/sre-book/handling-overload/)). Therefore:

> **QPS describes how often. Cost per request describes how much. You need both.**

## 3. A repeatable estimation workflow

### 3.1 Begin with decisions, not a ritual

Ask: "Which numbers might change my architecture?"

For a URL shortener, estimate redirect QPS, database/cache work, and retained mappings. For a video service, upload/transcode/storage/egress bytes are dominant. For chat, messages/s and simultaneous connections matter. Do not calculate every possible number just because a template lists it.

### 3.2 Step 1: write assumptions with units

A compact assumption table prevents invisible guesses:

| Input | Assumption | Why it matters |
|---|---:|---|
| DAU | 10 million users/day | Starting population |
| Reads/user/day | 20 | External read volume |
| Writes/user/day | 1 | Write volume |
| Peak factor | 5x reads, 10x writes | Uneven traffic |
| Read response | 20 KB compressed | Egress/LB/network |
| Write request | 2 KB | Ingress |
| Retention | 1 year | Storage |
| Read CPU time | 3 ms/request | Core demand |
| Write CPU time | 8 ms/request | Core demand |
| Average latency | 150 ms | In-flight concurrency |

Say "I will use round illustrative values; please correct any product assumption."

### 3.3 Step 2: draw the critical path

An external request can create many internal operations:

```text
Client
  |
  | 1 API request
  v
Load balancer
  |
  v
Feed service
  |---- 1 cache lookup
  |---- on miss: 2 DB queries
  |---- 1 authorization RPC
  '---- 1 analytics event
```

Do not call all of these "QPS" without a qualifier. Use:

- client/API RPS;
- cache operations/s;
- database queries/s or transactions/s;
- downstream RPC/s;
- broker messages/s.

### 3.4 Step 3: convert totals to average and peak rates

```text
average operations/s = operations/day / 86,400

peak operations/s = average operations/s x stated peak factor
```

Prefer a product-specific peak model if known:

```text
peak rate = operations occurring in busiest window / window seconds
```

Example: 1 million ticket attempts in a 2-minute sale:

```text
1,000,000 / 120 ~= 8,300 attempts/s
```

Dividing by the whole day would give only ~12/s and would be dangerously irrelevant.

### 3.5 Step 4: translate rate into each resource

| Resource | First-pass formula |
|---|---|
| External/API rate | users x actions/user / time |
| Internal query rate | API rate x operations/request, adjusted for cache/branch probabilities |
| Network | operations/s x bytes/operation |
| Persistent storage | new objects/time x bytes/object x retention multipliers |
| Concurrency | throughput x average latency |
| CPU cores at 100% | rate x CPU seconds/operation |
| RAM | base + live requests + connections + cache + runtime/OS overhead |
| Load-only server count | resource demand / measured safe capacity per server |

### 3.6 Step 5: account for the traffic shape

At minimum, state:

- average versus peak;
- read/write mix;
- cheap versus expensive request mix;
- payload distribution, not only average;
- steady traffic versus bursts;
- hot keys/tenants/regions;
- retries and cache misses;
- background jobs; and
- growth horizon.

### 3.7 Step 6: add safety and failure policy exactly once

Avoid vague multiplication such as:

```text
capacity x 2 for peak x 2 for safety x 2 for HA x 2 just in case
```

Instead separate:

1. **Demand model:** peak offered load.
2. **Safe per-node capacity:** benchmark point where latency/error targets still pass, perhaps at a chosen resource-utilization target.
3. **Failure policy:** survive one instance, one zone, or one region.
4. **Operational reserve:** deployments, autoscaling delay, growth until the next provisioning cycle.

If the safe benchmark already uses a 60% CPU target, do not silently add another "40% CPU headroom" multiplier.

### 3.8 Step 7: make the number cause a decision

End the estimate with statements like:

- "Text metadata fits comfortably in one database for year one, so I postpone sharding."
- "Images add petabyte-scale annual storage, so they go to object storage and a CDN."
- "CPU, not RAM or NIC, drives the app fleet."
- "The database sees 8,400 statements/s, not merely the 13,200 API RPS."
- "Multiple interchangeable servers require a health-aware traffic distributor."
- "The p95 target is impossible unless the synchronous third-party call is removed or its contract changes."

If no number changes a decision, stop estimating and move to the design.

## 4. Queries per second (QPS) and requests per second (RPS)

### 4.1 QPS is overloaded terminology

People use QPS for several things:

- external user requests;
- search queries;
- SQL statements;
- cache operations; or
- logical business transactions.

Use precise labels:

```text
API peak:          12,000 requests/s
cache peak:        12,000 GET/s
DB read peak:       4,800 SELECT/s
DB write peak:      3,600 statements/s
business writes:    1,200 transactions/s
```

A transaction containing three SQL statements is one business operation but three statement executions. Replication and index maintenance add more internal work without increasing client-visible QPS.

### 4.2 From DAU to average RPS

Suppose:

```text
10 million DAU
20 feed reads per user per day

reads/day = 10,000,000 x 20 = 200,000,000
average RPS = 200,000,000 / 86,400 ~= 2,315 RPS
```

This is an average, not a capacity target.

### 4.3 Peak factor is an assumption, not a law

If no traffic curve is supplied:

```text
assumed peak read RPS = 2,315 x 5 ~= 11,600 ~= 12,000 RPS
```

Mark "5x" as assumed. A globally distributed product may have a flatter curve; a lunch-delivery app, live match, flash sale, or alarm at the top of the hour may be far spikier.

Better sources, in order:

1. observed per-minute/per-second production histogram;
2. comparable product event data;
3. a product-behavior window such as "30% happens in 10 minutes";
4. an explicit interview assumption.

### 4.4 Read and write QPS

Calculate them separately because their cost and scaling mechanisms differ:

```text
read RPS  = DAU x reads/user/day  / seconds/day
write RPS = DAU x writes/user/day / seconds/day
```

Reads may use cache/CDN/replicas. Writes may require validation, transactions, indexes, replication, and event publication.

### 4.5 Internal fan-out

Suppose a cache hit rate is 80%, and each miss issues two DB reads:

```text
DB read QPS
  = API read RPS x miss rate x DB reads/miss
  = 12,000 x 0.20 x 2
  = 4,800 DB reads/s
```

If each write transaction executes three statements:

```text
DB write statements/s
  = 1,200 writes/s x 3
  = 3,600 statements/s
```

Important caveats:

- cache hit rate may be key-count-weighted rather than byte/work-weighted;
- a cache outage changes the miss rate from 20% toward 100%;
- one "query" can scan one row or a billion rows;
- a request can conditionally call different paths;
- batch queries can reduce QPS while increasing bytes/query.

### 4.6 Weighted request cost

If request types have different costs:

```text
CPU demand
  = sum(rate_i x CPU_seconds_i)

DB demand
  = sum(rate_i x DB_operations_i)
```

Example:

| Route | Peak rate | CPU/request | CPU core-equivalent |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Feed read | 12,000/s | 3 ms | 36.0 |
| Create post | 1,200/s | 8 ms | 9.6 |
| **Total** | **13,200/s** | mixed | **45.6 cores at 100%** |

One global "13,200 QPS" hides the write route's higher cost.

### 4.7 QPS can be the wrong unit

Use another unit when it matches the bottleneck:

| Workload | Better primary unit |
|---|---|
| Video delivery | Gb/s and concurrent streams |
| Chat gateway | open connections and messages/s |
| Batch ETL | bytes/s, records/s, completion deadline |
| ML inference | tokens/s, GPU memory, batch occupancy |
| Image processing | images/s by resolution/codec |
| Database | transactions/s plus rows/bytes/IOPS |
| Queue consumer | messages/s plus backlog age |

## 5. Latency, concurrency, and queues

### 5.1 Average is not a user-experience guarantee

For sorted latency observations:

- p50: half completed at or below this value;
- p95: 95% completed at or below it;
- p99: 99% completed at or below it.

An average can look healthy while a small but important percentage waits seconds. State an SLO such as:

```text
p95 API latency <= 250 ms
p99 API latency <= 600 ms
error rate <= 0.1%
```

These are illustrative, not universal targets.

### 5.2 Latency is both a requirement and a capacity input

By Little's Law:

```text
13,200 requests/s x 0.150 s average
  = 1,980 average in-flight requests
```

If a latency regression raises the average to 1 second at the same throughput:

```text
13,200 x 1 = 13,200 in-flight requests
```

That increases live request objects, sockets, thread/async state, connection-pool pressure, and queues even before traffic grows.

Use **average** latency with average throughput for a Little's Law average. Multiplying peak RPS by p99 latency can be a conservative scenario, but it is not literally the theorem's mean relationship; label it as a stress bound.

Do not invert latency and call the result an entire server's throughput. A 200 ms operation allows one strictly serial execution slot to complete at most about `1 / 0.2 = 5 operations/s`. With 100 independent concurrent slots, the same latency could coexist with roughly 500 operations/s if CPU, memory, connections, and dependencies support it. Latency, concurrency, and resource cost jointly determine throughput.

### 5.3 Build an end-to-end latency budget

Illustrative p95 target: 250 ms.

| Stage | Planning budget |
|---|---:|
| Client network/edge | 60 ms |
| TLS/LB/routing | 10 ms |
| App queue + application work | 30 ms |
| Cache/DB/downstream wait | 80 ms |
| Serialization/transfer | 20 ms |
| Unallocated risk buffer | 50 ms |
| **Total budget** | **250 ms** |

This is a budget, not valid percentile arithmetic. In general:

> **Do not add component p99 values and claim the result is end-to-end p99.**

Percentiles do not add cleanly because the slow events may occur on different requests and dependencies may be correlated. Measure the end-to-end distribution and use tracing to attribute it.

### 5.4 Sequential and parallel stages

Sequential stages approximately add for one request:

```text
A 20 ms -> B 40 ms -> C 30 ms
request path ~= 90 ms plus queue/network overhead
```

Parallel fan-out waits roughly for the slowest required branch:

```text
             +--> A 20 ms --+
request -----+--> B 80 ms --+--> join ~= at least 80 ms
             +--> C 30 ms --+
```

Fan-out amplifies tail risk. Under a simplifying independence assumption, if each of 100 required subrequests has a 99% chance to meet a threshold:

```text
P(all 100 meet threshold) = 0.99^100 ~= 36.6%
```

Real dependencies are often correlated, so this is an illustration, not a production prediction. Google's *The Tail at Scale* explains why rare component delays become important in large fan-out services ([Google Research](https://research.google/pubs/the-tail-at-scale/)).

### 5.5 Why planning at 100% utilization fails

When arrival rate approaches service capacity, small bursts and service-time variation create queues. Queue time becomes part of latency; timeouts cause retries; retries add load.

An intentionally simplified single-server M/M/1 model gives:

```text
mean time in system = mean service time / (1 - utilization)
```

With 10 ms mean service time:

| Utilization | Modelled mean total time |
|---:|---:|
| 50% | 20 ms |
| 80% | 50 ms |
| 90% | 100 ms |
| 95% | 200 ms |

The model assumes Poisson arrivals, exponential service, one server, steady state, and other conditions that real systems often violate. Use it only to build intuition: latency is nonlinear near saturation.

### 5.6 Deadlines, timeouts, queues, and backpressure

- A **deadline** says when the entire operation is no longer useful.
- A **timeout** bounds one wait/call.
- A **bounded queue** limits waiting work and memory.
- **Backpressure** slows/rejects upstream work when downstream cannot keep up.
- **Load shedding** rejects low-priority/excess work early to preserve useful work.

Google SRE recommends testing beyond capacity and protecting servers from overload rather than allowing queues, latency, memory, and retries to cascade ([Google SRE, *Addressing Cascading Failures*](https://sre.google/sre-book/addressing-cascading-failures/)).

## 6. Estimating CPU cores

### 6.1 The foundation

One fully busy core supplies approximately:

```text
1 CPU-second of execution per wall-clock second
```

Therefore:

```text
cores at 100% = requests/s x CPU-seconds/request
```

Example:

```text
10,000 requests/s x 0.005 CPU-second/request
  = 50 CPU-seconds/second
  = 50 fully utilized core-equivalents
```

At a chosen 60% CPU planning target:

```text
planned cores = 50 / 0.60 ~= 84 cores
```

"60%" is a policy assumption to preserve latency/burst/failure room, not a universal best value.

### 6.2 Mixed workload formula

```text
CPU core demand = sum(route_rate x CPU_time_per_route)

Illustrative feed:
read:  12,000/s x 0.003 s = 36.0 cores
write:  1,200/s x 0.008 s =  9.6 cores
total at 100%                  = 45.6 cores
at 60% target: 45.6 / 0.60    = 76 vCPU-equivalents
```

If using 8-vCPU instances of the **same type used for the measurement**:

```text
load-only instances = ceil(76 / 8) = 10
```

This is not yet the production fleet count; failure domains and deploy reserve come later.

### 6.3 CPU time must be measured

Good sources:

- profiling under representative data;
- CPU time by route from production tracing/profiling;
- load tests on the planned instance type;
- performance regression tests.

Weak sources:

- wall-clock latency;
- language choice alone;
- "a core handles 1,000 requests/s";
- a benchmark of different code, payload, hardware, runtime, or connection model.

### 6.4 What consumes CPU

- business logic and algorithms;
- JSON/Protobuf parsing and serialization;
- encryption/TLS;
- compression/decompression;
- image/video transformation;
- authentication/cryptography;
- database query execution;
- garbage collection and runtime work;
- kernel/network processing;
- logging and telemetry;
- retries and rejected requests.

Compression demonstrates a trade-off: it can reduce network bytes while increasing CPU and latency.

### 6.5 Why cores do not scale linearly forever

Doubling cores may produce less than 2x goodput because of:

- serial sections and global locks;
- memory-bandwidth/cache contention;
- shared connection pools;
- database/downstream limits;
- garbage collection;
- uneven request distribution;
- hyperthread/vCPU behavior;
- NUMA and scheduler effects; or
- the application simply lacking enough parallel work.

Google Research measurements show that OS, scheduling, interrupt routing, NUMA, and background interference can materially affect tail latency even at the same utilization ([*Tales of the Tail*](https://research.google/pubs/tales-of-the-tail-hardware-os-and-application-level-sources-of-tail-latency/)).

### 6.6 CPU-bound versus I/O-bound

| Workload | Likely immediate constraint | Scaling thought |
|---|---|---|
| Video transcoding | CPU/GPU | cores/accelerators per job |
| JSON API waiting on DB | DB/latency/connections, sometimes CPU | optimize dependency and measure CPU separately |
| TLS gateway | CPU + connections + network | handshake rate and reuse matter |
| In-memory ranking | CPU + RAM bandwidth | benchmark cores and data locality |
| File proxy | network + buffers, sometimes CPU | direct object/CDN path may be better |

An I/O-bound service still needs CPU, but adding cores may not help if every request waits on a saturated database.

### 6.7 Common CPU-estimation mistakes

- Using response latency as CPU time.
- Treating a vCPU as identical across providers/generations.
- Ignoring the expensive p99 request-size or route mix.
- Sizing for average CPU while p99 latency already fails.
- Ignoring deploys, sidecars, encryption, logging, and GC.
- Assuming 8 cores deliver exactly 8 times one-core throughput.
- Converting theoretical cores directly into fleet count without a load test.

## 7. Estimating RAM

### 7.1 RAM has several owners

A useful per-instance model is:

```text
RAM required
  = application/runtime base
  + live request/job state
  + connection/session buffers
  + in-process cache
  + queues/batches
  + sidecars/agents
  + OS/kernel/page cache
  + fragmentation/GC/native overhead
  + explicit safety margin
```

Do not allocate 16 GB to the application heap on a 16 GB machine. The operating system and non-heap/native work also need memory.

### 7.2 Base process and runtime

Measure resident memory after startup and under steady traffic. Depending on runtime, distinguish:

- configured heap maximum;
- currently committed heap;
- live objects;
- native/direct buffers;
- thread stacks;
- memory-mapped files;
- loaded code/libraries;
- sidecar proxies and monitoring agents; and
- process **RSS** versus virtual address space.

Virtual memory size is not the same as physical RAM resident now.

### 7.3 Live request memory

First estimate concurrency:

```text
in-flight requests = request rate x average latency
```

Then:

```text
live request RAM = in-flight requests x bytes of live state/request
```

Illustrative:

```text
13,200 RPS x 0.150 s = 1,980 in-flight requests fleet-wide

At an assumed 128 KB/request:
1,980 x 128 KB ~= 253 MB fleet-wide
```

This can be small compared with a cache, but large uploads, unbounded request bodies, aggregation, or slow clients can reverse that conclusion.

### 7.4 Connection memory

Persistent HTTP/2, WebSocket, TCP, and messaging connections consume state even when mostly idle:

```text
connection RAM
  = concurrent connections x measured bytes/connection
```

Assume 200,000 live connections and **hypothetically** 16 KB of combined application/kernel bookkeeping per connection:

```text
200,000 x 16 KB = 3.2 GB fleet-wide
```

The 16 KB assumption is deliberately not a universal number. TLS state, socket buffers, unread data, protocol implementation, kernel tuning, subscriptions, and user-session data can change it greatly. Measure realistic idle and active connections.

### 7.5 Thread and coroutine memory

A thread has a stack reservation/commitment and runtime bookkeeping. A coroutine/fiber/async task is often smaller, but not free. A thread-per-connection model can therefore run out of memory or scheduler capacity before CPU is busy.

Do not assume:

```text
number of threads = number of CPU cores
```

Many threads may wait on I/O; only runnable threads compete for CPU. The safe concurrency model depends on blocking behavior and memory.

### 7.6 Cache memory and working set

The **working set** is the data actively needed during a relevant window. To size a cache:

```text
cache RAM
  ~= hot items x bytes/item including key/value/metadata overhead
```

Illustrative:

```text
2 million hot post records x 1 KB raw = 2 GB raw
assume 2x for keys, object/allocator/index overhead = ~4 GB
```

The multiplier must be measured for the actual cache representation. Serialization, allocator fragmentation, replicas, eviction metadata, and hash-table load factor matter.

A hit-rate target is often better than "cache all data":

1. obtain an item-popularity trace;
2. replay or model it against cache sizes;
3. choose the smallest size that gives useful **work-weighted** hit rate;
4. include node loss, resharding, and warm-up;
5. verify with production metrics.

### 7.7 Queue and batch memory

Unbounded in-memory queues are hidden memory leaks under sustained overload:

```text
queue RAM = queued items x bytes/item
```

If arrivals exceed service rate by 5,000 jobs/s for 60 seconds:

```text
backlog growth = 5,000 x 60 = 300,000 jobs

at 50 KB/job:
RAM growth = 15 GB
```

Use bounded queues, backpressure, durable brokers where appropriate, and an explicit overload policy.

### 7.8 Garbage-collected runtimes need live-set room

If the live object graph almost fills the heap, the collector has little room to work and may collect more frequently or pause longer. A short test that reaches 1.99 GB on a 2 GB budget is not evidence of safe capacity; Google SRE makes this exact capacity-planning point ([Google SRE, *Operational Overload*](https://sre.google/sre-book/operational-overload/)).

The necessary heap-to-live-set ratio is runtime, collector, allocation-rate, and latency-target specific. Benchmark it.

### 7.9 Per-instance illustrative RAM budget

Assume the end-to-end example eventually uses 15 app instances:

| RAM owner per instance | Illustrative value |
|---|---:|
| App/runtime + sidecars | 2.0 GB |
| 132 average in-flight requests x 128 KB | 0.017 GB |
| ~13,300 connections x 16 KB | 0.213 GB |
| Local hot cache | 4.0 GB |
| OS, kernel, page cache, misc. | 1.0 GB |
| **Subtotal** | **~7.23 GB** |
| 25% explicit memory margin | ~1.81 GB |
| **Planning need** | **~9.0 GB** |

A 12-16 GB instance class might be evaluated, depending on available shapes and GC behavior. That is a candidate for load testing, not a guarantee. Notice that the cache, not active request state, dominates this example.

### 7.10 Common RAM-estimation mistakes

- Treating persistent database size as required application RAM.
- Ignoring connections, buffers, sidecars, native memory, or page cache.
- Sizing only for average latency while overload lengthens queues.
- Using serialized object size as in-memory object size.
- Allowing unbounded queues, bodies, result sets, or cardinality.
- Counting replicated local caches as if stored once fleet-wide.
- Ignoring cold-start cache fill and one-node loss.

## 8. Estimating persistent storage ("memory" in the rough bullets)

### 8.1 Start with logical new data

```text
logical bytes/time
  = new objects/time x average bytes/object

retained logical bytes
  = logical bytes/time x retention time
```

Example:

```text
10 million text posts/day x 1 KB/post
  = 10 GB/day
  = 3.65 TB/year logical data
```

### 8.2 Estimate a record field by field

For a post metadata row:

| Field | Illustrative logical bytes |
|---|---:|
| Post ID | 8 |
| User ID | 8 |
| Timestamp | 8 |
| Text average | 500 |
| Counters/status/flags | 32 |
| References and variable-length metadata | 100 |
| **Logical payload** | **~656 B** |

Round to 1 KB to include some schema/row overhead for first-pass arithmetic. Do not confuse this logical size with the eventual on-disk size of a particular engine.

### 8.3 Add storage multipliers separately

```text
provisioned primary storage
  = logical data
  + indexes
  + storage-engine/row overhead
  + compaction/version/tombstone headroom
  + temporary operational space
```

Then reason separately about:

- replicas;
- cross-region copies;
- snapshots/backups;
- transaction/change logs;
- analytics/search copies;
- derived thumbnails/transcodes; and
- legal or archive copies.

Do not multiply everything by one mystery "3x overhead."

Illustrative metadata calculation:

```text
3.65 TB/year logical
x 2.0 assumed primary-store factor for indexes/engine/headroom
= 7.3 TB/year provisioned primary

x 3 durable copies
= 21.9 TB/year across those copies
```

Both multipliers are explicit assumptions. Backups are still separate because incremental backup behavior depends on changed bytes and retention; "30 daily backups" does not automatically mean 30 full copies.

### 8.4 Media changes the answer

Assume 10% of the 10 million daily posts include one compressed 2 MB image:

```text
1 million images/day x 2 MB
  = 2 TB/day
  = 730 TB/year logical image data

with two object copies:
  ~= 1.46 PB/year
```

One assumption turned a few-terabyte metadata problem into a petabyte object-storage problem. This should cause an architecture decision:

- metadata in a database;
- image bytes in object storage;
- delivery through a CDN;
- lifecycle/archive/delete rules;
- quota and abuse controls.

### 8.5 Capacity is not enough: throughput and IOPS

A disk/database may hold all bytes but still fail the workload.

Estimate:

```text
write bytes/s = writes/s x bytes/write x write amplification
read bytes/s  = reads/s x bytes/read after cache
IOPS          = I/O operations/s, affected by access size and locality
```

Also consider:

- random versus sequential I/O;
- read/write ratio;
- index updates;
- compaction;
- fsync/durability latency;
- working-set cache hit rate;
- scan/query amplification;
- checkpoint and backup traffic.

"We only write 2.4 MB/s" can be misleading if each write synchronously touches several random index pages and a replicated log.

### 8.6 Retention, deletion, and growth

Ask:

- Is retention 7 days, one year, or forever?
- Does delete mean logical tombstone or immediate physical removal?
- Is there a legal hold?
- Are old tiers compressed or archived?
- How fast is historical data read?
- Does replication cross regions?
- How much temporary space is needed during compaction/reindexing?
- What is the restore time for the full dataset?

Storage capacity without restore bandwidth and RTO is incomplete.

### 8.7 Common storage-estimation mistakes

- Counting only payload, not indexes/engine/replicas.
- Multiplying daily growth by retention while ignoring deletes/updates.
- Treating backups as either zero bytes or all full copies without checking.
- Putting large blobs in the row database by default.
- Ignoring thumbnails/transcodes and derived indexes.
- Checking TB capacity but not IOPS, bytes/s, or restore time.
- Using average object size when a heavy tail dominates bytes.
- Calling decimal TB and binary TiB interchangeable in a precise budget.

## 9. Estimating network throughput

### 9.1 The basic formula

```text
bytes/s = operations/s x average bytes/operation
bits/s  = bytes/s x 8
```

Illustrative peak API payload:

```text
read egress:
12,000 responses/s x 20 KB
  = 240 MB/s
  = 1.92 Gb/s

write ingress:
1,200 requests/s x 2 KB
  = 2.4 MB/s
  = 19.2 Mb/s
```

These are payload-only estimates. Add measured effects from:

- headers;
- TLS and transport framing;
- retransmissions;
- request bytes and response bytes in both directions;
- health checks;
- service-to-service calls;
- replication and backups;
- logging/telemetry;
- compression ratio.

### 9.2 Separate traffic planes

```text
User/API traffic
      |
      v
edge/LB ---> app ---> cache/database
   |                   |
   |                   '-- replication/backups
   |
   '-- media may bypass app through CDN/object storage
```

Measure at least:

- public ingress;
- public egress;
- LB-to-app;
- app-to-cache/DB;
- database replication;
- cross-zone/region;
- backup/analytics pipelines.

The same user response can cross several billed or capacity-limited network links.

### 9.3 Average payload hides tails

A 20 KB average may consist of:

- many 2 KB responses; and
- a few 20 MB responses.

Large responses consume connection time and buffers and can interfere with small requests. Bound/paginate API responses and route large blobs through appropriate delivery systems.

### 9.4 Compression trade-off

If JSON compresses from 20 KB to 5 KB:

- network demand falls about 4x for that payload;
- LB/egress cost may fall;
- CPU time and possibly latency rise;
- already-compressed media may gain almost nothing.

Use measured compressed-size distributions and CPU cost.

### 9.5 Common network mistakes

- Forgetting bytes-to-bits conversion.
- Counting only inbound traffic.
- Ignoring replication and cross-region traffic.
- Sending media through app servers unnecessarily.
- Comparing payload Gb/s directly with a NIC's nominal line rate.
- Ignoring packets/s, connections/s, TLS handshakes, and retransmissions.
- Assuming CDN removes origin traffic entirely; misses and fill still reach origin.

## 10. Estimating server count

### 10.1 "How many servers?" has no answer without a server role

Count separately:

- load balancers/proxies;
- application/API instances;
- background workers;
- caches;
- database nodes/replicas;
- search nodes;
- message brokers;
- object/media processing workers.

A database node and a stateless API node do different work and cannot share one QPS rule.

### 10.2 Resource-envelope method

For an app instance, estimate the load-only count for every relevant dimension:

```text
N_cpu     = ceil(total CPU demand / safe CPU capacity per node)
N_ram     = ceil(total live RAM demand / safe RAM capacity per node)
N_network = ceil(total network demand / safe network capacity per node)
N_conn    = ceil(total connections / safe connections per node)
N_qps     = ceil(peak QPS / measured safe QPS per node)

N_load = max(N_cpu, N_ram, N_network, N_conn, N_qps)
```

Why maximum rather than sum? Every chosen node contributes all its CPU, RAM, NIC, and connection capacity simultaneously. The first exhausted resource determines the count.

Do not use both `N_cpu` and `N_qps` as independent truth if the safe QPS benchmark is already CPU-limited; they are cross-checks.

RAM sometimes needs a per-node feasibility check rather than simple division because process base memory and a full local-cache copy are repeated on every node:

```text
RAM_per_node(N)
  = fixed base per node
  + shardable/live fleet memory / N
  + local cache copy
  + margin
```

Start with the CPU/network/connection-derived `N`, verify this RAM inequality, and choose a larger-memory shape or revise the cache/concurrency plan if it fails.

### 10.3 Safe per-node capacity

Run a production-like load test and choose the highest rate where all required conditions hold:

- p95/p99 latency passes;
- error rate passes;
- CPU/memory/GC are stable;
- queues do not grow without bound;
- connection pools and dependencies are healthy;
- goodput stays near offered load;
- overload fails safely.

Call that **safe capacity**, not the absolute breaking point.

### 10.4 Add failure capacity with a stated policy

If `N_load = 10`:

- survive any one app-instance loss: at least `10 + 1 = 11`;
- survive two instances being unavailable: at least 12;
- survive one of three equally loaded zones while keeping the same safe target:

```text
N_total x (2/3) >= 10
N_total >= 15
```

This assumes even placement and traffic redistribution. A zonal dependency, uneven load, quota, or cross-zone-disabled LB can violate the model.

For one-of-two-zone survival at the same capacity target, each zone must be able to carry the entire load, which is effectively 2x normal capacity. Reliability policy is expensive; state it rather than hiding it.

### 10.5 Deploy and autoscaling reserve

Rolling deploy example:

- fleet: 15 instances;
- deployment allows 3 temporarily unavailable;
- one instance also fails;
- demand still requires 10 safe instances.

Remaining: `15 - 3 - 1 = 11`, so the policy passes.

Autoscaling does not remove reserve because:

- detection uses a time window;
- provisioning/startup takes time;
- new instances may warm caches/JIT/connection pools;
- provider quotas may block scale-out;
- the database or another dependency may not scale;
- scale-in can oscillate or remove warm capacity.

### 10.6 Worker-count estimation

Separate concurrent job slots from CPU:

```text
simultaneous job slots
  = arrival rate x average wall-clock processing time

CPU core demand at 100%
  = arrival rate x average CPU-seconds/job
```

The first is Little's Law and includes waiting. The second sizes actual compute.

Example:

```text
100 images/s x 2 CPU-seconds/image = 200 busy core-equivalents
```

If one worker instance safely provides 8 effective processing cores:

```text
load-only worker instances = ceil(200 / 8) = 25
```

If jobs also spend time waiting on object storage, their required concurrent job slots can exceed 200 even though CPU demand remains 200 cores.

For batch/backlog recovery:

```text
required processing rate
  = normal arrival rate + backlog / desired drain time
```

### 10.7 Database-node count is different

Database capacity depends on:

- query plans and rows scanned;
- cache/working set;
- transactions, locks, and contention;
- read replicas and tolerated staleness;
- write replication/commit policy;
- storage bytes, IOPS, and throughput;
- partition/key distribution;
- backup/restore;
- failover capacity.

Do not calculate `DB nodes = DB QPS / 10,000` from a memorized number. Benchmark the real schema, queries, and data distribution.

### 10.8 Cost cross-check

For each viable fleet, estimate:

```text
monthly compute-hours ~= instance count x 24 x 30
storage cost          ~= GB-month by tier/copies
network cost          ~= GB transferred by path
managed-service cost  ~= provider billing dimensions
```

Prices are region/provider/time specific, so look them up for a real decision. Cost can make a different architecture preferable even when both meet capacity.

## 11. Is a load balancer needed, and at what throughput?

### 11.1 What a load balancer does

A load balancer accepts traffic and selects a healthy backend:

```text
                        +--> App 1
Client ---> Load balancer ---> App 2
                        +--> App 3
```

It may also:

- terminate TLS;
- perform health checks;
- route by host/path/header;
- enforce connection/time limits;
- attach WAF/auth/rate-limiting features;
- collect access metrics;
- drain connections during deploys; and
- provide a stable endpoint while instances change.

### 11.2 Decision guide

| Situation | Dedicated LB needed? | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Local development | No | One process, no HA |
| One low-risk public server | Optional | Reverse proxy/LB can still give TLS/stable endpoint, but does not create backend HA |
| Two or more interchangeable app instances | Usually yes, or an equivalent distributor | Traffic and health must be routed |
| Serverless/API gateway | Often built into platform | Still capacity/quota/cost constrained |
| CDN/object delivery | CDN is the traffic distributor for that path | Do not route all bytes through app LB |
| Queue consumers | Broker/partition assignment distributes messages | Conventional HTTP LB may be irrelevant |
| Sharded stateful service | Router/client-side discovery may be required | Random balancing can send a key to the wrong owner |
| Peer-to-peer/direct device path | Maybe not on data path | Control/signaling still commonly needs routing |

The key idea:

> If multiple backends can serve the same request, some mechanism must choose one. That mechanism might be a managed LB, proxy fleet, DNS/global traffic manager, client-side load balancing, service mesh, queue, or shard router.

### 11.3 A load balancer is not sized by RPS alone

Estimate:

- requests/s or packets/s;
- new connections/s;
- simultaneous active connections;
- bytes/s in both directions;
- TLS handshakes/s and certificate/key cost;
- protocol: HTTP/1.1, HTTP/2, HTTP/3, TCP, UDP, WebSocket;
- rule/routing evaluations;
- longest connection lifetime;
- health-check and failover behavior;
- zones/regions and failure capacity.

Use Little's Law for connections:

```text
active connections ~= new connections/s x average connection lifetime
```

HTTP keep-alive means requests/s can greatly exceed new connections/s. WebSockets can create huge active-connection counts with modest messages/s.

### 11.4 L4 versus L7

| Layer | Sees | Strength | Cost/limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| L4 network LB | IP, port, TCP/UDP/TLS flow | High-throughput connection/packet routing; protocol-agnostic | Cannot route using HTTP path/header unless TLS/HTTP handled elsewhere |
| L7 application LB/reverse proxy | HTTP host/path/header/cookie and often TLS | Content routing, auth/WAF features, observability | More parsing/state/CPU; HTTP-specific behavior and limits |

Use L7 when HTTP-aware routing/features matter. Use L4 when connection/transport routing is enough or protocol transparency matters. Architectures can use both at different layers.

### 11.5 Managed LB sizing

A managed LB hides machine count, not capacity dimensions, quotas, scaling lag, cost, or failure planning.

As a **current AWS-specific example checked on 2026-07-16**, Application Load Balancer billing/capacity units consider four dimensions:

- new connections/s;
- active connections/minute;
- processed bytes/hour; and
- rule evaluations/s.

One documented LCU currently represents 25 new connections/s, 3,000 active connections/minute (with stated mTLS differences), 1 GB processed/hour for EC2/container/IP targets, or 1,000 rule evaluations/s; usage is governed by the highest dimension ([AWS ELB pricing and LCU details](https://aws.amazon.com/elasticloadbalancing/pricing/)).

These LCU values are **not** a claim that an ALB stops at one LCU or that every load balancer has those limits. They illustrate why "13,000 RPS" is not enough information. AWS says ALBs automatically scale with detected workload, supports minimum-capacity reservation for sudden events, and recommends using load tests or representative historical data to estimate reservations ([AWS ALB capacity reservation](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/elasticloadbalancing/latest/application/capacity-unit-reservation.html)). Account/service quotas and subnet capacity still matter ([AWS ALB quotas](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/elasticloadbalancing/latest/application/load-balancer-limits.html)).

### 11.6 Self-managed proxy count

Assume a production-like test of **your exact proxy configuration** shows one proxy safely handles:

```text
8,000 RPS
1.0 Gb/s processed traffic
60,000 active connections
while meeting latency/error goals
```

For:

```text
13,200 RPS
1.94 Gb/s
200,000 active connections
```

the load-only counts are:

```text
by RPS:         ceil(13,200 / 8,000)  = 2
by bandwidth:   ceil(1.94 / 1.0)      = 2
by connections: ceil(200,000 / 60,000)= 4

load-only proxies = max(2, 2, 4) = 4
```

Add explicit failure/zone capacity. The numbers above are hypothetical benchmark results, not NGINX/HAProxy/Envoy universal capacities.

### 11.7 Long-lived connections change balancing

When a WebSocket connects, it normally stays with the selected backend:

```text
Client ===== long connection =====> Gateway 2
```

Adding Gateway 4 does not automatically move existing connections. Scale-out mostly receives new connections; rebalancing may require graceful disconnect/reconnect, connection draining, or a protocol-aware layer. Balance by connection count and per-connection activity, not round-robin requests alone.

### 11.8 LB failure and architecture

Do not draw one self-managed LB box and declare high availability:

```text
Bad:                          Better abstraction:

clients -> one proxy -> apps  clients -> redundant managed/proxy endpoint -> apps
             ^
             single point
```

Ask:

- Is the LB itself multi-zone/redundant?
- How does DNS/global routing find it?
- What happens when health checks are wrong?
- Are connections drained on deploy?
- Can one zone receive all traffic after failure?
- Can backends authenticate that traffic came through the LB?
- Does the LB preserve client IP/trace context safely?

## 12. End-to-end worked example

### 12.1 Product and assumptions

We will size an **illustrative social-feed API**, not any named company's real system.

| Input | Assumption |
|---|---:|
| DAU | 10 million |
| Feed reads/user/day | 20 |
| New posts/user/day | 1 |
| Peak factor | 5x reads, 10x writes |
| Read response | 20 KB compressed |
| Write request | 2 KB |
| Read CPU time | 3 ms |
| Write CPU time | 8 ms |
| Average request latency | 150 ms |
| Cache hit rate | 80% |
| DB reads per cache miss | 2 |
| SQL statements per write transaction | 3 |
| Active client connections | 200,000 |
| App instance candidate | 8 vCPU, at 60% CPU planning target |
| Availability | survive loss of one of three equal zones |

### 12.2 External rates

```text
reads/day = 10M x 20 = 200M
average read RPS = 200M / 86,400 ~= 2,315
peak read RPS = 2,315 x 5 ~= 11,575 ~= 12,000

writes/day = 10M
average write RPS = 10M / 86,400 ~= 116
peak write RPS = 116 x 10 ~= 1,160 ~= 1,200

peak API RPS ~= 13,200
```

All later resource calculations intentionally carry the rounded peak values of 12,000 reads/s and 1,200 writes/s. Reusing the unrounded intermediate values would produce slightly different results without changing any design decision.

Decision: separate read/write calculations; read traffic dominates request count.

### 12.3 Internal database rate

```text
DB reads/s
  = 12,000 x (1 - 0.80) x 2
  = 4,800 SELECT/s

business write transactions/s = 1,200
DB write statements/s = 1,200 x 3 = 3,600
```

Decision: test the DB with the real 4,800-read/3,600-statement mix, data distribution, indexes, and transaction contention. Protect it against a cold-cache path that could approach:

```text
12,000 x 2 = 24,000 DB reads/s
```

### 12.4 CPU and app instances

```text
read CPU  = 12,000 x 0.003 = 36.0 core-seconds/s
write CPU =  1,200 x 0.008 =  9.6 core-seconds/s
total     =                       45.6 cores at 100%

at 60% target = 45.6 / 0.60 = 76 vCPU-equivalents
load-only 8-vCPU instances = ceil(76/8) = 10
```

To retain 10 safe instances after losing one of three equally sized zones:

```text
total x 2/3 >= 10
total >= 15 instances, placed 5 per zone
```

Decision: CPU drives a 15-instance multi-zone app fleet under these assumptions. A real load test could reveal a different safe count or a non-CPU bottleneck.

### 12.5 Concurrency and RAM

```text
in-flight requests
  = 13,200/s x 0.150 s
  = 1,980 fleet-wide
  = ~132 per instance across 15 instances
```

Using the explicit RAM assumptions from Section 7 gives ~9 GB planning need per instance after a 25% memory margin; evaluate a 12-16 GB shape.

Decision: local cache dominates app RAM; request concurrency does not. If average latency grows or responses/uploads become large, recalculate.

### 12.6 API network and LB

```text
read egress = 12,000 x 20 KB = 240 MB/s = 1.92 Gb/s
write ingress = 1,200 x 2 KB = 2.4 MB/s = 19.2 Mb/s
```

Protocol/headers/other responses are omitted, so round the payload estimate upward for a test.

Decision:

- an LB/equivalent is needed for 15 replaceable app instances and multi-zone health routing;
- validate RPS, ~2 Gb/s payload traffic, new connections/s, 200,000 active connections, TLS, and zone failure;
- use CDN/object delivery for large media rather than adding it to this app-LB estimate.

### 12.7 Persistent storage

Text/metadata:

```text
10M posts/day x 1 KB = 10 GB/day = 3.65 TB/year logical
x 2 assumed primary factor = 7.3 TB/year
x 3 copies = 21.9 TB/year across copies
```

Images under the stated optional-media assumption:

```text
1M images/day x 2 MB = 2 TB/day = 730 TB/year logical
x 2 object copies = 1.46 PB/year
```

Decision: database for metadata, object storage for media, CDN for delivery, retention/lifecycle policy for both.

### 12.8 Latency budget and overload

Illustrative p95 target: 250 ms. Allocate a budget across edge, LB, app queue/work, cache/DB, serialization, and risk; then measure end to end.

Protect the design with:

- per-hop deadlines;
- bounded request and connection queues;
- retry budgets with jitter and idempotency;
- cache request coalescing;
- rate limits/admission control;
- load shedding/degraded feed;
- autoscaling with warm capacity;
- a test for one-zone loss and cold cache.

### 12.9 Resulting illustrative architecture

```text
                             +----------------------+
                             | CDN / object storage |<--- image bytes
                             +----------^-----------+
                                        |
Clients ---> DNS/edge ---> managed/redundant LB
                              |
                    +---------+---------+
                    | 15 stateless apps |  5 per zone
                    +----+----------+---+
                         |          |
                         v          v
                    distributed   metadata DB
                       cache       + replicas
                         |
                         v
                   queue/analytics workers
```

The diagram follows from estimates; it is not proof that each component meets them. Tests and production measurements close that gap.

### 12.10 Sensitivity analysis: what changes the answer most?

| Changed assumption | Effect |
|---|---|
| Read CPU rises from 3 ms to 6 ms | Read core demand doubles; app fleet grows unless optimized |
| Cache hit falls from 80% to 50% | DB read rate rises from 4,800 to 12,000/s |
| Average latency rises 150 ms -> 1 s | In-flight requests rise 1,980 -> 13,200 |
| Read response falls 20 KB -> 5 KB with compression | Payload egress falls ~1.92 -> ~0.48 Gb/s, at extra CPU cost |
| Media ratio 10% -> 1% | Logical image growth falls 730 -> 73 TB/year |
| Need one-of-two-zone survival | Normal provisioned app capacity approaches 2x load need |
| Flash peak is 50x, not 5x | Average-derived app plan is invalid; pre-scale/shedding/event plan needed |

Sensitivity analysis is often more useful than adding decimal precision.

## 13. Real-product workload examples

The products below are real, but the diagrams and calculations are **illustrative workload models**. They are not claims about current private production architecture or traffic.

### 13.1 WhatsApp: connections and message fan-out

The wrong first question is only "how many HTTP QPS?"

```text
Sender ===== persistent connection =====> Messaging gateway
                                            |
                                            v
                                      durable routing
                                       /     |      \
                                      v      v       v
                                  device A device B device C
```

Estimate:

- concurrently connected devices;
- bytes and RAM per connection;
- new connections/s after a regional network recovery;
- messages/s by type and size;
- recipient fan-out, including groups and multi-device delivery;
- offline queue bytes and retention;
- acknowledgements/retries;
- end-to-end delivery latency.

One sender message can create multiple delivery attempts. A load balancer must handle long-lived connections; adding gateways does not redistribute old connections automatically.

### 13.2 YouTube: ingest, processing, storage, and egress are different planes

```text
Creator -> upload -> object storage -> transcode jobs -> many renditions
                                                     |
Viewer  <- CDN/edge <--------------------------------+
```

Estimate:

- uploaded video minutes/s and input bitrate;
- number of output codecs/resolutions;
- transcode CPU/GPU seconds per input minute;
- source + rendition storage;
- viewer concurrent streams;
- delivered Gb/s by region and cache hit rate;
- startup/rebuffer tail latency.

The API request rate for "play" can be modest compared with media bytes. YouTube has publicly described specialized video infrastructure and a CDN cache-eviction system, reinforcing the separation between control/API work and media delivery ([YouTube infrastructure article, 2021](https://blog.youtube/inside-youtube/new-era-video-infrastructure/); [Google Research YouTube CDN paper, 2023](https://research.google/pubs/halp-heuristic-aided-learned-preference-eviction-policy-for-youtube-content-delivery-network/)). These are dated public snapshots, not current capacity specifications.

### 13.3 Uber: location updates versus trip transactions

```text
Driver phones -- location pings --> location ingestion/store
Rider app ----- trip request ----> matching ------> driver offer
Payments ------------------------> durable ledger/order path
```

Estimate separately:

- online drivers x pings/second;
- location-event bytes/s and short retention;
- nearby-search query rate and geographic hot spots;
- trip-request peak RPS;
- matching computation and fan-out;
- notification connections;
- payment/trip-state write correctness and latency.

Location pings may dominate write rate while completed trips dominate correctness requirements. They need not use the same store or retention.

### 13.4 Netflix: control plane versus video bytes

```text
App/API path:  viewer -> API -> catalog/personalization/playback control
Video path:    viewer <================ CDN/cache <==== origin
```

Estimate:

- browsing/API RPS and personalization fan-out;
- concurrent streams by resolution/bitrate;
- regional egress Gb/s;
- catalog/artwork cache behavior;
- play-start p95/p99;
- origin-fill bandwidth and cache locality.

Netflix describes Open Connect as its content-delivery network and documents ISP/edge delivery publicly ([Netflix Open Connect](https://openconnect.netflix.com/en/)). The design lesson is that routing video through ordinary API instances would make the wrong fleet absorb the dominant bytes.

### 13.5 Twitter/X: average feed load versus celebrity skew

```text
ordinary author -> fan-out/write caches -> follower feeds

celebrity author -> huge follower set
                      |
                      +-> hybrid/on-read handling or partitioned fan-out
```

Estimate:

- timeline reads/s;
- posts/s;
- followers per author distribution, not only average;
- fan-out writes per post;
- hot-post cache read rate;
- feed object bytes and retention;
- read latency during viral events.

`average followers x posts/s` can be misleading because the distribution is heavy-tailed. One celebrity post can be a different workload class requiring a hybrid fan-out strategy.

### 13.6 Amazon: ordinary browsing versus flash-sale inventory

```text
catalog reads ---> cache/search/read path

checkout ---> inventory reservation ---> order ---> payment
                  ^ serialized/hot SKU correctness
```

Estimate:

- product-page/search RPS and payload bytes;
- checkout attempts in the busiest seconds;
- hot-SKU contention;
- downstream calls per order;
- reservation timeout and abandoned carts;
- write amplification to order/payment/inventory/events;
- retry/idempotency behavior.

A flash sale can have moderate final orders/s but enormous attempts/s on one SKU. The critical dimension is contention and admission control, not only total fleet CPU.

### 13.7 Cross-product comparison

| Product shape | Dominant first estimates | Common trap |
|---|---|---|
| WhatsApp-like chat | connections, messages/s, fan-out, offline bytes | treating connections as short HTTP requests |
| YouTube-like video | ingest/transcode/storage/egress Gb/s | sizing only API QPS |
| Uber-like mobility | location events, geo skew, match latency | mixing ephemeral telemetry with trip transactions |
| Netflix-like streaming | concurrent streams, bitrate, CDN hit/origin fill | proxying media through API fleet |
| X-like feed | reads, fan-out distribution, hot keys | using average follower count |
| Amazon-like commerce | browse reads, peak attempts, hot inventory, correctness | sizing from completed orders rather than attempts |

## 14. Trade-offs, alternatives, and when to avoid detailed estimation

### 14.1 Accuracy versus interview time

| Approach | Benefit | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Skip all numbers | More architecture time | Unjustified scale claims; missed dominant axis |
| Two-minute dominant estimates | Usually enough to anchor design | May omit a follow-up dimension |
| Detailed resource model | Useful for infrastructure/capacity roles | Can consume the entire interview |
| Exact-looking arithmetic from guessed inputs | None beyond rough scale | False precision and fragile decisions |

Recommended interview default:

1. ask whether the interviewer wants explicit estimates;
2. calculate peak external rate, dominant bytes/storage, and one resource that changes the design;
3. state the decision;
4. offer to deepen into cores/RAM/server/LB if useful.

### 14.2 Vertical versus horizontal compute

| Scale up | Scale out |
|---|---|
| Simpler coordination and debugging | Incremental capacity and instance redundancy |
| Useful when one node still fits | Requires routing, replaceable/stateless compute, distributed operations |
| Hardware ceiling and larger failure blast radius | Shared DB/lock can remain bottleneck |

Estimation shows whether one tested node is enough; reliability can still justify a second even when capacity does not.

### 14.3 Provisioned versus elastic capacity

| Provisioned warm capacity | Autoscaling/elasticity |
|---|---|
| Handles immediate spikes and failures | Reduces idle cost for slow/predictable changes |
| More idle cost | Scale lag, quotas, warm-up, dependency limits |
| Stable caches/JIT/connections | Can churn and create cold-load pressure |

Use both: a safe warm floor plus autoscaling for growth, with overload protection.

### 14.4 In-process cache versus distributed cache

| In-process | Distributed |
|---|---|
| Lowest network latency; per-node memory | Shared larger working set |
| Duplicate/stale copies across fleet | Network hop and cluster dependency |
| Lost/warmed on every instance | Central hot-key/partition/failover concerns |
| Can reduce shared-cache traffic | Easier global invalidation than many local copies |

RAM estimation must include replication of local caches.

### 14.5 Managed versus self-managed load balancer

| Managed | Self-managed proxy fleet |
|---|---|
| Provider operates nodes/scaling/health integration | Full configuration/control/portability |
| Product quotas, opaque internals, provider pricing | You own HA, upgrades, scaling, CVEs, capacity |
| Reserve/pre-scale mechanisms vary | Benchmark exact instances/config and add failure nodes |

For most ordinary application HLDs, a managed multi-zone LB is a sensible default unless protocol, cost, portability, or control requirements demand otherwise.

### 14.6 When not to calculate a server count

Avoid pretending to know a concrete count when:

- no CPU/latency/per-node benchmark is available;
- the architecture decision depends only on order of magnitude;
- the provider abstracts instances and exposes a concurrency/quota unit instead;
- the database query plan/data distribution is unknown;
- request mix is unspecified;
- interviewer asks to move to the design.

Say:

> "The formula is peak demand divided by measured safe per-instance capacity, then adjusted for zone failure and deploy reserve. Without a representative benchmark I can estimate resource demand, but a precise node count would be invented."

That is stronger than choosing a magic QPS/server.

### 14.7 When estimation itself can be harmful

- False precision can create confidence in bad assumptions.
- Average-based planning can understate burst/hot-key risk.
- Overprovisioning every layer can waste cost without improving the real bottleneck.
- Exact fleet math can distract from correctness, data ownership, and failure behavior.
- A count can become stale after code/hardware/request-mix changes.

Use estimates as revisable models, not commitments carved in stone.

## 15. Edge cases and common mistakes

### 15.1 Mistake: beginning with DAU and ending at average QPS

```text
DAU -> requests/day -> average QPS -> stop
```

What is missing:

- peak window;
- time zones and product schedule;
- expensive route mix;
- retries;
- background work;
- failures;
- hot keys;
- payload bytes.

Correct response:

> "Average is ~2,300 read RPS. I will design and test against an assumed 5x peak of ~12,000, and I will call out that a flash event needs a separate burst model."

### 15.2 Mistake: users equal requests

One user may:

- make zero requests today;
- open one connection for hours;
- generate hundreds of requests from polling;
- trigger fan-out to millions of recipients;
- upload gigabytes in one request.

Always translate user behavior into operations/bytes/connections.

### 15.3 Mistake: all requests have equal cost

A health check, cached profile read, full-text search, and video transcode are not comparable QPS. Split classes or compute weighted resource demand.

### 15.4 Mistake: external QPS equals database QPS

Internal work changes with:

- cache hit/miss;
- read replicas;
- N+1 queries;
- transaction statements;
- index updates;
- fan-out;
- replication;
- asynchronous event consumers.

Draw the critical path and multiply each branch.

### 15.5 Mistake: assuming the cache's steady-state hit rate during failure

At 80% hits, the normal DB estimate may be 20% of API reads. During cache failure/cold start, it can approach 100%, often focused on hot keys.

Protect the DB with:

- request coalescing/single flight;
- stale serving;
- gradual warm-up;
- admission control/rate limiting;
- bounded concurrency;
- fallback degradation;
- cache-rebuild priority.

### 15.6 Mistake: retries are free

If 10,000 original requests/s each average 1.3 attempts:

```text
actual attempts = 10,000 x 1.3 = 13,000/s
```

If multiple layers each retry three times, attempts can grow combinatorially. Retry only safe/idempotent operations as appropriate, use deadlines, exponential backoff with jitter, a retry budget, and one responsible layer.

### 15.7 Mistake: CPU percentage alone sizes an I/O-bound service

CPU can be 25% while:

- DB connection pool is exhausted;
- downstream p99 is slow;
- lock wait is high;
- memory is swapping;
- NIC/packet limit is hit;
- thread pool is blocked;
- one shard is hot.

Use saturation signals for every finite resource and trace latency.

### 15.8 Mistake: latency percentiles add

Do not say:

```text
service A p99 100ms + service B p99 100ms = end-to-end p99 200ms
```

That arithmetic does not generally describe a percentile. Use budgets for planning, measure the end-to-end distribution, and inspect correlation/fan-out.

### 15.9 Mistake: server count equals peak QPS divided by a magic number

The same server could handle:

- many simple in-memory reads;
- far fewer cryptographic/compression operations;
- almost no giant scans while meeting a strict p99.

Estimate CPU/RAM/network/connections, then use measured safe goodput.

### 15.10 Mistake: replication only multiplies storage

Replication also affects:

- write network;
- commit latency;
- CPU;
- IOPS;
- failure behavior;
- consistency/staleness;
- read capacity;
- cross-region cost.

### 15.11 Mistake: "LB needed because QPS is high"

An LB can be needed at low QPS for availability and replaceable instances. Conversely, a high-throughput partitioned batch system may use broker partitions rather than an HTTP LB.

Tie the LB to routing/health/TLS/HA needs, then validate throughput dimensions.

### 15.12 Mistake: ignoring LB connection shape

Two workloads with 10,000 RPS:

| Workload | Connection behavior | Likely pressure |
|---|---|---|
| HTTP/2 keep-alive | Few reused connections | requests/bytes/rules |
| Short TLS requests | Many new connections | handshakes/new connections/s |
| WebSocket | Mostly messages over long connections | active connections/RAM |

Same RPS, different LB capacity.

### 15.13 Mistake: adding redundancy without a failure-domain equation

"Three replicas for HA" is incomplete. Ask:

- three instances across how many zones?
- can remaining instances carry full peak at safe utilization?
- does storage have a quorum?
- does the network/LB route around loss?
- are dependencies also redundant?

### 15.14 Mistake: ignoring deploys

Capacity can fail during:

- rolling updates;
- schema migrations;
- cache flushes;
- JVM/runtime warm-up;
- certificate rotation;
- resharding;
- reindexing.

Size the operational state, not only the quiet steady state.

### 15.15 Mistake: bytes fit, therefore storage is fine

Check:

- read/write IOPS;
- sequential/random throughput;
- compaction and index build;
- backup/restore window;
- hot partition;
- record/object count and metadata overhead;
- per-request latency.

### 15.16 Mistake: counting derived media once

One uploaded video can produce:

- multiple resolutions;
- multiple codecs;
- audio tracks;
- thumbnails;
- previews;
- subtitles;
- regional/cache copies.

Estimate expansion factor explicitly.

### 15.17 Mistake: false precision

Prefer:

> "About 12k peak read RPS from 10M DAU under a 5x assumption."

Avoid:

> "The service will receive exactly 11,574.074 RPS."

The inputs are not that precise.

### 15.18 Subtle scenario: coordinated reconnect storm

After a mobile carrier or region recovers, millions of clients may reconnect together:

```text
steady state: low new connections/s, many active
recovery:     enormous new TLS connections/s + authentication + state restore
```

Use randomized reconnect backoff, admission control, staged recovery, cached auth where safe, and reserved/warm gateway capacity.

### 15.19 Subtle scenario: hot tenant breaks an even fleet estimate

Total CPU may be fine while one tenant/shard consumes most of a node. Estimate distributions and per-key/tenant limits; use load-aware routing, key splitting, isolation cells, or dedicated capacity where justified.

### 15.20 Subtle scenario: cron/top-of-hour synchronization

If every client refreshes at 00 seconds:

```text
one-minute total / 60
```

still understates the first-second burst. Add jitter to schedules and examine sub-minute histograms.

### 15.21 Subtle scenario: autoscaling the app overloads the DB

More instances create:

- more DB connections;
- more cache cold misses;
- more retries;
- higher parallel query pressure.

Cap fleet-wide dependency concurrency and scale/protect the dependency, not only the caller.

## 16. Interview angle

### 16.1 How estimation shows up

An interviewer may ask you to:

- estimate average and peak QPS from DAU;
- separate reads and writes;
- estimate storage and bandwidth;
- decide whether data fits on one node or needs partitioning;
- estimate cores/RAM/app servers;
- decide whether/where an LB is needed;
- find the bottleneck after one assumption changes;
- explain why a magic per-server QPS is invalid;
- reason about one-zone failure or a cache outage;
- fit estimates into a time-limited HLD answer.

They are usually evaluating:

- assumption clarity;
- unit correctness;
- order-of-magnitude sense;
- ability to connect numbers to decisions;
- awareness of peak/skew/failure;
- willingness to validate guesses with measurement;
- communication under ambiguity.

These are **self-reported public candidate experiences**, not verified company question banks:

   **What it demonstrates:** some rounds can explicitly drill mental capacity math far deeper than a normal HLD.

   **What it demonstrates:** interviewer steering overrides a memorized template.

3. **LinkedIn SWE, reported from Bangalore:** the candidate could not share the HLD prompt, but reports discussion of scale, capacity estimation, load-balancing algorithms, Redis/caching, and sync versus async server communication ([LeetCode report](https://leetcode.com/discuss/interview-experience/4767240/LinkedIn-or-SWE-or-Bangalore/)).

   **What it demonstrates:** capacity can be interleaved with component trade-offs rather than isolated arithmetic.

   **What it demonstrates:** even correct estimation has an opportunity cost.

   **What it demonstrates:** calculations must cause design decisions quickly.

There is genuine disagreement among experienced engineers about how much estimation belongs in a 45-minute design interview. The safe behavior is:

> **Offer a compact estimate, ask/observe whether the interviewer wants depth, and never ignore explicit steering.**

### 16.3 Two-minute interview version

> "I will estimate only the dimensions that can change the design. Assuming 10 million daily users, 20 reads and one write per user per day, that is about 2.3k average reads and 116 average writes per second. With assumed 5x/10x peaks, I will plan around 12k reads and 1.2k writes per second. At 20 KB per read, API egress is roughly 2 Gb/s. With an 80% cache hit and two DB reads per miss, the DB sees about 4.8k read queries/s normally, but I will protect it for a cold-cache event. I would derive app count from measured CPU time and safe per-node load-test capacity, then add zone/deploy reserve; multiple instances need a redundant health-aware LB sized for RPS, bytes, new and active connections. The major storage decision is that media, unlike text metadata, belongs in object storage behind a CDN."

This answer uses numbers to justify components and leaves room for deep dives.

### 16.4 Answer spine: **S-I-Z-E-D**

Use these cues to reconstruct the reasoning live.

#### S - Scope the operation and success target

- Which product flow: browse, write, upload, message, stream?
- DAU, concurrency, region, retention, growth horizon?
- p95/p99 latency, availability, durability?
- Which number could change the design?

#### I - Inputs with units and labels

- actions/user/time;
- read/write ratio;
- average and tail payload;
- CPU time/operation if supplied or measured;
- average latency;
- retention/replicas;
- peak/burst assumption.

Write units beside every number.

#### Z - Zoom from average into peak, skew, and failure

- busiest window, not daily average alone;
- hot key/tenant/celebrity/SKU;
- cache miss/outage;
- retries/reconnects;
- zone loss/deploy/autoscaling delay.

#### E - Estimate the resource envelope

- API and internal QPS;
- bytes/s and storage growth;
- concurrency via rate x time;
- CPU via rate x CPU time;
- RAM ownership;
- per-role count via demand / measured safe capacity.

#### D - Decide, defend, and describe validation

- which resource is dominant?
- LB, cache, object store, replicas, queue, sharding: which number justifies each?
- what simpler design is enough?
- what benchmark/load/failure test replaces the guess?
- what changes if one assumption is 10x?

### 16.5 Example opening answer

> "Before calculating, I want to distinguish registered users, daily active users, concurrent connections, and requests. I'll assume 10 million DAU for one region unless you prefer another scale. I'll estimate the peak read/write paths, payload bandwidth, retained data, and concurrency because those decide cache, object storage, app fleet, and LB shape. I can estimate cores and exact server count only from CPU time or a representative per-node benchmark, so if neither is supplied I will show the formula rather than invent a magic server capacity."

### 16.6 Sourced versus synthesized questions

The reports in Section 16.2 are sourced. The questions below are **our own plausible synthesis** for practice; they are not attributed to a company.

#### "Ten million DAU make 20 reads/day. What is average and peak QPS?"

Answer cues:

- `200M reads/day`;
- divide by `86,400` -> ~2.3k average RPS;
- ask/assume traffic curve;
- 5x illustrative peak -> ~12k RPS;
- state that a flash window can invalidate the multiplier.

#### "A route consumes 10 ms CPU at 10,000 RPS. How many 8-vCPU instances?"

Answer cues:

```text
10,000 x 0.010 = 100 cores at 100%
at 60% target = 100/0.60 ~= 167 vCPU
load-only instances = ceil(167/8) = 21
```

Then add explicit zone/deploy/failure capacity and verify with a load test. Do not call 21 the final fleet.

#### "The response latency is 200 ms. Does every request consume 200 ms of CPU?"

Answer cues:

- no; wall-clock includes network/queue/dependency wait;
- CPU time comes from profiling;
- latency does estimate in-flight concurrency;
- 10k RPS x 0.2 s = 2k average in flight.

#### "The cache hit rate is 90%. Can the DB be sized for 10%?"

Answer cues:

- normal-path estimate may use 10%;
- safe failure design cannot assume it;
- cold cache/outage can approach 100%;
- request coalescing, stale data, concurrency limits, admission control, gradual warm-up;
- hit rate should be workload/byte weighted and per route/key class.

#### "One million WebSockets each need 32 KB. What follows?"

Answer cues:

```text
1,000,000 x 32 KB = 32 GB raw connection state fleet-wide
```

- add app/kernel/TLS/session overhead and margin;
- divide by measured safe connections/gateway;
- LB capacity needs active and new connections, not just messages/s;
- model reconnect storm and connection draining;
- shard/routable session ownership if gateways keep state.

#### "Do we need a load balancer at only 100 RPS?"

Answer cues:

- capacity alone: possibly one server;
- availability/deploy/TLS/stable endpoint may justify LB + at least two backends;
- LB without redundant backend does not create app HA;
- managed gateway/CDN/platform may already provide distribution;
- stateful/sharded requests may need a different router.

#### "The service has 2 Gb/s traffic. How many LBs?"

Answer cues:

- need RPS, new/active connections, protocol/TLS/rules, zone policy;
- for managed LB, check current service dimensions, quotas, reserve/pre-scale process;
- for self-managed, divide every dimension by measured safe per-proxy capacity, take max, add failure nodes;
- avoid a universal number.

#### "Why can't I add two p99 latencies?"

Answer cues:

- percentiles may refer to different requests and correlated events;
- sequential per-request times add, but percentile of sum needs joint distribution;
- use a latency budget for planning;
- measure end-to-end p99 and use traces/fan-out analysis.

#### "CPU is 30%, but p99 latency is terrible. What do you inspect?"

Answer cues:

- dependency and DB latency;
- queue/thread/connection-pool wait;
- locks/hot shard;
- memory/GC/page faults;
- network/retransmissions;
- only one core/thread saturated while average CPU appears low;
- trace critical path and check per-resource saturation.

#### "How do you size for one-zone loss across three equal zones?"

Answer cues:

- find safe load-only count first;
- remaining two zones must carry it;
- `N_total x 2/3 >= N_load`;
- for `N_load=10`, `N_total>=15` with even placement;
- validate LB redistribution and zonal dependencies/quotas.

#### "Storage is 10 GB/day. Is one year just 3.65 TB?"

Answer cues:

- that is logical payload;
- add indexes/engine/temp space;
- replicas/cross-region copies;
- backup/change rate;
- derived/search/analytics copies;
- check IOPS/throughput/restore, not bytes alone;
- subtract lifecycle deletion/compression only when justified.

#### "Traffic increases 10x. Does server count increase exactly 10x?"

Answer cues:

- if same mix and one parallel CPU resource is limiting, roughly;
- fixed overhead may amortize; cache hit and batching may improve;
- DB/lock/hot partition may hit a nonlinear ceiling;
- latency/queues can worsen before throughput;
- rebenchmark and identify the new bottleneck;
- architecture may change instead of multiplying every node.

#### "What if my mental arithmetic is wrong?"

Answer cues:

- write formula and units first;
- use powers of ten and round;
- sanity-check in a second direction;
- state order of magnitude;
- correct openly: design judgment matters more than pretending;
- ask whether a calculator is allowed in real work; interviews vary.

### 16.7 Interviewer follow-up map

```text
QPS estimate
  |--> "What is peak?" ---------> burst model / observed window
  |--> "DB QPS?" --------------> cache miss + fan-out + statements
  |--> "How many servers?" ----> CPU/RAM/net/conn envelope + benchmark
  |--> "Need an LB?" ----------> multi-backend routing + HA + TLS
  |--> "LB throughput?" -------> RPS + new/active conn + bytes + protocol
  |--> "What is latency?" -----> budget + concurrency + queuing + percentiles
  |--> "What fails?" ----------> cache outage / zone loss / retry storm
  '--> "10x growth?" ----------> sensitivity + new bottleneck
```

### 16.8 Red flags in an answer

- "One server handles 10k QPS" without workload or benchmark.
- Exact decimals from invented inputs.
- No peak or burst model.
- No distinction between API, DB, transaction, and message rates.
- Treating latency as CPU time.
- Counting only process heap as RAM.
- Counting only logical payload as stored bytes.
- Converting TB to server count without IOPS/throughput.
- Adding p99 values.
- Saying "use an LB" without active/new connections, bytes, health, and HA.
- Sizing every component for average steady-state cache hits.
- Multiplying by several overlapping safety factors.
- Spending ten minutes calculating without stating one design consequence.

## 17. Compact revision sheets

### 17.1 Formula card

```text
TIME
seconds/day  = 86,400 ~= 10^5
seconds/year ~= 31.5 million

TRAFFIC
average RPS = operations/day / 86,400
peak RPS    = average RPS x explicit peak factor
             OR busiest-window operations / window seconds

INTERNAL WORK
dependency ops/s = route RPS x branch probability x ops/request
DB reads/s       = read RPS x cache miss rate x reads/miss

NETWORK
bytes/s = ops/s x bytes/op
bits/s  = bytes/s x 8

CONCURRENCY
average in flight = throughput/s x average latency_seconds
active connections ~= new connections/s x average lifetime_seconds

CPU
cores at 100% = sum(route RPS x CPU_seconds/route)
planned cores = cores at 100% / target utilization

RAM
base + in_flight x bytes/request
     + connections x bytes/connection
     + cache + queues + sidecars + OS + margin

STORAGE
logical growth = objects/time x bytes/object
primary storage = logical + indexes + engine/temp/headroom
total copies = primary x explicit replication/copy policy
backups/derived data calculated separately

SERVER COUNT
N_load = max(N_cpu, N_ram, N_network, N_connections, measured_N_qps)
N_final = N_load adjusted for explicit zone/failure/deploy policy

BACKLOG
drain rate required = normal arrival rate + backlog / drain deadline
```

### 17.2 Unit sanity card

| Check | Question |
|---|---|
| Rate | Did numerator and denominator use the same time window? |
| Network | Did I convert bytes to bits only when needed? |
| Latency | Did I convert ms to seconds before Little's Law/CPU math? |
| Storage | Is KB decimal or KiB binary, and does precision matter? |
| CPU | Is this CPU time or wall-clock time? |
| Count | Did I round instances up, not down? |
| Copies | Did I separate primary overhead, replicas, backups, derived data? |
| Peak | Is the factor stated and product-sensitive? |

### 17.3 Decision card

| Estimate says... | Likely design implication |
|---|---|
| Small load and dataset | Keep architecture simple; still add required redundancy |
| Read repetition dominates | Cache/CDN/read replicas, with miss/outage plan |
| Large media bytes dominate | Object storage + CDN/direct transfer |
| CPU dominates | Optimize/profile, more cores/instances or accelerators |
| Live connections dominate | Connection-aware gateways/LB and RAM planning |
| DB writes/contended keys dominate | Data model/partitioning/batching/serialization plan |
| Bursts exceed steady capacity | Queue where deferrable; pre-scale/admission/shedding |
| Tail latency misses near saturation | Lower utilization, remove fan-out/queues, isolate workload |
| One zone must fail safely | Capacity equation for remaining zones |
| Count depends on magic benchmark | Stop and request/plan representative load test |

### 17.4 Five-number interview minimum

For most product HLDs, start with only:

1. peak read RPS;
2. peak write RPS;
3. dominant bytes/s or storage growth;
4. concurrency if requests/connections are long-lived;
5. the resource/result that changes one design decision.

Deepen into cores, RAM, server/LB count only if data or interviewer focus supports it.

### 17.5 Estimation checklist

- [ ] I named the operation and time window.
- [ ] I separated DAU, concurrency, connections, and requests.
- [ ] I wrote assumptions and units.
- [ ] I separated average from peak/burst.
- [ ] I separated reads/writes and cheap/expensive routes.
- [ ] I translated API requests into cache/DB/RPC/message work.
- [ ] I used payload bytes for network and retained bytes for storage.
- [ ] I used CPU time, not response latency, for cores.
- [ ] I used average latency for average in-flight concurrency.
- [ ] I included base, request, connection, cache, queue, OS, and margin in RAM.
- [ ] I calculated every server role separately.
- [ ] I used measured safe capacity rather than a magic per-node QPS.
- [ ] I added a stated zone/failure/deploy policy exactly once.
- [ ] I evaluated LB RPS, bytes, new/active connections, TLS, protocol, and HA.
- [ ] I considered cache outage, retry/reconnect storm, and hot keys.
- [ ] Every calculation causes a decision or is removed.
- [ ] I named the benchmark/load/failure test that validates the estimate.

## 19. Say-out-loud 30-second narrative

> "Back-of-the-envelope estimation turns product behavior into the few numbers that can change my design. I state assumptions and units, convert actions into average and peak read/write rates, then expand external requests into cache, database, and downstream work. I estimate bandwidth as rate times payload, storage as retained logical data plus explicit indexes and copies, concurrency as rate times average latency, and CPU as rate times measured CPU time. RAM includes the process, requests, connections, cache, queues, OS, and margin. Server and load-balancer counts come from the maximum measured safe resource demand, then explicit zone and deployment headroom - never a magic QPS number. Finally, I state which decision the estimate justifies and how a load or failure test will validate it."

## 20. True prerequisite gaps to study next

There is **no blocking prerequisite gap for understanding or performing the interview-level calculations in this chapter**; the necessary units, CPU/memory distinctions, Little's Law, percentiles, and failure-capacity logic were introduced inline.

For real implementation-level capacity work, the remaining prerequisite gaps are:

- **Performance testing and observability:** workload modelling, profiling, load generation, saturation metrics, tracing, goodput, and regression detection.
- **Database/storage internals:** indexes and query plans, transactions/locks, page cache, IOPS/throughput, compaction, replication, backup and restore.
- **Network/server concurrency:** TCP/TLS/HTTP connection reuse, socket buffers, async versus thread-per-request models, proxies, and connection draining.
- **Applied queueing and statistics:** distributions, percentile measurement, confidence intervals, coordinated omission, and tail amplification.

These should be studied next before treating a calculated server count as deployable production capacity.
