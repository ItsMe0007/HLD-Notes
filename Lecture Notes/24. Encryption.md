# Encryption for System Design (HLD)

> Beginner-first study notes expanded from the supplied bullets. The goal is to reason from the data and threat model to a secure design, not to memorize a list of algorithm names.

## Scope, assumptions, and important corrections

These notes cover the cryptography a general software/system-design candidate is expected to reason about: terminology, symmetric and asymmetric cryptography, AES internals, Diffie-Hellman, digital signatures, TLS, encryption at rest, end-to-end encryption, envelope encryption, and key management.

The mathematical walkthroughs explain *why* the primitives work. They are not instructions to implement production cryptography yourself. In production, use a mature, reviewed protocol and cryptographic library.

Your bullets have the right topic map, with these corrections:

1. The usual spelling is **cipher**. `Cypher` is an uncommon variant. A cipher is an algorithm for transforming plaintext and ciphertext under a key.
2. **Plaintext does not have to be visible text.** A JPEG, video segment, database row, or random binary key is called plaintext whenever it is the unencrypted input.
3. **DSA is a digital-signature algorithm, not an encryption algorithm.** Current NIST FIPS 186-5 no longer approves DSA for generating new signatures; it retains it only to verify older signatures ([NIST FIPS 186-5](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/fips/186-5/final)).
4. **Diffie-Hellman and ECDHE are key-agreement mechanisms, not data-encryption algorithms.** They let two parties derive shared keying material. A symmetric cipher such as AES then encrypts the data.
5. A raw Diffie-Hellman exchange is **not authenticated** and is vulnerable to a man-in-the-middle attack. A complete protocol binds the exchange to an identity, often with a certificate and digital signature.
6. DES has a 64-bit block and a 56-bit effective key and uses 16 rounds, but it is obsolete. NIST withdrew DES in 2005 because it no longer provided adequate security ([NIST DES withdrawal](https://csrc.nist.gov/News/2005/Withdrawal-of-FIPS-46-3-FIPS-74-and-FIPS-81)). TDEA/3DES is also disallowed by NIST for applying new cryptographic protection after 31 December 2023 ([NIST TDEA withdrawal](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2023/06/nist-withdraw-special-publication-800-67-revision-2)). Study DES only for history and Feistel-network intuition.
7. AES-128 expands its four-word input key into **44 words**, which form **11 round keys of four words each**. Encryption performs an initial `AddRoundKey`, nine full rounds, and a tenth final round **without `MixColumns`**. It is not ten identical repetitions, and `AddRoundKey` does not use four independent keys.
8. **Encryption alone normally gives confidentiality, not integrity.** Prefer authenticated encryption such as AES-GCM, or a complete protocol that supplies both. Merely saying “AES” leaves out a security-critical choice.
9. There is no universal procedure for “generating a public/private key pair.” RSA, finite-field Diffie-Hellman, elliptic-curve cryptography, and post-quantum schemes generate structurally different keys.
10. A digital signature supports integrity and authentication of the signer *only if the verifier already has a trustworthy binding between that public key and the claimed identity*. A public key with no trusted ownership story proves only that the holder of the matching private key signed.

### Evidence labels used in this chapter

- **Verified public fact**: backed by a linked standard or first-party source.
- **Illustrative design**: a reasonable interview architecture, not a claim about a named company's private implementation.
- **Self-reported interview source**: a candidate/community report; useful evidence, but not an independently verified company question bank.
- **Unverified — worth checking**: used if a specific claim cannot be confirmed. No claim carrying that label should be treated as a fact.

### Standards snapshot as of 17 July 2026

- AES is standardized by [NIST FIPS 197](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/fips/197/final), updated editorially in 2023 without changing the algorithm.
- Digital-signature guidance is in [NIST FIPS 186-5](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/fips/186-5/final): RSA, ECDSA, and EdDSA are specified; DSA is retained only for legacy verification.
- Finite-field and elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman key-establishment schemes are covered by [NIST SP 800-56A Rev. 3](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/56/a/r3/final). NIST announced in January 2026 that an update is planned; the 2018 revision is still the final publication at the time of these notes.
- TLS 1.3 is standardized by [RFC 8446](https://www.rfc-editor.org/info/rfc8446/).
- NIST finalized the first post-quantum standards in 2024: ML-KEM for key establishment, plus ML-DSA and SLH-DSA for signatures ([NIST announcement](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2024/08/announcing-approval-three-federal-information-processing-standards-fips)).

Algorithm policy, approved curves, library defaults, product behavior, and regulatory requirements can change. Recheck current standards before making a production compliance decision.

# Part I — Plain-language foundation

## 1. What problem does encryption solve?

Suppose Alice sends Bob this message through a network controlled by Eve:

```text
Plaintext: Transfer Rs 5,000 to account B.
```

Without protection, Eve can read it. Encryption transforms it using a key:

```text
plaintext + encryption algorithm + key
                  |
                  v
        unreadable-looking ciphertext
```

Bob, who has the required key, reverses the transformation:

```text
ciphertext + decryption algorithm + key
                  |
                  v
               plaintext
```

The algorithm does not have to be secret. Modern cryptography assumes that an attacker may know the algorithm and protocol. The security should depend on protecting the key and on the computational difficulty of attacking the scheme.

Think of a strong, mass-produced safe:

- Everyone may know the safe's design.
- The secret is the particular combination.
- The safe must still resist attack even when its design is public.
- A strong safe is useless if the combination is written on its door.

That final point is the system-design lesson: a strong cipher with weak key storage is a weak system.

## 2. The core vocabulary

| Term | Plain-language meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Plaintext** | Data before encryption or after successful decryption | Text, JPEG bytes, database value, another key |
| **Ciphertext** | Encrypted output | Bytes that should reveal no useful plaintext information without the key |
| **Cipher** | Keyed algorithm that transforms plaintext and ciphertext | AES |
| **Key** | Secret or private value controlling the transformation | A random 256-bit AES key |
| **Encryption** | Plaintext to ciphertext | `Enc(key, plaintext)` |
| **Decryption** | Ciphertext to plaintext | `Dec(key, ciphertext)` |
| **Cryptography** | Broader field covering encryption, hashes, MACs, signatures, key agreement, and more | TLS combines several cryptographic mechanisms |
| **Cryptanalysis** | Study of breaking or evaluating cryptographic systems | Finding attacks on a mode or implementation |
| **Cryptosystem/scheme** | Primitive plus rules required to use it securely | RSA-OAEP, not “raw RSA” |

### A cipher and ciphertext are different

```text
AES-GCM           = algorithm/mode construction
random AES key    = secret input
encrypted bytes   = ciphertext output
```

Calling ciphertext “a cipher” is like calling a baked loaf “an oven.”

## 3. What is “non-cipher” data or an operation?

“Non-cipher” is not a standard opposite category. Usually the intended distinction is one of these:

- **Plaintext** versus ciphertext;
- **encryption** versus encoding;
- a **cipher** versus a hash, MAC, signature, checksum, or compression algorithm.

Examples of operations that are not encryption:

| Operation | Reversible? | Secret required? | Primary purpose |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Base64/UTF-8/URL encoding | Yes | No | Representation and transport compatibility |
| Compression | Yes | No | Reduce size |
| Checksum/CRC | Usually not inverted | No | Detect accidental errors |
| Cryptographic hash | Designed as one-way | No | Fingerprint/integrity building block |
| HMAC | Verification, not decryption | Shared secret | Integrity and shared-key authenticity |
| Digital signature | Verification, not decryption | Private key to sign | Integrity and public-key authenticity |
| Tokenization | Depends on system | Often a protected lookup table | Replace sensitive value with a surrogate |
| Redaction | Intentionally destructive | No | Remove sensitive content |

A Caesar shift is technically a cipher, but not a secure modern cipher. “It produces unreadable text” is not evidence of cryptographic security.

## 4. Encryption versus encoding; decryption versus decoding

This is a common interview filter.

### Encoding

Encoding changes representation so another system can store or transmit the data.

```text
"Hi" --UTF-8--> 48 69 --Base64--> SGk=
```

Anyone who knows Base64 can decode `SGk=`. There is no secret key. Base64 is therefore **not protection**.

### Encryption

Encryption is intended to keep data confidential from parties without the key.

```text
"Hi" + secret key --AES-GCM--> ciphertext + authentication tag
```

### The correct verbs

- Encode ↔ decode
- Encrypt ↔ decrypt
- Hash → verify by hashing again; **do not say “decrypt a hash”**
- Sign ↔ verify; **do not say “decrypt a signature”**

### A real payload often uses both

Encryption produces arbitrary binary bytes. JSON and URLs need textual fields, so the bytes may then be Base64-encoded:

```text
plaintext
   -> encrypt with AES-GCM
   -> ciphertext bytes
   -> Base64 encode for JSON
   -> text sent over an API

receiver:
   -> Base64 decode
   -> authenticated decrypt
   -> plaintext
```

Base64 is the outer packaging; encryption is the security control.

## 5. The security properties: do not ask encryption to solve everything

Cryptographic mechanisms provide different properties.

| Property | Question answered | Typical mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Confidentiality** | Can an unauthorized party read it? | Encryption |
| **Integrity** | Was it changed? | AEAD tag, MAC, digital signature |
| **Data-origin authenticity** | Did a holder of the expected key create/approve it? | MAC or digital signature |
| **Peer authentication** | Am I communicating with the intended service/person? | Certificate/signature, PSK, authenticated protocol |
| **Freshness / replay resistance** | Is this a new valid message rather than a captured old one? | Nonce, sequence number, timestamp plus protocol checks |
| **Availability** | Can authorized users access the service? | Replication, failover, capacity—not encryption alone |

### Why encryption without integrity is dangerous

An attacker may not need to understand a ciphertext to alter it in a useful way. Some encryption modes are malleable: changing ciphertext bits can cause controlled changes or errors in decrypted plaintext.

Authenticated encryption rejects modified ciphertext:

```text
Encrypt(key, nonce, plaintext, AAD)
    -> ciphertext, authentication_tag

Decrypt(key, nonce, ciphertext, AAD, tag)
    -> plaintext OR failure
```

The system must **stop on authentication failure** and must not process unauthenticated plaintext.

## 6. Why encryption matters in system design

A distributed system puts sensitive data in many places:

```text
phone -> internet -> CDN/load balancer -> service -> cache -> database
                                      \-> queue -> worker -> object store
                                                  \-> backup / analytics
```

Encryption can reduce risk at several boundaries:

- **In transit:** TLS protects data crossing untrusted networks.
- **At rest:** storage or application encryption limits exposure from stolen disks, snapshots, backups, or some storage-layer compromises.
- **End to end:** only user endpoints possess content keys, so intermediate servers route ciphertext.
- **Field/application level:** selected values such as tax IDs are encrypted before entering a general database.
- **Envelope encryption:** many data keys are protected by a smaller, centrally managed set of key-encryption keys.

It also introduces design costs:

- key-service latency and availability dependencies;
- key rotation, revocation, backup, recovery, and audit work;
- reduced search, indexing, deduplication, compression, moderation, and analytics capability;
- client recovery and multi-device complexity for end-to-end encryption;
- larger payloads and some CPU/memory overhead;
- catastrophic data loss if the only decryption key is destroyed.

The HLD question is not “Should I use AES or RSA?” It is:

> What data is protected, from which attacker, while the data is in which state, who must be able to decrypt it, and how are those keys governed for the entire system lifetime?

# Part II — Prerequisites explained inline

## 7. Bits, bytes, blocks, and words

- A **bit** is `0` or `1`.
- A **byte** is normally 8 bits.
- A **128-bit AES block** is 16 bytes.
- In AES, a **word** is 32 bits = 4 bytes.
- A **block cipher** transforms one fixed-size block at a time under a key.
- A mode or higher-level construction safely handles arbitrary-length messages.

Key size and block size are different:

```text
AES-256:
  key size   = 256 bits
  block size = 128 bits
```

AES-256 does **not** process 256-bit data blocks.

## 8. XOR: the operation seen everywhere

Exclusive OR, written `XOR` or `⊕`, operates bit by bit:

```text
0 XOR 0 = 0       1 XOR 0 = 1
0 XOR 1 = 1       1 XOR 1 = 0
```

Its useful property is self-reversal:

```text
P XOR K = C
C XOR K = P
```

AES `AddRoundKey` XORs the state with a round key. XOR by itself is not a secure reusable cipher: reusing a one-time-pad-like keystream leaks relationships between plaintexts.

## 9. Modulo arithmetic, primes, and the discrete logarithm

### Modulo

`a mod p` is the remainder after dividing `a` by `p`:

```text
27 mod 5 = 2
```

Numbers “wrap around,” like a clock. Diffie-Hellman uses exponentiation modulo a large prime:

```text
g^a mod p
```

Computing that public value is efficient. Given only suitable large public parameters and the result, recovering `a` is believed to be computationally hard. That reverse problem is the **discrete logarithm problem**.

### Prime number

A prime integer greater than 1 has no positive divisors other than 1 and itself. Prime moduli give finite sets with algebraic structure that Diffie-Hellman can use.

### Group, in only the depth needed here

A **group** is a set plus an operation that behaves consistently: it is closed, associative, has an identity, and every element has an inverse. The non-zero residues modulo a prime `p`, under multiplication modulo `p`, form a group of size `p - 1`.

Repeatedly multiplying a generator `g` walks through a group or subgroup. The **order of `g`** is the smallest positive number `k` for which:

```text
g^k mod p = 1
```

This language will make “primitive root” precise later.

## 10. Hashes, MACs, and password hashing

### Cryptographic hash

A cryptographic hash maps arbitrary input to a fixed-length digest:

```text
H(message) -> digest
```

Expected properties include:

- hard to recover the input from only its digest (**preimage resistance**);
- hard to find a second input with the same digest (**second-preimage resistance**);
- hard to find any two different inputs with the same digest (**collision resistance**).

A plain hash does **not authenticate a sender**. An attacker who changes a downloaded file can calculate a new unkeyed hash too.

### MAC / HMAC

A Message Authentication Code uses a shared secret:

```text
HMAC(shared_key, message) -> tag
```

Anyone holding the shared key can create and verify tags. A MAC is fast and useful inside a trust domain, but it cannot prove to a third party which of two shared-key holders created a tag.

### Passwords are a special case

Passwords are low-entropy, human-chosen guesses—not cryptographic keys. A service that only needs to validate a login should store a unique salt and a slow, memory-hard password hash, normally Argon2id where available, rather than reversible encryption or a fast SHA-256 digest. OWASP explicitly recommends password hashing rather than encryption in almost all circumstances ([OWASP Password Storage Cheat Sheet](https://cheatsheetseries.owasp.org/cheatsheets/Password_Storage_Cheat_Sheet.html)).

```text
registration:
password + random salt -> Argon2id -> stored verifier

login:
candidate + stored salt -> Argon2id -> constant-time comparison
```

The salt is not secret. It prevents identical passwords from sharing identical stored hashes and defeats one precomputed table covering every user.

## 11. Randomness is a security dependency

Cryptographic keys must come from a **cryptographically secure random number generator (CSPRNG)** backed by sufficient entropy. Ordinary simulation-oriented random functions, timestamps, usernames, UUID versions not designed for secrecy, and “keyboard randomness” are not key generators.

Randomness is also needed for many:

- nonces and IVs;
- ephemeral private keys;
- signature nonces;
- salts;
- session IDs and reset tokens.

NIST's SP 800-90 series covers entropy sources and random-bit-generator constructions ([NIST Random Bit Generation project](https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/random-bit-generation)). The application-level rule is simpler: use the operating system's or a vetted library's secure-random API; do not invent or manually seed a PRNG.

## 12. Authentication and authorization are prerequisites

- **Authentication:** who is this principal?
- **Authorization:** may that principal perform this operation on this object?
- **Encryption:** can an unauthorized observer understand this content?

They complement one another. An encrypted database connection does not mean Alice may read Bob's row. An authorization check over plaintext HTTP leaks credentials. Encryption at rest does not stop an application that is authorized to decrypt but is tricked by SQL injection.

## 13. Threat model: name the attacker before choosing the control

| Threat | Helpful control | What it does not solve |
|---|---|---|
| Wi-Fi/network eavesdropper | TLS | Compromised endpoint or server |
| Stolen disk/snapshot | Encryption at rest | Authorized malicious application process |
| Database-only exfiltration | App/field encryption with separated keys | App + KMS compromise |
| Cloud/service operator must not read message content | End-to-end encryption | Metadata, compromised user device |
| Message modification | AEAD/MAC/signature | Replay unless freshness is checked |
| Fake server/public key | Certificates or trusted key verification | Compromised CA/device trust store |
| Key theft from source repository | KMS/HSM and secret separation | Runtime compromise with decrypt authority |

“Encrypted” is not a complete security statement until the protected boundary and remaining plaintext locations are clear.

# Part III — Cryptographic keys and key-pair generation

## 14. What exactly is a cryptographic key?

A key is an input that selects one transformation from a huge family of possible transformations.

For an ideal 128-bit symmetric key, there are `2^128` possible bit patterns. An attacker who does not know the key should not have a practical shortcut to the plaintext.

Important distinctions:

- **Algorithm:** AES
- **Key size:** 256 bits
- **Mode:** GCM
- **Nonce/IV:** unique value for this encryption under this key
- **Key:** the actual secret random 256-bit value
- **Key ID/version:** non-secret label telling the system which key was used

Do not put the key itself in a field called `keyId`.

### Password versus key

A password such as `Coffee@9` has far less unpredictability than a uniformly random 256-bit key. If a human password must protect encrypted data, run it through a password-based key-derivation function with a salt and work factor. Do not pad/truncate the password or hash it once and call the result an AES key.

### Key length is not directly comparable between algorithm families

AES-128 and RSA-128 are not equally strong. Attacks exploit different mathematical structure. NIST's classical-security comparison maps approximately 128-bit security strength to AES-128, RSA with a 3072-bit modulus, and elliptic-curve parameters of roughly 256–383 bits ([NIST SP 800-57 Part 1 Rev. 5, Table 2](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/specialpublications/nist.sp.800-57pt1r5.pdf)). These are standards-guidance equivalences, not a claim that all schemes have identical implementation risks.

## 15. Symmetric-key generation

Conceptually:

```text
key = CSPRNG.generate(128 or 256 random bits)
```

Then:

1. Assign purpose and owner metadata.
2. Store it in a KMS/HSM/key vault or wrap it under another key.
3. Restrict encrypt/decrypt permissions by least privilege.
4. Record a non-secret key ID and version with ciphertext.
5. Define activation, rotation, compromise, retirement, and destruction behavior.

Generating random bits is the easy part. Governing who can use them without leaking or losing them is the harder system-design problem.

## 16. Public/private key pairs: the shared idea

An asymmetric scheme creates mathematically related values:

```text
private key --one-way mathematical operation--> public key
```

- The **private key** must remain controlled by its owner.
- The **public key** may be distributed.
- Deriving the private key from the public key should be computationally infeasible for the chosen parameters.

This does **not** imply that every asymmetric algorithm can both encrypt and sign. Its capabilities depend on the scheme.

| Scheme | Private/public structure | Primary job |
|---|---|---|
| RSA | Large primes and modular exponents | Encryption/key transport and/or signatures with distinct schemes |
| DSA | Discrete-log signing key | Legacy digital signatures only |
| Finite-field DH | Secret exponent and modular public value | Key agreement |
| ECDH/ECDHE | Secret scalar and curve point | Key agreement |
| ECDSA/EdDSA | Secret scalar/seed and curve public key | Digital signatures |
| ML-KEM | Post-quantum encapsulation keys | Key establishment |

Use separate keys for separate purposes unless a vetted protocol explicitly defines otherwise. Separation limits cross-protocol attacks and blast radius.

## 17. How an RSA key pair is generated

This is the conceptual process. Production libraries add strict prime testing, parameter checks, side-channel resistance, and optimized private-key fields.

1. Securely generate two large, independent secret primes `p` and `q`.
2. Compute the public modulus:

   ```text
   n = p * q
   ```

3. Compute a totient-related value, commonly `lambda(n) = lcm(p - 1, q - 1)` in modern specifications. Euler's `phi(n) = (p - 1)(q - 1)` is often used to teach the idea.
4. Choose public exponent `e` so it is coprime with that value. `65537` is a conventional choice, but let a vetted library choose and validate parameters.
5. Compute private exponent `d`, the modular inverse of `e`:

   ```text
   e * d = 1 mod lambda(n)
   ```

6. Publish `(n, e)` as the public key.
7. Protect `d`, `p`, `q`, and optimized CRT parameters as private-key material.

### Tiny educational RSA example

```text
p = 61, q = 53
n = 3233
phi(n) = 60 * 52 = 3120
choose e = 17
d = 2753, because 17 * 2753 mod 3120 = 1

public key  = (3233, 17)
private key = (3233, 2753) plus normally p, q and CRT values
```

These tiny values are completely insecure. They exist only to expose the relationship.

### Never use “textbook RSA”

Raw modular exponentiation is deterministic and lacks required security structure. Use a reviewed scheme:

- **RSA-OAEP** for encryption/key transport;
- **RSA-PSS** for signatures.

[RFC 8017](https://www.rfc-editor.org/info/rfc8017/) requires OAEP support for new RSA-encryption applications and PSS for new RSA-signature applications; the older PKCS #1 v1.5 forms remain mainly for compatibility. Encryption padding and signature encoding are different constructions.

## 18. How finite-field Diffie-Hellman keys are generated

For agreed, validated public parameters `p`, `g`, and subgroup order information:

```text
private key a = securely random secret exponent
public key  A = g^a mod p
```

Another party independently chooses `b` and calculates `B = g^b mod p`. The public values are exchanged; private exponents are not.

Use standardized groups and libraries. Choosing a random large prime and a plausible-looking base is not safe parameter generation.

## 19. How elliptic-curve key pairs are generated

For public curve/domain parameters with a base point `G` of order `n`:

```text
private key d = secure random integer in the valid range
public key  Q = dG
```

`dG` means elliptic-curve scalar multiplication: repeated group addition, not normal integer multiplication. Computing `Q` from `d` is efficient; recovering `d` from `Q` is the elliptic-curve discrete logarithm problem.

Elliptic-curve schemes reach a given classical security strength with much smaller public keys than RSA, which can reduce bandwidth and computation. Correct curve choice, public-key validation, nonce generation, and implementation remain critical.

## 20. Generation does not establish identity

Alice can generate a valid key pair. Mallory can also generate a valid key pair and label it “Alice.” Cryptographic validity is not identity.

Common trust mechanisms are:

- a Certificate Authority signs a certificate binding a domain/identity to a public key;
- users compare a key fingerprint or safety number over another trusted channel;
- an administrator provisions keys into a trusted registry;
- an account-authenticated key-transparency service records key ownership and changes;
- an application pins an expected key, with a safe rotation plan.

```text
Public key answers: “Which private key matches this value?”
Trust mechanism answers: “Why should I believe this value belongs to Alice?”
```

# Part IV — Symmetric encryption

## 21. The symmetric model

The same secret key is used for encryption and decryption:

```text
Alice                                   Bob
plaintext --Encrypt(K)--> ciphertext --Decrypt(K)--> plaintext
                    both possess K
```

### Strengths

- fast and efficient for large data;
- small keys;
- natural for disk, database, object, backup, and session encryption;
- supported by hardware acceleration on common platforms.

### Costs

- the parties must establish the shared key securely;
- anyone with the key can decrypt and usually create valid ciphertext/tags;
- pairwise sharing becomes hard to manage as participant count grows;
- a shared key does not give public proof of which holder created a message.

If every pair among `n` independent parties needs a unique shared key, the number of pairwise keys is:

```text
n(n - 1) / 2
```

Real systems avoid manually distributing this many keys by using sessions, a central KMS, public-key key agreement, or group protocols.

## 22. DES: useful history, unsafe choice

DES is a symmetric Feistel block cipher:

| Property | DES |
|---|---|
| Block size | 64 bits |
| Supplied key representation | 64 bits, including 8 parity bits |
| Effective cryptographic key | 56 bits |
| Rounds | 16 |
| Modern status | Obsolete; do not use for new protection |

At a high level:

```text
64-bit block
   -> initial permutation
   -> split into 32-bit L0 and R0
   -> 16 Feistel rounds
   -> swap/combine
   -> final permutation
```

Each Feistel round resembles:

```text
L(i) = R(i-1)
R(i) = L(i-1) XOR F(R(i-1), round_key_i)
```

Inside `F`, the right half is expanded, combined with a 48-bit round subkey, compressed nonlinearly through S-boxes, and permuted. The Feistel structure lets decryption reuse the same structure with round keys reversed.

DES's 56-bit key space is too small, and its 64-bit block is also unsuitable for modern high-volume use. Triple-DES applied DES multiple times to extend its useful life; it is now obsolete for new protection too.

**Interview answer:** know the numbers and historical role, then explicitly reject DES/3DES for a new design.

## 23. AES: the normal block-cipher family to know

AES is a symmetric substitution-permutation block cipher standardized in FIPS 197.

| Variant | Key size | Block size | Rounds | Expanded words | Round keys |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| AES-128 | 128 bits | 128 bits | 10 | 44 | 11 |
| AES-192 | 192 bits | 128 bits | 12 | 52 | 13 |
| AES-256 | 256 bits | 128 bits | 14 | 60 | 15 |

The block is always 128 bits. The variant number names the key length.

### What “rounds” accomplish

Each round combines:

- **nonlinearity/confusion:** hide simple relationships to the key;
- **diffusion:** spread a local input change across the state;
- **key mixing:** make the transformation depend on the expanded key.

This is intuition, not a security proof. AES's security comes from extensive public cryptanalysis of the precisely standardized construction.

## 24. A block cipher is not yet a safe file-encryption design

AES transforms exactly one 16-byte block. A **mode of operation** specifies how to handle many blocks, randomness/nonces, and sometimes integrity.

| Mode/construction | Confidentiality | Integrity/authenticity | Design guidance |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| ECB | Poor for structured multi-block data | No | Do not use; equal plaintext blocks expose patterns |
| CBC | Yes if used correctly | No | Legacy; needs unpredictable IV, padding, and separate Encrypt-then-MAC |
| CTR | Yes if used correctly | No | Nonce/counter reuse is dangerous; pair with a MAC if required by a vetted protocol |
| GCM | Yes | Yes, via authentication tag | Common modern AEAD; nonce uniqueness under a key is critical |
| CCM | Yes | Yes | AEAD; useful in some constrained/protocol contexts |

OWASP recommends authenticated modes such as GCM or CCM where available and warns against ECB ([OWASP Cryptographic Storage Cheat Sheet](https://cheatsheetseries.owasp.org/cheatsheets/Cryptographic_Storage_Cheat_Sheet.html)).

### Why ECB leaks structure

```text
same key + same 16-byte plaintext block -> same ciphertext block
```

Repeated image regions or record fields remain visibly patterned even though individual values are unreadable.

### Why “AES-256” is an incomplete answer

You still need to specify:

- mode/construction, preferably AEAD;
- nonce/IV generation and uniqueness rules;
- authentication-tag handling;
- key origin, scope, storage, rotation, and access control;
- message format and algorithm/key version;
- behavior on failure;
- library/protocol.

## 25. AEAD, nonce, IV, tag, and associated data

### AEAD

**Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data** protects:

- plaintext confidentiality;
- ciphertext integrity/authenticity;
- integrity/authenticity of selected unencrypted metadata.

```text
plaintext: invoice bytes                 encrypted
AAD: tenant=42, object=91, version=7     visible but authenticated
```

If an attacker moves the ciphertext to another tenant or changes the version field, verification fails—provided the application supplies exactly that metadata as AAD during decryption.

### Nonce / IV

A nonce (“number used once”) or Initialization Vector varies encryptions under one key. Its exact requirements depend on the mode:

- it is normally stored/transmitted with ciphertext;
- it usually does not need to be secret;
- for GCM, it must not repeat under the same key;
- “random” and “unique” are not interchangeable requirements across all modes.

NIST states that GCM's authentication assurance critically depends on IV uniqueness ([NIST SP 800-38D](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/38/d/final)). Follow the library/protocol's nonce strategy rather than improvising.

### Authentication tag

The tag is checked during decryption. If it fails:

```text
return generic failure
discard plaintext
do not partially process it
avoid revealing a detailed oracle to the caller
```

### IV, salt, and key are not synonyms

| Value | Secret? | Main purpose |
|---|---:|---|
| Encryption key | Yes | Controls encryption/decryption |
| Nonce/IV | Usually no | Makes each use safe/distinct under a key |
| Password salt | No | Makes password derivation unique per record |
| Authentication tag | No | Lets receiver detect modification/wrong inputs |
| Key ID/version | No | Selects the required key and algorithm metadata |

# Part V — AES internals, step by step

## 26. When this depth matters

For a normal HLD interview, know AES as a fast symmetric 128-bit block cipher and choose a safe AEAD construction. The state-array and key-schedule detail becomes relevant when:

- the interviewer explicitly asks how AES works;
- the role is security, infrastructure, embedded, performance, or cryptography heavy;
- you need to explain why the final round or key expansion differs from a simplistic diagram.

Do not spend ten minutes deriving the S-box before clarifying an HLD threat model.

## 27. State array

AES loads the 16 input bytes into a 4 × 4 **state** in column-major order:

```text
input bytes:
b0 b1 b2 b3  b4 b5 b6 b7  b8 b9 b10 b11  b12 b13 b14 b15

state:
| b0  b4  b8   b12 |
| b1  b5  b9   b13 |
| b2  b6  b10  b14 |
| b3  b7  b11  b15 |
```

The state is the working data transformed throughout encryption.

## 28. Word and round key

- One AES **word** = 4 bytes = 32 bits.
- The AES state contains four words/columns.
- One **round key** = four words = 16 bytes = one state-sized value.

For AES-128:

```text
input cipher key: 4 words = 16 bytes
key expansion:    44 words
round keys:       44 / 4 = 11
```

Why 11 round keys for 10 rounds?

```text
round key 0: initial AddRoundKey before round 1
round keys 1..9: nine full rounds
round key 10: final round
```

## 29. AES-128 encryption sequence

The accurate sequence is:

```text
16-byte plaintext
       |
       v
State array
       |
       v
Initial AddRoundKey                 uses words w[0..3]
       |
       v
Rounds 1 through 9:
    SubBytes
    ShiftRows
    MixColumns
    AddRoundKey                     uses four schedule words per round
       |
       v
Round 10:
    SubBytes
    ShiftRows
    AddRoundKey                     NO MixColumns
       |
       v
16-byte ciphertext
```

AES-192 and AES-256 have the same overall round shape with 12 and 14 rounds respectively, plus different key schedules.

## 30. SubBytes

`SubBytes` replaces every byte independently through a fixed 256-entry substitution box (S-box):

```text
state byte x -> SBox[x]
```

The S-box is not an arbitrary secret lookup table. It is constructed using multiplicative inversion in the finite field `GF(2^8)`, with a special case for zero, followed by an affine transformation.

Purpose:

- introduce nonlinearity;
- break simple algebraic relationships between plaintext, key, and ciphertext;
- contribute “confusion.”

## 31. ShiftRows

Rows are cyclically shifted left:

```text
row 0: shift by 0
row 1: shift by 1 byte
row 2: shift by 2 bytes
row 3: shift by 3 bytes
```

Before:

```text
| a0 a1 a2 a3 |
| b0 b1 b2 b3 |
| c0 c1 c2 c3 |
| d0 d1 d2 d3 |
```

After:

```text
| a0 a1 a2 a3 |
| b1 b2 b3 b0 |
| c2 c3 c0 c1 |
| d3 d0 d1 d2 |
```

Purpose: move bytes between columns so later column mixing spreads their influence.

## 32. MixColumns

Each four-byte column is treated as values in `GF(2^8)` and multiplied by a fixed invertible matrix:

```text
| 02 03 01 01 |   | s0 |
| 01 02 03 01 | x | s1 |
| 01 01 02 03 |   | s2 |
| 03 01 01 02 |   | s3 |
```

All addition and multiplication here follow finite-field rules, not ordinary decimal matrix arithmetic.

Purpose: diffuse each input byte across all four bytes of its column. Combined with `ShiftRows`, influence spreads across the state over rounds.

The final AES round omits `MixColumns` as specified by the algorithm. Its omission does not mean the last round lacks security; it is part of the analyzed AES construction.

## 33. AddRoundKey

The 16-byte state is XORed with one 16-byte round key:

```text
new_state = state XOR round_key
```

This is the step that directly incorporates key material. The four words used are pieces of **one expanded key schedule**, not four unrelated cryptographic keys.

Because XOR is self-inverse, applying the same round key reverses this particular step during decryption.

## 34. AES-128 key expansion: one key to 44 words

Let the original 128-bit key be words `w[0]` through `w[3]`. Generate `w[4]` through `w[43]`:

```text
for i from 4 to 43:
    temp = w[i - 1]

    if i mod 4 == 0:
        temp = SubWord(RotWord(temp)) XOR Rcon[i / 4]

    w[i] = w[i - 4] XOR temp
```

Where:

- `RotWord([a,b,c,d]) = [b,c,d,a]`;
- `SubWord` applies the AES S-box to each byte;
- `Rcon` supplies a round-dependent constant;
- XOR chains new material to earlier words.

Round-key mapping:

```text
initial key addition: w[0..3]
round 1:              w[4..7]
round 2:              w[8..11]
...
round 10:             w[40..43]
```

AES-192 and AES-256 start with 6 and 8 input words and expand to 52 and 60 words. AES-256 also applies an additional `SubWord` case partway through each eight-word cycle. [FIPS 197](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/FIPS/NIST.FIPS.197-upd1.pdf) contains authoritative key-schedule diagrams for all three variants.

### What key expansion is not

- It does not create 11 independent secrets.
- It does not increase a 128-bit key's security strength to 1,408 bits.
- It does not mean applications should save 44 words separately in a database.
- It is an internal schedule derived deterministically from the cipher key.

## 35. AES decryption

AES decryption uses inverse transformations and the round keys in the appropriate reverse order:

- `InvShiftRows`;
- `InvSubBytes`;
- `InvMixColumns` except where the corresponding encryption round omitted mixing;
- `AddRoundKey`, which remains XOR.

A library exposes encryption/decryption at a mode or AEAD level. Application code should not manually call round transformations.

## 36. AES facts worth remembering versus deriving

### Remember

- 128-bit block for every AES variant.
- Keys: 128/192/256 bits.
- Rounds: 10/12/14.
- AES-128: 44 words = 11 round keys.
- Initial `AddRoundKey`, then full rounds, final round without `MixColumns`.
- Prefer a vetted AEAD construction; nonce rules and key management matter.

### Be able to derive

```text
AES-128 needs 1 initial round key + 10 round keys
= 11 round keys
* 4 words per round key
= 44 words
```

That derivation is more reliable than memorizing “44” in isolation.

# Part VI — Asymmetric cryptography and hybrid encryption

## 37. The asymmetric model

Asymmetric cryptography uses a related public/private pair. One typical encryption direction is:

```text
Anyone: Encrypt(recipient_public_key, small plaintext/key material)
Owner:  Decrypt(recipient_private_key, ciphertext)
```

A signature uses a different operation:

```text
Owner:  Sign(private_signing_key, message)
Anyone with trusted public key: Verify(public_key, message, signature)
```

Avoid the shortcut “asymmetric crypto just encrypts with one key and decrypts with the other.” It confuses distinct encryption, key-agreement, and signature schemes and encourages dangerous “private-key encryption” explanations.

### Strengths

- no pre-shared secret is required for every new pair;
- public keys can be distributed through PKI/directories;
- supports signatures and authenticated key establishment;
- makes Internet-scale identity/key bootstrapping possible.

### Costs

- slower and more computationally expensive than symmetric encryption;
- larger keys, signatures, or ciphertexts;
- needs trusted public-key ownership and revocation mechanisms;
- algorithms are easy to misuse without correct padding/encoding/protocols;
- current RSA, finite-field DH, and ECC are not resistant to a sufficiently capable cryptographically relevant quantum computer.

## 38. RSA: what it can and cannot do

RSA is based on modular exponentiation and the difficulty of recovering the secret factors of a suitably generated large modulus.

It can underpin:

- RSA-OAEP encryption of small payloads/key material;
- RSA-PSS digital signatures.

Do not use RSA to encrypt a video, database dump, or arbitrary large request. RSA-OAEP's maximum plaintext is smaller than the modulus and depends on the hash overhead. Bulk data belongs under a random symmetric key.

Do not reuse one RSA key pair casually for both encryption and signatures. Keys have purpose, policy, and lifecycle.

## 39. DSA: place it in the correct box

**DSA = Digital Signature Algorithm.** It signs and verifies; it does not encrypt data and does not perform ordinary key agreement.

Historically, DSA security depended critically on a per-signature secret value. Reuse or bias of that value can expose the private signing key. Modern signature libraries use approved contemporary schemes and carefully defined nonce generation.

For a new design in a general interview:

- do not propose classic DSA;
- mention RSA-PSS, ECDSA, EdDSA, or a mandated modern standard depending on platform and compliance;
- use a library/protocol rather than implementing signature arithmetic.

## 40. Diffie-Hellman: solving the key-distribution problem

Alice and Bob want a shared secret, but every message between them can be observed. Diffie-Hellman lets them derive the same value without transmitting that value directly.

### Public setup

- `p`: a suitable prime modulus;
- `g`: a generator with the required order in the chosen group/subgroup.

### Private and public values

```text
Alice chooses private a             Bob chooses private b
Alice publishes A = g^a mod p       Bob publishes B = g^b mod p
```

### Shared value

```text
Alice computes B^a mod p
Bob computes   A^b mod p

B^a = (g^b)^a = g^(ab) mod p
A^b = (g^a)^b = g^(ab) mod p
```

They match because multiplication of the exponents is commutative.

### Tiny worked example

These numbers are intentionally insecure:

```text
public p = 23
public g = 5

Alice private a = 6
Alice public  A = 5^6 mod 23 = 8

Bob private b = 15
Bob public  B = 5^15 mod 23 = 19

Alice: 19^6 mod 23 = 2
Bob:    8^15 mod 23 = 2

shared DH value = 2
```

An eavesdropper sees `23`, `5`, `8`, and `19`, but not `a` or `b`. With real validated parameters, recovering the exponents should be infeasible.

### Do not use the raw DH value directly

A protocol feeds the shared secret and transcript/context into a Key Derivation Function (KDF), deriving separate keys for directions and purposes:

```text
DH shared secret + handshake transcript + context
                    |
                   KDF
           +--------+---------+
           |                  |
    client->server key  server->client key
```

Key separation prevents one key from being reused for incompatible jobs.

## 41. Primitive root: what it means

For prime `p`, a number `g` is a **primitive root modulo `p`** if its powers generate every non-zero residue modulo `p` before repeating. Equivalently, its order is `p - 1`.

For `p = 7`, `g = 3`:

```text
3^1 mod 7 = 3
3^2 mod 7 = 2
3^3 mod 7 = 6
3^4 mod 7 = 4
3^5 mod 7 = 5
3^6 mod 7 = 1
```

The powers visited `{1,2,3,4,5,6}`, so `3` is a primitive root modulo `7`.

## 42. How to test whether a base is a primitive root

For a prime `p`:

1. Factor `p - 1` into its **distinct prime factors** `q1, q2, ..., qk`.
2. For a candidate `g`, calculate:

   ```text
   g^((p - 1) / qi) mod p
   ```

   for every distinct prime factor `qi`.
3. `g` is a primitive root exactly when none of those results equals `1`.

### Example: is `5` a primitive root modulo `23`?

```text
p - 1 = 22 = 2 * 11
distinct prime factors: 2 and 11

5^(22/2)  mod 23 = 5^11 mod 23 = 22, not 1
5^(22/11) mod 23 = 5^2  mod 23 = 2,  not 1

therefore 5 has order 22 and is a primitive root modulo 23
```

Why the test works: the order of an element must divide the group size `p - 1`. These checks rule out every proper factor of `p - 1` as the order.

Pseudocode for education:

```text
isPrimitiveRoot(g, p):
    factors = distinctPrimeFactors(p - 1)
    for q in factors:
        if modularExponentiation(g, (p - 1) / q, p) == 1:
            return false
    return true
```

### Crucial production caveat

Cryptographic DH commonly operates in a **large prime-order subgroup**. In that case, `g` should generate the intended subgroup of order `q`; it need not be a primitive root of the entire multiplicative group of order `p - 1`. A safe-prime construction may have `p = 2q + 1` and deliberately use the order-`q` subgroup.

Therefore:

- “find any primitive root” is a number-theory exercise;
- “choose secure DH parameters” is a protocol/standards task;
- use standardized, validated groups and validate peer public values;
- do not generate a production `p` and `g` from this hand test.

## 43. The man-in-the-middle weakness of unauthenticated DH

Raw DH proves that each side shares a secret with *someone*, not which someone.

```text
Alice             Mallory              Bob
  |--- A ----------X                    |
  |<-- M1 ---------|                    |
  |                |------ M2 --------->|
  |                |<----- B -----------|

Alice shares K_AM with Mallory
Bob shares   K_MB with Mallory
Mallory decrypts, changes, and re-encrypts traffic
```

A complete authenticated protocol signs/binds the ephemeral exchange to a trusted identity or uses an already authenticated pre-shared key.

## 44. ECDH and ECDHE

Elliptic-Curve Diffie-Hellman uses curve points rather than modular exponentiation over finite-field integers:

```text
public parameters: curve and base point G

Alice: private a, public A = aG
Bob:   private b, public B = bG

Alice computes aB = abG
Bob computes   bA = abG
```

### What the final E means

**ECDHE = Elliptic-Curve Diffie-Hellman Ephemeral.** At least the relevant DH private key is freshly generated for the session and discarded afterward.

This enables **forward secrecy**:

> If the server's long-term signing key is stolen later, previously recorded sessions cannot be decrypted solely from that stolen long-term key, because their ephemeral private values are gone.

Forward secrecy does not save a session whose ephemeral secret or plaintext endpoint was compromised while active.

## 45. Hybrid encryption: how real systems combine the strengths

Asymmetric mechanisms solve identity/key-establishment problems; symmetric mechanisms efficiently protect bulk data.

```text
                asymmetric phase
Alice -------------------------------------- Bob
      authenticate and agree/transport key
                         |
                         v
                  shared session keys
                         |
                symmetric AEAD phase
Alice <======== encrypted bulk data =======> Bob
```

Two common variants:

1. **Authenticated ephemeral key agreement:** ECDHE + certificate signature + KDF, as in a normal TLS 1.3 certificate-authenticated handshake.
2. **Key transport/envelope:** generate a random AES data key, encrypt data with AES-GCM, and wrap the AES key with the recipient's RSA-OAEP public key or a KMS key.

Do not say “the entire session is asymmetrically encrypted.” In modern protocols, public-key operations typically establish/authenticate secrets; symmetric keys protect application data.

# Part VII — Digital signatures

## 46. What a digital signature does

Conceptually:

```text
Signer:
message -> hash/encoding -> sign with private key -> signature

Verifier:
message + signature + trusted public key
        -> verify
        -> valid or invalid
```

A signature can provide:

- **integrity:** changing the signed bytes invalidates verification;
- **data-origin authentication:** a holder of the matching private key produced/approved it;
- **public verifiability:** verification does not require sharing the private key.

It does not provide:

- confidentiality—the signed message may be readable;
- freshness—an old signed request can be replayed unless time/nonce/sequence is signed and checked;
- identity by itself—the public key must be bound to the claimed signer;
- proof that a human personally clicked something if malware or another operator used the key.

## 47. Why “signing is encrypting a hash with the private key” is unsafe shorthand

That phrase loosely resembles the arithmetic intuition behind some RSA signatures, but it fails generally:

- ECDSA, EdDSA, and DSA do not work that way;
- RSA signatures require a signature encoding such as PSS, not encryption padding;
- signing and decryption have different security definitions and interfaces;
- it encourages developers to call raw RSA operations.

Interview-safe wording:

> A signature algorithm combines a private signing key with the message, normally through a standardized hashing/encoding process, to produce a signature that the corresponding public key can verify.

## 48. Authentication, integrity, and non-repudiation nuance

NIST describes digital signatures as detecting unauthorized modification and authenticating the signatory, and notes their use as evidence for non-repudiation ([FIPS 186-5](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/fips/186-5/final)). In a system-design answer, qualify “non-repudiation”:

- Was the private key exclusively controlled?
- Was the certificate valid and not revoked at signing time?
- Was the exact business context signed?
- Is there a trusted timestamp and audit trail?
- Could malware, an administrator, or shared service credentials have used the key?
- What does applicable law recognize?

Cryptographic verification can support non-repudiation evidence; it is not magical legal certainty.

## 49. MAC versus digital signature

| Dimension | HMAC/MAC | Digital signature |
|---|---|---|
| Keys | Same shared secret for creation/verification | Private key signs; public key verifies |
| Speed | Usually faster | Usually slower |
| Who can create valid proof? | Every secret holder | Private-key holder |
| Public verification | No | Yes |
| Third-party attribution | Weak: verifiers can forge too | Stronger, subject to key identity/control |
| Typical use | Internal API messages, protocol record protection | Certificates, releases, documents, handshake authentication |

## 50. Signature failure and canonicalization edge cases

A signature covers bytes, not vague “meaning.” Two equivalent JSON objects can have different byte order or whitespace. Define a canonical form or sign an unambiguous protocol serialization.

Include context to prevent cross-use:

```text
bad:  Sign(private_key, amount)

better signed structure:
  protocol = "payments-v2"
  action = "transfer"
  payer_id
  payee_id
  amount
  currency
  request_id
  expires_at
```

Verify all fields, expected algorithm, expected key purpose, expected audience/context, certificate/key status, expiry/freshness, and signature result before acting.

# Part VIII — Encryption as an HLD building block

## 51. The four locations of data

### In transit

Data moves between processes or machines. TLS commonly provides encryption, integrity, and peer authentication.

```text
client ==TLS==> CDN ==TLS==> origin ==mTLS/TLS==> service
```

Each `==TLS==>` is a separate protected hop. If TLS terminates at the CDN, the CDN can normally see plaintext and a new connection protects the next hop. That is not end-to-end encryption between user devices.

### At rest

Data sits on disks, databases, object stores, snapshots, logs, or backups.

Storage-layer encryption is operationally transparent and broad, but an authorized database/service still sees plaintext. Application/field-level encryption creates a higher boundary but complicates queries and key access.

### In use

Ordinary CPUs usually need plaintext in memory to compute on it. TLS and disk encryption do not protect against a fully compromised process reading active plaintext. Hardware-backed confidential-computing techniques can reduce some risks but do not remove application bugs.

### End to end

The content is encrypted at a sender endpoint and decrypted only at intended recipient endpoints:

```text
sender device -> ciphertext -> service/storage -> ciphertext -> recipient device
```

The server may still see metadata such as account identifiers, time, IP information, device information, and approximate message size unless separately protected.

## 52. TLS 1.3 in one accurate diagram

This is a simplified certificate-authenticated handshake:

```text
Client                                             Server
  | ClientHello, supported groups,                 |
  | ephemeral ECDHE key share A ------------------>|
  |                                                |
  |<-- ServerHello, ECDHE key share B -------------|
  |<-- certificate + signature over handshake -----|
  |                                                |
  | Verify certificate/hostname/signature          |
  | Both calculate ECDHE shared secret             |
  | KDF binds keys to handshake transcript         |
  |                                                |
  |<====== AEAD-protected application data =======>|
```

Roles:

- **ECDHE:** ephemeral shared secret / forward secrecy;
- **certificate + signature:** authenticate the server's long-term identity and bind the handshake;
- **HKDF/key schedule:** derive separate traffic secrets;
- **AES-GCM or ChaCha20-Poly1305:** symmetric authenticated application-data protection.

TLS 1.3 independently negotiates a cipher suite, an (EC)DHE group/key share, and a signature/certificate when not using a PSK ([RFC 8446](https://www.rfc-editor.org/info/rfc8446/)). It is inaccurate to call the entire handshake “RSA encryption.”

TLS authenticates the server by default in common web use. **mTLS** also asks the client to prove possession of a client private key/certificate. User login and application authorization remain separate.

## 53. Encryption at rest: choose the layer deliberately

| Layer | Protects well against | Does not protect against | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|---|
| Full-disk/device | Lost physical disk/device | Running OS/app with access | Simple, broad baseline |
| Storage/object managed encryption | Raw media, some snapshot/storage compromise | Authorized cloud/storage API and app | Very low app complexity |
| Database transparent encryption | Database files/backups | SQL access through live DB | Transparent; broad key scope |
| Column/field encryption | Database-only dump, selected operators | App plus key access | Query/index complexity |
| Client-side/E2EE | Storage/provider reading content | Compromised endpoints | Maximum provider blindness; feature/recovery cost |

These layers can coexist. Multiple layers only add meaningful defense when their keys, trust boundaries, or threat coverage differ.

## 54. Envelope encryption

Encrypting every large object directly through a remote KMS would create payload-size, latency, cost, and availability problems. Envelope encryption uses a hierarchy:

- **DEK (Data Encryption Key):** random symmetric key that encrypts the data locally.
- **KEK (Key Encryption Key):** protected key in KMS/HSM that wraps/encrypts the DEK.

### Write path

```text
                        KMS / HSM
                           |
                 protect DEK with KEK
                           |
                           v
plaintext --AES-GCM with DEK--> ciphertext
      |                            |
      +--> discard plaintext DEK   +--> store together:
                                         ciphertext
                                         wrapped DEK
                                         key/algorithm version
                                         nonce/tag/AAD metadata
```

### Read path

```text
authorize request
      |
load ciphertext + wrapped DEK + metadata
      |
KMS policy check and unwrap DEK
      |
AEAD verify and decrypt locally
      |
use plaintext briefly; clear key/plaintext buffers where practical
```

### Why this scales

- KMS handles small DEKs, not gigabytes of data.
- A different DEK can limit each object's/user's blast radius.
- KEK rotation can often **rewrap DEKs** without re-encrypting bulk data.
- KMS centralizes authorization and audit events.
- Wrapped DEKs can live beside ciphertext because they still require KEK access.

Google documents this DEK/KEK pattern and recommends locally generated DEKs, a central KEK, and AES-256-GCM as one current envelope-encryption design ([Google Cloud envelope encryption](https://docs.cloud.google.com/kms/docs/envelope-encryption)). AWS S3's SSE-KMS workflow likewise uses a plaintext data key transiently and stores its encrypted copy with the object ([AWS S3 SSE-KMS](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/AmazonS3/latest/userguide/UsingKMSEncryption.html)).

## 55. KMS, HSM, and secret manager

| Component | Main role |
|---|---|
| **KMS** | Managed key lifecycle and cryptographic API with identity policy, versions, audit, and availability features |
| **HSM** | Tamper-resistant hardware boundary that generates/stores keys and performs operations without exporting key material in ordinary use |
| **Secret manager** | Stores retrievable application secrets such as API tokens/passwords; not every stored secret is a cryptographic key |

A KMS may be backed by HSMs, but the abstractions are not identical. In an HLD interview, state whether the application receives plaintext DEKs, sends operations to a service, or performs them inside hardware; that changes threat boundaries and performance.

## 56. Key lifecycle: the part most incomplete answers omit

### Generate

- use a CSPRNG or KMS/HSM;
- attach purpose, owner, region/tenant, algorithm, and creation metadata;
- make keys independent across purposes.

### Distribute/use

- prefer cryptographic service calls, short-lived grants, or wrapped keys over copying root keys;
- authenticate callers and apply least privilege;
- separate encrypt, decrypt, administer, disable, and delete permissions where useful;
- never hard-code keys or commit them to source control.

### Store

- keep KEKs/private keys in KMS/HSM/secure platform storage;
- store wrapped DEKs with ciphertext;
- encrypt backups and protect their keys separately enough to matter;
- prevent keys and ciphertext from being casually exposed in the same logs/configuration dump.

### Rotate

A safe versioned pattern is:

```text
new writes -> active key version v3
old reads  -> select v1/v2/v3 from ciphertext metadata
background -> rewrap or re-encrypt based on risk/policy
retire old version only after all required data is migrated/expired
```

Scheduled rotation reduces future exposure and tests operational readiness. It does not erase plaintext already stolen. A suspected compromise demands incident response, access revocation, scope analysis, and often re-encryption/rewrapping.

### Revoke/disable/delete

- **Disable** first when possible; observe impact and retain a recovery option.
- **Delete** only after inventorying live data, replicas, backups, legal holds, and rollback needs.
- Deleting the only KEK can make every wrapped DEK—and therefore all protected data—unrecoverable.
- “Crypto-shredding” can make data inaccessible by destroying keys, but copies, caches, plaintext exports, and backups must be part of the threat analysis.

## 57. KMS scale, latency, and failure handling

A KMS is both a security boundary and a dependency.

Ask:

- What are request quotas and peak decrypt QPS?
- Is the key regional or multi-region, and what is the failover model?
- Is cross-region key material permitted by policy?
- Can the service cache unwrapped DEKs, and for how long?
- How are caches protected and invalidated after revocation?
- Are audit logs on the hot path or asynchronously delivered?
- What happens when KMS is slow/unavailable?

Trade-off:

```text
no DEK cache:     stronger immediate revocation, higher KMS load/latency
short DEK cache:  lower latency/load, larger plaintext-key exposure window
```

Never “fail open” by returning ciphertext as plaintext, skipping authentication, or silently using a hard-coded fallback key. Depending on the product, fail closed, serve already-safe cached results, or degrade features explicitly.

## 58. A secure document-upload design

**Illustrative design**, not a claim about a specific product.

### Requirements

- authenticated user uploads a document;
- only authorized tenant members can download;
- protect transit and storage;
- objects may be large;
- support key rotation, audit, deletion, and multiple regions.

### Architecture

```text
Client
  |
 TLS + user authentication
  v
Upload API ----authorization/metadata----> SQL metadata DB
  |
  | generate object DEK / obtain wrapped DEK
  v
Streaming encryption worker <-----------> KMS/HSM (tenant KEK)
  |
  | framed/chunked AEAD ciphertext
  v
Object storage

Stored metadata:
object_id, tenant_id, storage_uri, algorithm_suite,
key_id/version, wrapped_DEK, nonce/framing metadata, AAD schema,
content length, status, created_at
```

### Write flow

1. Authenticate and authorize the upload; allocate immutable `object_id` and version.
2. Generate a fresh DEK with a vetted encryption SDK/library.
3. Encrypt in an authenticated streaming/framed format; do not invent “GCM per chunk.” The format must guarantee unique per-frame nonces and authenticate ordering, final-frame status, and object context.
4. Bind `tenant_id`, `object_id`, version, and algorithm context as AAD where the format supports it.
5. Wrap the DEK under the tenant/service KEK.
6. Store ciphertext and wrapped DEK; never log plaintext, DEK, or sensitive request body.
7. Mark metadata `READY` only after durable object commit and integrity metadata are complete. Reconcile abandoned `UPLOADING` records asynchronously.

### Read flow

1. Authenticate and authorize **before** requesting a DEK unwrap.
2. Load the exact object version and cryptographic metadata.
3. Ask KMS to unwrap under an encryption context containing tenant/object identity.
4. Authenticated-decrypt/stream; release no data from a failed or truncated frame.
5. Audit subject, object, outcome, key version, and reason without logging plaintext.

### Edge cases

- **Multipart retry:** retries must not cause nonce reuse under the same DEK.
- **Range read:** use an encryption format designed for independently authenticated frames.
- **Cross-tenant object ID:** authorization plus AAD prevents a copied blob from becoming another tenant's valid object.
- **KMS outage:** uploads/downloads may degrade or queue; do not bypass encryption.
- **Replica/backup:** copy ciphertext and key metadata consistently; ensure the destination can lawfully access the KEK or an approved rewrapped DEK.
- **Delete:** coordinate metadata tombstone, object versions, replicas, backups, and retention policy.
- **Malware upload:** encryption preserves malicious bytes perfectly; scan at a controlled plaintext boundary if the product requires it.

# Part IX — Real-system examples

## 59. How to read these examples

Named companies change implementations. The labels below distinguish documented behavior from a design inference. A public product statement is not evidence for every internal service or every historical version.

## 60. WhatsApp: end-to-end content encryption

**Verified public fact:** Meta states that WhatsApp uses the Signal Protocol as the foundation for end-to-end encrypted chats and uses the Noise Protocol Framework for client-to-server transport in its interoperability architecture ([Meta engineering](https://engineering.fb.com/2024/03/06/security/whatsapp-messenger-messaging-interoperability-eu/)). Its multi-device description says a one-to-one message is encrypted separately for the sender's and recipient's devices, while groups use a Sender Key scheme ([WhatsApp multi-device engineering post](https://engineering.fb.com/2021/07/14/security/whatsapp-multi-device/)).

```text
Alice phone --pairwise E2EE ciphertext--> WhatsApp routing/storage
                                                  |
                             +--------------------+----------------+
                             v                                     v
                        Bob phone                             Bob laptop
                     decrypts locally                       decrypts locally
```

System-design lessons:

- TLS to the service and E2EE serve different threat boundaries.
- Every device needs identity/session keys; “one user = one key” is too simple.
- A new/revoked device changes fan-out and key state.
- The server can route ciphertext but features needing plaintext become difficult.
- Key verification/transparency matters because a maliciously substituted recipient key defeats the intended identity guarantee.
- Encrypted backup and account recovery are separate designs from live-message encryption.

## 61. YouTube: encryption in transit at CDN scale

**Verified historical public fact:** Google described YouTube's multi-year HTTPS migration and reported in 2016 that more than 90% of YouTube traffic was then encrypted, explicitly noting the challenge of serving large video volume through Google Global Cache ([Google Security Blog](https://security.googleblog.com/2016/08/adding-youtube-and-calendar-to-https.html)). That percentage is historical, not asserted as a current 2026 statistic.

```text
Viewer ==HTTPS/TLS==> edge cache/CDN ==protected internal path==> origin/storage
```

System-design lessons:

- Encrypting high-bandwidth video traffic affects CPU, connection setup, certificate operations, caching, and observability.
- The CDN endpoint usually terminates the user's TLS and therefore is inside the plaintext trust boundary.
- HTTPS protects the viewer-to-edge connection; it is not E2EE from the video creator to every viewer.
- CDN authorization, signed URLs/tokens, and DRM/content policy are different controls from TLS encryption.

## 62. Uber: a concrete hybrid-encryption API

**Verified public fact:** Uber's vehicle API documentation describes a hybrid scheme for driver identifiers: a third party supplies a 2048-bit RSA public key; Uber encrypts identifiers using AES-256-GCM, encrypts the symmetric key with RSA-OAEP, and returns the encrypted symmetric key with encrypted fields ([Uber developer documentation](https://developer.uber.com/docs/vehicles/guides/authorization)).

```text
Partner setup:
partner private RSA key stays with partner
partner public RSA key ----------------------> Uber

Response construction:
driver identifiers --AES-256-GCM with random key--> encrypted identifiers
random AES key ------RSA-OAEP partner public key--> encrypted_symmetric_key

Partner:
RSA private key unwraps AES key -> AES-GCM verifies/decrypts identifiers
```

This is the textbook hybrid pattern in a real API:

- AES efficiently handles the payload;
- RSA protects only small key material;
- GCM supplies payload integrity as well as confidentiality;
- the partner's private key determines who can recover the AES key;
- TLS is still required to protect transport metadata and the entire API channel.

## 63. Netflix: TLS and content protection are separate layers

**Verified public fact:** Netflix's help center requires protected-content support and identifies Widevine Content Decryption Module for playback in Chrome ([Netflix Help Center](https://help.netflix.com/en/node/27451/)). Netflix Open Connect publications separately discuss TLS for high-bandwidth delivery and note that DRM is pre-encoded into media objects ([Netflix Open Connect TLS paper](https://openconnect.netflix.com/publications/asiabsd_2015_tls.pdf)).

Simplified conceptual separation:

```text
Netflix video object protected for authorized playback (DRM/content keys)
                    |
                    v
          delivered through HTTPS/TLS
                    |
                    v
browser/app -> protected-content module -> authorized playback path
```

System-design lessons:

- TLS protects the network connection; DRM controls content usage at a potentially hostile client.
- The player ultimately needs a usable decryption capability, so DRM raises the cost of copying rather than making client compromise impossible.
- License/key authorization, device capability, subscription entitlement, and video delivery are related but distinct services.
- Do not describe DRM as ordinary end-to-end private messaging; the service/licensing ecosystem intentionally controls key release.

## 64. X Chat: public/private keys plus per-conversation keys

**Verified current public description:** X says Chat creates a user public/private key pair and uses a per-conversation key to encrypt conversation content; the public/private keys exchange the conversation key. It also describes PIN-based recovery using the Juicebox protocol and key shares across three realms, two hardware-backed ([X Chat help](https://help.x.com/en/using-x/about-chat)). X says it intends to publish further technical details, so do not infer undocumented primitives from the help page.

```text
user key pairs
     |
securely establish/share per-conversation key
     |
message content --conversation key encryption--> X infrastructure
```

System-design lessons:

- Hybrid key hierarchy makes group/conversation data efficient.
- Cloud recovery improves multi-device usability but expands the trust and attack model.
- PIN strength, rate limiting, hardware realms, share threshold, device revocation, and public-key authenticity are part of the cryptographic design.
- “Private key recoverable on a new device” requires a recovery protocol; it is not the same architecture as a device-only non-exportable key.

## 65. Amazon S3 and KMS: envelope encryption at object-storage scale

**Verified public fact:** S3 encrypts new object uploads by default with S3-managed server-side keys; SSE-KMS optionally uses a KMS key and envelope encryption. In the documented SSE-KMS workflow, KMS returns a plaintext data key and encrypted copy, S3 encrypts the object, removes the plaintext key from memory as soon as possible, and stores the encrypted data key with the encrypted object ([AWS S3 SSE-KMS documentation](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/AmazonS3/latest/userguide/UsingKMSEncryption.html)).

```text
                 AWS KMS (KMS key / KEK)
                       |
                 generate/wrap DEK
                       |
                       v
upload -> S3 --encrypt object with DEK--> encrypted object
                       |
                  store wrapped DEK
```

System-design lessons:

- service-managed encryption is different from client-side encryption;
- customer-managed KMS keys add policy/audit/control but also cost, quota, permission, and availability considerations;
- object encryption does not replace bucket/IAM authorization;
- KMS request volume can become a design dimension; S3 documents bucket keys as an optimization that reduces KMS traffic, with a corresponding change in key hierarchy/granularity.

## 66. Google-scale storage: why envelope encryption exists

**Verified public fact:** Google documents encrypting customer content at rest, splitting data into chunks with DEKs, and wrapping DEKs under KEKs held in a central keystore ([Google default encryption at rest](https://docs.cloud.google.com/docs/security/encryption/default-encryption)).

This illustrates a reusable HLD principle:

```text
many data chunks -> many DEKs -> fewer centrally controlled KEKs
```

The hierarchy balances blast radius, throughput, and central policy. It also explains why “one AES key for the whole database” is operationally simple but creates a very large compromise scope.

# Part X — Trade-offs, alternatives, and edge cases

## 68. Symmetric versus asymmetric versus hybrid

| Dimension | Symmetric | Asymmetric | Hybrid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bulk throughput | Best | Poor | Best after setup |
| Key distribution | Hard if manually pre-shared | Public keys can be distributed, but need trust | Public-key setup + symmetric data keys |
| Typical key size | Small | Larger and scheme-dependent | Both |
| Public signatures | No | Yes, with signature scheme | Can include them |
| Forward secrecy | Possible with appropriate evolving/session design | ECDHE provides it in authenticated protocol | Common in TLS/messaging |
| Common use | Storage/session data | Identity, signatures, key establishment | TLS, encrypted files/messages |

Rule of thumb:

> Use symmetric AEAD for data volume; use public-key mechanisms to authenticate and establish/protect the symmetric keys; use a complete protocol or envelope format.

## 69. AES-128 versus AES-256

Both are standardized and widely considered strong when used correctly. The choice depends on policy, desired security margin, performance/platform behavior, compliance, and long-term data lifetime.

- AES-128 uses 10 rounds; AES-256 uses 14.
- AES-256 has a larger key space but can be somewhat slower depending on hardware/software.
- A 256-bit key does not repair nonce reuse, exposed keys, unauthenticated CBC, weak passwords, or a compromised endpoint.
- Do not compare “256” directly to a 256-bit ECC key; bit lengths mean different things across schemes.

In an interview, a well-operated AES-128-GCM design can be stronger than a badly operated AES-256-CBC design. Explain construction and key lifecycle, not just the largest number.

## 70. Encryption at rest versus tokenization versus hashing

| Need | Better starting tool | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Recover original address later | Encryption | Reversible for authorized service |
| Validate password | Password hashing | Original normally need not be recovered |
| Replace card/account identifier across apps | Tokenization | Limits sensitive value distribution; vault controls mapping |
| Check accidental transfer corruption | Checksum | Cheap error detection, not adversarial security |
| Verify artifact publisher | Digital signature | Origin and integrity with public verification |

## 71. Server-side versus client-side encryption

### Server-side

The service receives plaintext and encrypts before storage.

Pros:

- simple clients;
- server-side search, moderation, transformations, and recovery remain possible;
- centralized key rotation and access policy.

Cons:

- server/service operators and compromised application paths can access plaintext;
- provider is inside the trust boundary.

### Client-side/end-to-end

Client encrypts before service receives the content.

Pros:

- storage/provider cannot ordinarily read content;
- stronger separation from server compromise.

Cons:

- device enrollment, group membership, key backup/recovery, abuse handling, search, notification previews, and server processing become difficult;
- lost keys can mean lost data;
- compromised endpoints still see plaintext.

## 72. Search and indexes over encrypted fields

Randomized secure encryption intentionally makes the same plaintext produce different ciphertexts. That defeats ordinary equality indexes.

Options have leakage trade-offs:

- decrypt only after narrowing by non-sensitive fields;
- store a keyed blind index/HMAC for exact-match lookup;
- tokenize the field through a vault;
- use deterministic encryption only under a rigorously reviewed design, accepting that equality/frequency leaks;
- use specialized searchable-encryption research/technology only when its threat model and leakage are understood.

Never promise “fully encrypted and supports every SQL query normally” without explaining leakage or trusted computation.

## 73. Compression, deduplication, and encrypted data

- High-quality ciphertext looks random and normally compresses poorly. Compress before encryption when safe and needed.
- Cross-user compression/deduplication involving secrets and attacker-controlled input can create side channels; protocol context matters.
- Randomized encryption prevents ordinary plaintext deduplication.
- Convergent/deterministic encryption can restore deduplication but leaks equality and enables confirmation attacks for guessable content.

## 74. Multi-region and disaster recovery

Data replication is useless if the recovery region cannot use the required keys. Key replication is dangerous if it expands jurisdiction and compromise scope.

Decide:

- single-region versus multi-region key;
- independent regional KEKs with DEKs rewrapped for each region;
- recovery-time and recovery-point objectives;
- whether failover can decrypt already-written data;
- how key policy and audit work during a control-plane outage;
- how backups retain old key versions;
- which jurisdictions may hold or use key material.

Test disaster recovery. A diagram showing two KMS boxes is not evidence that ciphertext is recoverable.

## 75. Rotation at billion-object scale

Three strategies:

| Strategy | Work | When useful | Risk/trade-off |
|---|---|---|---|
| New key only for new writes | Low | Routine version roll | Old data remains under old key |
| Rewrap each DEK under new KEK | Moderate metadata work | Envelope encryption / KEK rotation | Requires access to old and new KEKs; bulk ciphertext unchanged |
| Decrypt and re-encrypt all data with new DEKs | High I/O/compute | DEK compromise or algorithm/format migration | Long migration, dual-read complexity, large operational risk |

Migration state must be idempotent. Store source/destination key versions, make retries safe, verify before deleting old access, rate-limit background work, and monitor objects that fail conversion.

## 76. Key granularity

| Key scope | Advantage | Cost/risk |
|---|---|---|
| One global data key | Simple | Catastrophic blast radius; rotation painful |
| Per-service key | Better isolation | Still broad within service |
| Per-tenant KEK | Tenant isolation and selective revocation | More key objects, policy/quota management |
| Per-object DEK under shared/per-tenant KEK | Fine blast radius; scalable envelope pattern | More metadata and unwrap operations |
| Per-record key | Maximum granularity | Often excessive operational overhead |

Choose based on threat boundaries, revocation needs, data volume, KMS cost/quotas, and compliance—not “more keys is always better.”

## 77. Common mistakes interviewers listen for

1. **“Base64 encrypts the value.”** It only encodes it.
2. **“Hashing can be decrypted.”** A proper cryptographic hash is one-way; password guesses are tested by recomputation.
3. **“DSA is asymmetric encryption.”** It is a signature algorithm and legacy for new generation under current NIST guidance.
4. **“Diffie-Hellman encrypts the data.”** It establishes shared keying material.
5. **“DH alone authenticates Bob.”** Raw DH is vulnerable to MITM.
6. **“RSA encrypts the whole file.”** Use hybrid encryption; RSA protects small key material.
7. **“A signature is encryption with the private key.”** Use signature-specific semantics and encoding.
8. **“AES-256 means 256-bit blocks.”** AES always has 128-bit blocks.
9. **“AES is secure” without mode/nonce/tag.** The construction is incomplete.
10. **Using ECB.** It leaks patterns.
11. **Reusing a GCM nonce with the same key.** This can destroy confidentiality/authenticity guarantees.
12. **Hard-coding keys next to code/ciphertext.** Key separation and access control are missing.
13. **Encrypting passwords.** Normally use salted adaptive password hashing.
14. **Assuming at-rest encryption stops SQL injection.** The live database/app can decrypt authorized reads.
15. **Calling TLS end-to-end through a terminating proxy.** Each TLS hop has a plaintext endpoint.
16. **Ignoring backups and logs.** Sensitive plaintext often escapes through secondary paths.
17. **Rotating without key version metadata.** Old data becomes unreadable or every key must be guessed.
18. **Deleting a KEK before migration validation.** This can irreversibly destroy data.
19. **Rolling a custom cipher/protocol.** Correct primitives can still compose into an insecure protocol.
20. **Logging detailed decryption errors.** Error oracles and secret leakage may result.

## 78. Subtle edge cases

### Nonce coordination across replicas

If several writers share a key, a process-local counter may repeat after failover or on another node. Use a library/format that defines safe nonce allocation, partition nonce space, or use independent keys per writer as appropriate.

### Retry after uncertain write

A timeout may occur after ciphertext was stored. Retrying with the same nonce but different plaintext under a GCM key is unsafe. Make the encrypted artifact deterministic at the operation level by persisting its generated nonce/ciphertext for retry, or generate a fresh DEK/non-conflicting nonce under a vetted format and treat versions idempotently.

### Associated-data drift

If one service serializes AAD as `tenant=7;object=9` and another uses JSON with different whitespace/order, decryption fails. Version and canonicalize the AAD schema.

### Clock and replay

Encryption does not make an old transfer request invalid. Sign/authenticate a request ID, expiration, audience, and operation details; store/check used IDs where replay matters. Allow bounded clock skew intentionally.

### Key compromise versus algorithm compromise

- **Key compromise:** replace/revoke that key and analyze data exposed under it.
- **Algorithm/construction weakness:** migrate format/algorithm, often requiring bulk re-encryption or re-signing.
- **Implementation compromise:** patch library, rotate affected keys, and assess side-channel/plaintext leakage.

### Insider with decrypt permission

Encryption cannot protect data from a principal explicitly allowed to decrypt it. Add approval workflows, just-in-time grants, separation of duties, detailed audit, anomaly detection, and smaller key/data scope.

### Plaintext in logs and telemetry

Encrypting the primary database while logging request bodies to a searchable analytics system moves the leak. Inventory traces, crash dumps, dead-letter queues, cache snapshots, support exports, and ML pipelines.

### Tag truncation and algorithm agility

Do not shorten tags or allow caller-selected algorithms without protocol guidance. Store an authenticated algorithm-suite/version identifier; make migrations explicit and prevent downgrade to legacy suites.

## 83. Answer spine: “Symmetric versus asymmetric?”

1. **Define:** same shared key versus public/private pair.
2. **Purpose:** symmetric for fast bulk confidentiality; asymmetric for signatures and key establishment/transport.
3. **Constraint:** symmetric key distribution; asymmetric cost and public-key trust.
4. **Reality:** combine them in hybrid encryption.
5. **Example:** TLS uses authenticated ECDHE and then symmetric AEAD traffic keys.
6. **Caveat:** DSA is signature-only; DH/ECDHE is agreement-only.

## 84. Answer spine: “Protect PII at rest.”

1. Classify exact fields and adversary: stolen disk, DB dump, operator, or compromised app?
2. Use cloud/disk encryption as a baseline, then app/field encryption if DB separation is required.
3. Use vetted AEAD, unique nonce rules, and tenant/record context as AAD.
4. Use envelope encryption: per-object/tenant DEK, KMS/HSM KEK, store wrapped DEK and version.
5. Authorize before decrypt; least-privilege KMS identity; audit decrypts.
6. Explain search/index leakage and key-granularity choices.
7. Cover rotation, backups, multi-region, KMS outage, and compromise.
8. State remaining gap: a compromised authorized app can still read plaintext.

## 85. Answer spine: “Explain Diffie-Hellman.”

1. Problem: agree on secret keying material over an observed network.
2. Public parameters `p, g`; private exponents `a, b`.
3. Public values `g^a` and `g^b mod p`.
4. Equality: `(g^b)^a = (g^a)^b = g^(ab)`.
5. Hardness: discrete log hides private exponents for suitable parameters.
6. KDF: derive separate session keys; do not use the raw value directly.
7. Authentication: raw DH has MITM; bind exchange using signature/certificate/PSK.
8. ECDHE: curve-based ephemeral version supporting forward secrecy.

## 86. Answer spine: “Explain AES internally.”

1. Symmetric block cipher; 128-bit blocks.
2. Key variants 128/192/256; rounds 10/12/14.
3. Load 16 bytes into 4 × 4 column-major state.
4. Initial `AddRoundKey`.
5. Full round: `SubBytes -> ShiftRows -> MixColumns -> AddRoundKey`.
6. Final round omits `MixColumns`.
7. AES-128 expansion: four words to 44 words = 11 four-word round keys.
8. HLD return: AES needs a secure mode such as GCM, nonce discipline, tag verification, and managed keys.

## 87. Answer spine: “Digital signature?”

1. Signer uses private signing key; verifier uses trusted public key.
2. Standard algorithm signs a defined message/hash encoding.
3. Provides integrity and data-origin authentication, not confidentiality.
4. Public key needs certificate/fingerprint/directory binding to identity.
5. Sign context, audience, operation, nonce/request ID, and expiry to prevent replay/cross-protocol use.
6. Protect the private key in KMS/HSM and cover rotation/revocation.
7. Qualify non-repudiation with key custody, timestamp, audit, and legal context.

## 89. Fast follow-up answers

### Is plaintext always readable text?

No. It is any unencrypted input data, including binary files and keys.

### Is Base64 encryption?

No. It is publicly reversible encoding with no secret key.

### Can a hash be decrypted?

No. Password attacks guess candidates and recompute hashes; they do not decrypt the digest.

### Does encryption guarantee integrity?

Not by itself. Use authenticated encryption or a correctly composed integrity mechanism.

### Is DSA encryption?

No. DSA is a signature algorithm, and current NIST guidance keeps it only for legacy verification.

### Does Diffie-Hellman encrypt data?

No. It derives a shared secret from which traffic keys are derived.

### Why is raw DH unsafe?

It lacks peer authentication and is vulnerable to a man-in-the-middle.

### What is ECDHE?

Ephemeral elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman; fresh session key agreement that can provide forward secrecy when authenticated in a complete protocol.

### AES key size and block size?

Keys are 128, 192, or 256 bits; the block is always 128 bits.

### What are the AES round counts?

10, 12, and 14 respectively.

### Why 44 words for AES-128?

Eleven state-sized round keys are needed—one initial plus ten rounds—and each has four words.

### Which AES round omits MixColumns?

The final round.

### Why not ECB?

Equal plaintext blocks encrypt equally under one key, leaking structure.

### Is a GCM nonce secret?

Normally no, but it must be unique for each encryption under a given key.

### Why not RSA-encrypt a 5 GB file?

RSA is slow and size-limited. Encrypt the file with a random symmetric DEK and protect that DEK with RSA-OAEP or a KMS KEK.

### What does a digital signature provide?

Message integrity and origin authentication relative to a trusted public key; not confidentiality or replay protection by itself.

### HMAC or signature?

HMAC is fast but every verifier sharing the secret can forge. A signature separates private signing from public verification.

### Does encryption at rest stop SQL injection?

Usually no. The live authorized database/application decrypts data; fix input handling and authorization too.

### What is envelope encryption?

A DEK encrypts data; a KEK in KMS/HSM wraps the DEK. Store ciphertext and wrapped DEK together.

### Why store key version with ciphertext?

So reads can choose the correct historical key during and after rotation.

### What happens when KMS is unavailable?

New unwrap/encrypt operations fail or degrade unless an intentionally bounded cache covers them. Never bypass encryption or use a hard-coded fallback key.

### Does key rotation fix past theft?

No. It limits future use and aids recovery; already exposed plaintext remains exposed.

### Why can encrypted data be hard to search?

Secure randomized encryption hides equality and order. Searchable alternatives leak selected information or require trusted decryption.

## 93. Production design checklist

### Goal and boundary

- [ ] Sensitive data and classification identified.
- [ ] Confidentiality/integrity/authenticity/freshness requirements separated.
- [ ] Threat actors and trusted plaintext endpoints named.
- [ ] In-transit, at-rest, in-use, and E2EE requirements separated.

### Construction

- [ ] Standard, reviewed protocol/library selected.
- [ ] Authenticated encryption used where data is encrypted.
- [ ] Algorithm suite and format version stored/authenticated.
- [ ] Nonce/IV strategy safe across nodes, retries, restore, and failover.
- [ ] AAD schema canonicalized and versioned.
- [ ] Authentication failures fail closed without exposing an oracle.

### Keys

- [ ] Keys from CSPRNG/KMS/HSM, never ordinary PRNG/human text.
- [ ] Key purpose and scope are explicit.
- [ ] KEKs/private keys protected by KMS/HSM/platform secure storage.
- [ ] Least-privilege encrypt/decrypt/admin/delete roles defined.
- [ ] Key ID/version travels with every encrypted artifact.
- [ ] Rotation, compromise, revocation, recovery, and destruction runbooks exist.
- [ ] Backups and replicas retain lawful access to required historical keys.

### Distributed operation

- [ ] KMS quota, latency, cache, regional failure, and cost modeled.
- [ ] Multi-region key strategy tested.
- [ ] Background rewrap/re-encryption is idempotent and observable.
- [ ] Authorization happens before sensitive unwrap/decrypt.
- [ ] Logs, queues, caches, traces, dumps, and analytics do not bypass protection.
- [ ] Search/index/dedup/compression trade-offs are acknowledged.
- [ ] Audit detects abnormal key and decrypt activity.

### Human and device lifecycle

- [ ] Public key is bound to a verified identity.
- [ ] Device addition/removal and group-membership changes rotate the right state.
- [ ] Recovery does not silently defeat the promised trust boundary.
- [ ] Loss of the last key has an explicit product outcome.

# Part XIII — Close

## 95. Say-out-loud 30-second narrative

> Encryption turns plaintext—including binary data—into ciphertext under a key to provide confidentiality; encoding only changes representation, and hashes and signatures solve different problems. In real systems I use symmetric authenticated encryption such as AES-GCM for bulk data, and public-key mechanisms such as authenticated ECDHE or RSA-OAEP to establish or protect those symmetric keys. I define who owns the keys and the plaintext boundary, store master keys in KMS or HSM, use envelope encryption, version every artifact, and plan rotation, failure, backup, and multi-region recovery. The algorithm name is only one part: nonce uniqueness, integrity, public-key trust, authorization, and key lifecycle determine whether the system is actually secure.
