# CAP, PACELC, and Replication Strategies

> Beginner-first HLD study notes expanded from: “CAP Theorem, PACELC, replication strategy.”
>
> The useful question is not “Is this database CP or AP?” It is: **for this particular operation, what will the system do when replicas cannot communicate, what does it wait for when they can communicate, and how will it recover?**

## Scope, assumptions, and confidence labels

I assume “replication strategy” means **data replication in distributed databases**, including:

- where copies are placed;
- which replica may accept a write;
- how many replicas must acknowledge it;
- where reads go;
- how failover and conflicting versions are handled.

This chapter uses four evidence labels:

- **Verified concept** — supported by a paper, standard, or first-party documentation linked nearby.
- **Source-era production example** — a company publicly described this design at the linked publication date. It may not describe that company’s current private architecture.
- **Illustrative design** — a reasonable interview design using a familiar product; it is not a claim about the product’s actual implementation.
- **Self-reported interview experience** — a candidate said they encountered it. These reports are useful practice signals, but are not official or independently verified question banks.

No unsourced production scale numbers are used. Any source-era number is labeled with its source and date.

## 1. Plain-language foundation: one notebook, then several copies

### 1.1 One copy is simple

Imagine a shop has one notebook containing the stock count:

~~~text
blue-shirt stock = 1
~~~

Every purchase and every stock check goes to that notebook. If Alice buys the shirt and the count becomes zero, Bob’s later check sees zero.

This is easy to reason about, but the notebook is also:

- a single failure point;
- a throughput bottleneck;
- slow for users far away from it.

### 1.2 Replication creates copies

The shop therefore keeps copies in Mumbai and Delhi:

~~~text
                    replication
Mumbai copy  <---------------------->  Delhi copy
stock = 1                               stock = 1
~~~

Copies can improve survival, read capacity, and geographic latency. But they create a new question:

> When one copy changes, when and how must every other copy learn about it?

If Alice buys through Mumbai while the link to Delhi is broken, Mumbai may say zero while Delhi still says one. A Delhi request now forces a choice:

- **preserve one correct view:** refuse or delay the Delhi operation until it can coordinate; or
- **keep serving:** answer from Delhi even though that answer may be stale, or accept another write that may conflict.

That is the intuition behind CAP.

### 1.3 PACELC asks about the ordinary day too

Suppose the Mumbai–Delhi link is healthy. To keep both copies strongly consistent, Mumbai may wait for Delhi before confirming Alice’s purchase. The answer is safer, but the wide-area round trip adds latency.

Alternatively, Mumbai can confirm immediately and send the change to Delhi asynchronously. The response is faster, but Delhi can temporarily lag.

That normal-operation choice is the intuition behind PACELC:

~~~text
If there is a Partition:      choose Availability or Consistency
Else, while healthy:          choose lower Latency or stronger Consistency
~~~

### 1.4 Replication, sharding, and backup are different

These terms are often mixed up in beginner notes:

| Mechanism | Plain-language meaning | Main purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Replication** | Keep multiple copies of the same data | Availability, durability, locality, read scale |
| **Sharding / partitioning data** | Split different data across machines | Capacity and write/read throughput |
| **Backup** | Keep a recoverable historical copy, normally isolated from live writes | Recovery from deletion, corruption, ransomware, or operator error |
| **Network partition** | Some live nodes cannot exchange messages even though they may still be running | A failure condition, not data sharding |

A common real design uses all three: shard the dataset, replicate each shard, and back it up separately.

Replication is **not** a backup. An accidental deletion or corrupt update can be faithfully copied to every live replica.

## 2. Inline prerequisites

These concepts are required for CAP and replication, so they are explained here rather than deferred.

### 2.1 Node, replica, read, and write

- A **node** is a participating server/process.
- A **replica** is a node holding a copy of some data.
- A **write** changes state: create an order, decrement stock, update a profile.
- A **read** observes state: fetch an order, check stock, load a profile.
- A **client** can be an end-user application or another service.

One physical database node may hold replicas for many shards. “Three replicas” means three copies of a given data unit, not necessarily only three machines in the whole database.

### 2.2 Failure is not the same as partition

A crashed node is clearly unavailable. A **network partition** is more deceptive:

~~~text
             link broken or messages indefinitely lost
Replica A  X-------------------------------------------X  Replica B
   alive                                                   alive
~~~

Both sides may still accept client traffic, but they cannot learn what the other side is doing. A long delay is operationally indistinguishable from a broken link until a message arrives; the system cannot instantly know whether a peer is dead, slow, or unreachable.

### 2.3 Latency, availability, durability, and consistency are separate

- **Latency:** how long an operation takes.
- **Availability in everyday operations:** how often the service is usable, often measured by an SLI/SLO.
- **CAP availability:** a specific theoretical property explained later.
- **Durability:** once a write is reported successful, will it survive failures?
- **Consistency model:** rules describing which values reads may return and how operations appear to be ordered.

A system can reply quickly with stale data: low latency, high practical availability, weak consistency. It can also reply quickly and then lose the acknowledged write after a failure: low latency, but weak durability.

### 2.4 The consistency ladder needed in this chapter

“Consistent” is not a single vague setting. Useful models include:

| Model | Beginner intuition | Example user-visible behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Linearizable / strong single-object consistency** | Operations appear to happen one at a time on one up-to-date copy, respecting real-time order | After Alice’s stock-decrement completes, Bob’s later stock read cannot return the older count |
| **Causal consistency** | Cause is seen before effect; unrelated concurrent operations need not share one global order | A reply is not shown before the post it replies to |
| **Read-your-writes** | A user sees their own completed updates | After editing a profile, that user does not see the old bio |
| **Monotonic reads** | Once a user has seen version 5, they do not later fall back to version 4 | Timeline refresh does not move backward for one session |
| **Eventual consistency** | If writes stop and communication resumes, replicas eventually converge | A like count or profile replica can be briefly stale but later agrees |

Linearizability is the “C” in the formal CAP result: the system behaves like one atomic copy for the object being discussed. It is stronger and more precise than “all nodes have identical bytes at every instant.”

**Eventual** does not mean “within a guaranteed five seconds.” It only promises convergence under stated assumptions; a bounded-staleness guarantee is a separate, stronger promise.

For a deeper model hierarchy, see Jepsen’s [consistency-model reference](https://jepsen.io/consistency/models) and [linearizability explanation](https://jepsen.io/consistency/models/linearizable).

### 2.5 CAP consistency is not ACID consistency

The word **consistency** is overloaded:

- **CAP consistency** normally means linearizable single-copy behavior.
- **ACID consistency** means a transaction preserves application/database invariants, assuming the transaction and constraints encode them.

ACID’s isolation property is more directly related to concurrent observations; strict serializability combines transaction isolation with real-time ordering. Saying “SQL has C because it is ACID” is therefore a category error.

### 2.6 Concurrent writes and stale reads are different problems

- **Replication lag** creates a stale read: one replica has not received/applied a completed update.
- **Concurrent writes** create divergent versions: two sides update the same logical value without first agreeing on one order.

Both can occur together, but their remedies differ. Waiting, leader reads, or session routing can reduce stale reads. Divergent writes need ordering, rejection, or conflict resolution.

## 3. Why replication is needed in HLD

Replication is not added merely because “large systems use replicas.” Tie it to a requirement.

### 3.1 Availability and failover

If one copy fails, another may serve:

~~~text
Client --> Replica A (failed)
             |
             +---- failover/reroute ----> Replica B
~~~

This works only if B has sufficiently current data, clients can discover/reroute to it, and the system safely prevents two conflicting primaries.

### 3.2 Durability

Multiple failure-independent copies reduce the chance that a disk or machine loss destroys the only copy. Durability depends on:

- how many copies stored the write before success was returned;
- whether those copies share a failure domain;
- whether data reached durable storage rather than only volatile memory;
- whether failures are correlated.

Three copies in one rack do not protect against losing that rack.

### 3.3 Read throughput

Read-only replicas can spread read traffic:

~~~text
                 +--> Replica 1
Read router -----+--> Replica 2
                 +--> Replica 3
~~~

But a read replica may lag. “Scale reads with replicas” is incomplete until the design says which reads may be stale.

### 3.4 Geographic latency

A nearby replica can answer a user faster than a far-away primary. The trade-off is whether it may answer locally, must contact a remote leader, or must coordinate with several regions.

### 3.5 Disaster recovery

A copy in another region can support recovery after a regional disaster. Replication alone still needs:

- a tested promotion/failover process;
- routing changes;
- capacity in the recovery region;
- an understood **RPO** (acceptable data-loss window);
- an understood **RTO** (acceptable restoration-time target);
- independent backups.

### 3.6 What replication does not automatically solve

Replication by itself does not guarantee:

- zero downtime;
- no data loss;
- strong consistency;
- exactly-once operations;
- protection from corrupt writes or deletions;
- safe failover;
- unlimited read or write scaling.

Those properties depend on the replication protocol and application design.

## 4. CAP theorem, carefully

### 4.1 The accurate interview statement

> In a distributed shared-data system, **while a network partition prevents the required replicas from communicating**, it is impossible to guarantee both linearizable consistency and CAP availability for every operation. The design must sometimes reject/delay an operation to preserve consistency, or complete it without coordination and risk stale/conflicting state to preserve availability.

Seth Gilbert and Nancy Lynch formalized Brewer’s conjecture in 2002 in [“Brewer’s Conjecture and the Feasibility of Consistent, Available, Partition-Tolerant Web Services”](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/564585.564601). Eric Brewer later explained that the popular “pick two out of three” wording is misleading because the forced trade-off is specifically during partitions and can be made at fine granularity ([Brewer, 2012](https://www.infoq.com/articles/cap-twelve-years-later-how-the-rules-have-changed/)).

### 4.2 The three letters

| Property | Precise beginner meaning | During a partition, what the user may see |
|---|---|---|
| **C — Consistency** | Reads/writes act as if there were one linearizable copy | An isolated side may reject or wait rather than expose an older/conflicting value |
| **A — Availability** | Every request reaching a non-failing node eventually receives a response permitted by the service interface | Both reachable sides keep responding, possibly from divergent or stale state |
| **P — Partition tolerance** | The system’s model/design allows communication between groups of nodes to be lost while the system has defined behavior | Nodes cannot assume they can always coordinate |

Two precision notes:

1. In the formal asynchronous model, “eventually responds” has no fixed deadline. Real systems use timeouts and SLOs, so **CAP availability and a 99.9% uptime metric are not interchangeable**.
2. “P” is not usually a feature you toggle off in a multi-node, multi-zone system. The network can partition whether the architect likes it or not. The practical question is what the system sacrifices when it does.

### 4.3 Why C and A cannot both be guaranteed during the partition

Start with two replicas that both store <code>x = 0</code>:

~~~text
Initial:

Client 1 --> Replica A: x = 0  <------healthy link------>  Replica B: x = 0
~~~

Now sever the link:

~~~text
Partition:

Client 1 --> Replica A: write x = 1       X       Replica B <-- Client 2: read x
~~~

Replica B cannot tell whether:

- A accepted <code>x = 1</code>;
- A is merely slow;
- A failed;
- no write occurred.

B has only two relevant choices:

1. **Return x = 0.** The read completes, preserving availability, but violates linearizability if A already confirmed <code>x = 1</code>.
2. **Refuse or wait for coordination.** It avoids returning a known-possibly-stale value, preserving consistency, but the read is unavailable under CAP’s definition.

No algorithm can manufacture the missing information. That indistinguishability is the proof intuition.

### 4.4 CP behavior

A **CP-style operation** preserves consistency during the partition and gives up availability on any side that cannot prove it is safe to proceed.

One common implementation uses a leader and a majority:

~~~text
Three voting replicas: A, B, C

Partition:        A <----> B       X       C
                 majority                 minority

A/B can elect/retain a valid leader and commit.
C cannot obtain a majority, so it rejects writes.
~~~

Good fits:

- allocating the last ticket;
- account balance/debit invariants;
- lock ownership;
- unique username reservation;
- configuration or metadata where two active owners would be unsafe.

Costs:

- the minority side is unavailable for those operations;
- leader election and coordination add latency;
- if no majority is reachable, progress stops;
- operations may return timeout/unknown outcomes and still need idempotency.

“CP” does not mean the whole product is down. The affected write might be blocked while cached pages, media, search, or stale read-only views remain available.

### 4.5 AP behavior

An **AP-style operation** continues on multiple isolated sides and accepts that state may temporarily diverge:

~~~text
Before partition:  cart = {book}

Region A adds pen             X             Region B adds mug
cart = {book, pen}                          cart = {book, mug}

After healing: reconcile --> {book, pen, mug}   (if union is the chosen rule)
~~~

Good fits when operations can be merged, compensated, or tolerate staleness:

- social likes and counters where an approximate temporary value is acceptable;
- presence/“last seen”;
- some shopping-cart additions;
- telemetry and analytics;
- draft edits with conflict-aware merging.

Costs:

- stale reads;
- conflicting versions;
- application-specific merge logic;
- temporary invariant violations;
- surprising user experience if conflict resolution discards intent.

AP does **not** mean “there is no consistency.” It usually means weaker guarantees during the partition plus eventual convergence afterward.

### 4.6 What about CA?

The label **CA** is often taught as “a system that chooses consistency and availability but not partition tolerance.” The problem is that a real multi-node network can partition. A design can assume a fault model with no partitions, or behave C+A while healthy, but it cannot promise to keep both if the required communication is actually lost.

A single-node database is not a useful escape hatch:

- it avoids replica disagreement;
- if that one node is unreachable, it is unavailable;
- CAP was formulated for replicated/distributed shared state.

The interview-safe wording is:

> In a partitionable distributed system, P is a failure condition I must handle. Under that condition I choose C or A per operation; without a partition I can normally have both, subject to ordinary failures and latency.

### 4.7 CAP is per operation, not a database personality test

One application can make different choices:

| Product operation | Partition-time choice | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Create a payment ledger entry | Prefer C; reject/queue on unsafe side | Duplicate or inconsistent money movement is unacceptable |
| Show product reviews | Prefer A; stale view acceptable | Reading a slightly older review list is better than an error |
| Reserve the last seat | Prefer C | Overselling violates a hard invariant |
| Accept a “like” | Often prefer A and merge later | Temporary count divergence is tolerable |
| Display account statement cache | Possibly A for an explicitly stale view | It must not be used to authorize spending |

Even within one database, read concern, write concern, transaction type, region, or table can change the behavior. Avoid saying “MongoDB is CP” or “Cassandra is AP” without naming configuration, operation, and failure case.

### 4.8 Partition recovery is part of the design

Brewer’s later guidance emphasizes more than the moment of choice:

~~~text
detect or suspect partition
          |
          v
enter explicit partition behavior
  - reject unsafe writes
  - serve labeled stale data
  - queue intent
  - limit operations
          |
          v
communication returns
  - compare versions/logs
  - reconcile or replay
  - compensate invalid outcomes
  - restore normal routing
  - audit what happened
~~~

An answer that says only “we choose AP” has not explained convergence, conflicts, or user-visible repair.

## 5. PACELC: what happens when there is no partition?

### 5.1 Why CAP is incomplete for everyday design

Partitions matter, but most requests occur while replicas can communicate. Replication still forces a choice:

- wait for remote agreement and get stronger consistency; or
- respond using nearby/fewer replicas and get lower latency.

Daniel Abadi described this broader model in [“Consistency Tradeoffs in Modern Distributed Database System Design: CAP is Only Part of the Story”](https://www.cs.umd.edu/~abadi/papers/abadi-pacelc.pdf).

PACELC is commonly called a theorem, but it is best used in an interview as a **design principle/classification model**. CAP has a formal impossibility proof under a defined model; PACELC highlights an ever-present engineering trade-off introduced by replication.

### 5.2 Decode the acronym

~~~text
PACELC

P  -> if there is a Partition,
A/C   choose Availability or Consistency;
E  -> Else (normal operation),
L/C   choose lower Latency or stronger Consistency.
~~~

Read it aloud as:

> “If P, A versus C; else, L versus C.”

### 5.3 A healthy-network example

Assume one replica in India and one in the US.

**Stronger-consistency path**

~~~text
User --> India leader --> send/coordinate with US replica --> ACK
        <---------------- wait for required agreement --------
~~~

- Later reads can rely on a stronger committed state.
- The write pays inter-region network latency.
- A slow required replica can increase tail latency or make the operation fail.

**Lower-latency path**

~~~text
User --> India replica --> local ACK
                           |
                           +---- asynchronously replicate ----> US
~~~

- The user gets a faster response.
- The US copy can temporarily be stale.
- A failure before replication may lose an acknowledged update, depending on the durability setup.

The speed-of-light/network-distance cost cannot be removed by a faster CPU or index.

### 5.4 PACELC labels

A system or configuration is sometimes summarized with two choices:

| Label | During partition | Else, while healthy | Typical priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PA/EL** | Availability | Lower latency | Local responses and eventual convergence |
| **PA/EC** | Availability | Stronger consistency | Keep serving in partition mode but coordinate more during normal mode |
| **PC/EL** | Consistency | Lower latency | Reject unsafe partition-time work, but permit weaker/faster normal reads for some paths |
| **PC/EC** | Consistency | Stronger consistency | Coordinate for correctness in both failure and normal paths |

These labels compress nuance. A real product can be PC/EC for inventory writes, PA/EL for like counts, and something in between for profile reads. Many databases expose tunable read/write settings, so classifying the product name alone is rarely precise.

### 5.5 CAP and PACELC together

~~~text
                       Are required replicas able to communicate?
                                  /                 \
                                no                   yes
                                |                     |
                     CAP partition trade-off    PACELC normal trade-off
                         /             \          /               \
                    availability   consistency  latency       consistency
                    + reconcile    + reject     + possible    + coordination
                                                    staleness
~~~

### 5.6 A better way to use PACELC in interviews

Do not merely attach a four-letter label. State:

1. which data/operation needs which consistency guarantee;
2. which replicas are on the critical path;
3. how many acknowledgements are required;
4. the latency consequence;
5. what happens during a partition;
6. how lag/conflicts are repaired afterward.

## 6. Replication strategy: the full design space

“Single leader, multi-leader, or leaderless” is only one axis. A complete replication strategy answers several independent questions.

| Axis | Questions to answer |
|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Failover, durability, read scale, geo-locality, disaster recovery, or all of them? |
| **Data placement** | How many copies? Which zones/regions/racks? Full copy or subset? |
| **Write ownership** | One leader, several leaders, any replica, or a consensus leader per shard? |
| **Acknowledgement** | Synchronous, asynchronous, semi-synchronous, quorum? Durable log or memory? |
| **Read routing** | Leader, follower, local replica, quorum, or session-pinned replica? |
| **Consistency** | Linearizable, causal, session guarantees, bounded staleness, eventual? |
| **Conflict handling** | Prevent, detect/reject, last-write-wins, merge, version vectors, CRDT, application resolution? |
| **Failover** | How is a new leader chosen? How is the old one fenced? What is RPO/RTO? |
| **Repair** | Log catch-up, hinted handoff, read repair, anti-entropy, snapshot restore? |
| **Operations** | How are lag, quorum health, disk loss, split brain, and regional failure tested and alerted? |

The three common write-ownership topologies come next.

## 7. Single-leader replication

Also called primary–replica or leader–follower replication.

### 7.1 How it works

One replica accepts writes. Followers copy an ordered change log:

~~~text
                         replication log
Client --write--> Leader ----------------------> Follower 1
                    |
                    +--------------------------> Follower 2

Client --read-------------------------------> leader or follower
~~~

The leader provides one place to order writes, which simplifies conflict prevention and many constraints.

### 7.2 Synchronous versus asynchronous followers

**Asynchronous**

~~~text
Client --> Leader: write
Client <-- Leader: success
                 \
                  \ later --> Follower
~~~

Benefits:

- lower write latency;
- leader can keep accepting writes while a follower is slow;
- good for read replicas and cross-region disaster-recovery copies.

Risks:

- followers can be stale;
- an acknowledged write can be lost if the leader dies before any eligible follower receives it;
- failover can promote a lagging replica.

**Synchronous**

~~~text
Client --> Leader --> required follower(s)
Client <-- success only after required ACK(s)
~~~

Benefits:

- a successfully acknowledged write exists on the required additional replica(s);
- reduces the failover data-loss window;
- can support stronger read/durability guarantees, depending on when an ACK is sent.

Costs:

- write latency includes the required replica path;
- a slow/unreachable required replica can block or fail writes;
- waiting for storage is not the same as waiting until a follower has applied the write and can serve it.

**Semi-synchronous** normally means the leader waits for at least one or a configured subset of followers, not every follower. Exact semantics vary by database, so always read the product definition.

PostgreSQL streaming replication is asynchronous by default, while synchronous settings can wait for standby receipt, durable flush, or even replay depending on configuration ([PostgreSQL documentation](https://www.postgresql.org/docs/16/warm-standby.html)).

### 7.3 Read replicas and replication lag

Suppose a user changes their name on the leader, then a load balancer sends the next read to a lagging follower:

~~~text
1. write "Asha"  --> Leader: version 8
2. immediate read ----------------------> Follower: version 7, returns old name
~~~

Mitigations:

- read from the leader for correctness-sensitive data;
- after a write, pin that user/session to the leader until a log position is visible;
- carry a required version/log offset and wait or reroute if the replica is behind;
- use a database-provided causal/session consistency token;
- tolerate and clearly scope stale reads;
- monitor lag in both bytes/events and time.

Blindly sending every read to a follower can break read-your-writes and monotonic-read expectations.

### 7.4 Leader failure and safe failover

A credible failover design needs:

1. **Failure detection:** suspect the old leader using leases, heartbeats, or missed communication.
2. **Election/promotion:** select an eligible, sufficiently caught-up replica.
3. **Fencing:** ensure the old leader cannot continue accepting valid writes.
4. **Routing update:** direct clients to the new leader.
5. **Catch-up/repair:** rejoin recovered replicas safely.

The dangerous outcome is **split brain**:

~~~text
             network partition
Old leader  X----------------X  promoted new leader
 accepts writes                   accepts writes
~~~

Both sides may believe they are primary. Fencing can use a majority lease/term, consensus epoch, or storage-level token so writes from an old term are rejected. “Health checks will elect another leader” is not enough; failure detectors can be wrong.

### 7.5 When to use and avoid

Use single leader when:

- writes need a simple global order per shard;
- relational constraints/transactions are important;
- most writes can go to one region or shard leader;
- follower staleness is acceptable for selected reads.

Avoid or redesign when:

- every region must accept local writes during inter-region isolation;
- one leader becomes a hot write bottleneck and sharding cannot solve it;
- offline/multi-site writers must operate independently;
- the failover/data-loss semantics do not meet the invariant.

## 8. Multi-leader replication

Also called multi-primary or active–active write replication.

### 8.1 How it works

Several leaders accept writes and replicate them to each other:

~~~text
Client India --> Leader India ----async----> Leader US <-- Client US
                      ^                           |
                      +-----------async-----------+
~~~

It can lower local write latency and allow regions to keep accepting writes when cross-region connectivity fails.

### 8.2 The hard part: concurrent conflicts

Both regions read <code>email = old@example.com</code>:

~~~text
India writes email = a@example.com
US writes    email = b@example.com
~~~

When communication resumes, what wins?

Conflict approaches:

- **Avoid:** route all writes for one user/entity to a home region.
- **Reject/detect:** use versions and ask the application/user to resolve.
- **Last-write-wins (LWW):** choose by timestamp or deterministic version. Simple, but can silently discard valid intent and depends on clock/version semantics.
- **Field-level merge:** preserve changes to independent fields.
- **Domain merge:** union cart additions, but treat removals carefully.
- **CRDT:** choose a data type whose concurrent operations deterministically converge.
- **Serialize selected operations:** send invariant-sensitive work through a consensus/leader path.

No generic merge can preserve every business invariant. Two regions can each issue the “last” seat or claim the same username unless that operation coordinates or the resource is pre-allocated into disjoint pools.

### 8.3 Useful fits

- geographically separate writers updating mostly disjoint records;
- offline-first/mobile synchronization;
- collaborative editing with an intentional merge model;
- directory/configuration systems designed for convergent updates;
- active–active workloads whose operations commute.

Avoid as the default for:

- money ledgers;
- global uniqueness;
- inventory with a hard non-negative invariant;
- cross-record constraints;
- any domain where “last timestamp wins” would lose unacceptable intent.

### 8.4 Topology and operational concerns

Multi-leader links may form:

- all-to-all replication;
- a ring;
- a hub-and-spoke;
- region pairs.

Design for duplicate delivery, reordering, loops, clock skew, schema changes, backfill, and a region rejoining after a long outage. Multi-leader moves complexity from write routing into conflict correctness and operations.

## 9. Leaderless / Dynamo-style replication

“Leaderless” means no fixed leader owns all writes for a key. A client or coordinator sends an operation to several replicas.

### 9.1 Basic write and read path

For replication factor <code>N = 3</code>:

~~~text
                         +--> Replica A -- ACK
Client --> Coordinator --+--> Replica B -- ACK
                         +--> Replica C -- slow/down

Return write success after W required ACKs.
~~~

A read asks <code>R</code> replicas and reconciles versions:

~~~text
                         +<-- Replica A: version 8
Client <-- Coordinator --+<-- Replica B: version 7
                         +<-- Replica C: timeout

Coordinator chooses/merges the newest valid version and may repair B.
~~~

The coordinator is a request role, not necessarily a permanent leader. Any suitable node may coordinate a request.

### 9.2 N, W, and R

- <code>N</code>: number of designated replicas for the key.
- <code>W</code>: write acknowledgements required before success.
- <code>R</code>: read responses consulted before returning.

Example:

~~~text
N = 3, W = 2, R = 2

Write set has 2 replicas.
Read set has 2 replicas.
Any two subsets of a fixed three-member set must overlap.
~~~

For **strict quorums over the same replica set**, <code>W + R > N</code> ensures at least one read replica overlaps the acknowledged write set. Common majority choice: <code>W = 2, R = 2, N = 3</code>.

But the formula alone does **not** magically guarantee linearizability. The design must also handle:

- concurrent writes;
- how versions are ordered;
- whether the overlapping replica returns the acknowledged value;
- failed/incomplete writes;
- membership changes;
- sloppy quorums that temporarily write outside the normal replica set;
- read and write timing.

The accurate phrase is: **quorum intersection is a building block for stronger consistency, not the whole proof.**

### 9.3 Tunable trade-offs

| Setting for N = 3 | Effect |
|---|---|
| W = 1 | Fast/highly available writes, but a greater durability/staleness window |
| W = 3 | All designated replicas must ACK; high write consistency/durability cost, low write availability |
| R = 1 | Fast read, but more chance of staleness |
| R = 3 | Compare all replicas; slower and unavailable if any required response is missing |
| W = 2, R = 2 | Majority overlap under strict assumptions; tolerates one missing replica |

Apache Cassandra exposes per-operation consistency levels such as ONE, QUORUM, ALL, LOCAL_QUORUM, and EACH_QUORUM. Its documentation also explains that writes are sent to replicas while the selected level controls how many responses the coordinator waits for ([Cassandra Dynamo architecture](https://cassandra.apache.org/doc/latest/cassandra/architecture/dynamo.html)).

### 9.4 Sloppy quorum and hinted handoff

If a designated replica is down, an availability-oriented system may temporarily store its write on another healthy node:

~~~text
Normal owners: A, B, C
C down

Write --> A, B, temporary D
                         |
                         +-- hint: deliver this to C later
~~~

This is **hinted handoff**. It improves write availability, but the successful write set may not intersect a later read’s normal replica set in the way the simple formula assumes. Background repair remains necessary.

### 9.5 Convergence mechanisms

- **Read repair:** a read notices an older replica and updates it.
- **Hinted handoff:** a temporary holder forwards missed writes when the target returns.
- **Anti-entropy repair:** replicas compare summaries, often Merkle trees, and synchronize differences.
- **Version metadata:** timestamps, logical clocks, or version vectors help determine order/concurrency.
- **Conflict merge:** LWW, application merge, or a convergent data type resolves siblings.

Read repair only fixes frequently read keys. Anti-entropy is needed for cold data too.

### 9.6 When to use and avoid

Use when:

- high write availability and horizontal scale dominate;
- operations tolerate eventual consistency or have a safe merge;
- per-operation consistency tuning is valuable;
- there is no need for broad cross-partition transactions/constraints.

Avoid as the easy default when:

- application developers cannot safely reason about stale/conflicting data;
- strict multi-record invariants dominate;
- repair and operational complexity are unacceptable;
- a simpler single-leader database meets the scale and availability target.

Amazon’s 2007 Dynamo paper is the canonical source-era example: it describes a highly available key-value store that used object versioning and application-assisted conflict resolution, sacrificing consistency in some failure scenarios ([Amazon Science publication page](https://www.amazon.science/publications/dynamo-amazons-highly-available-key-value-store)).

## 10. Consensus-based replicated state machines

This category is important because “a database has a leader” does not tell you whether failover is safe.

### 10.1 Core idea

Protocols such as Raft/Paxos-family consensus arrange for a majority to agree on an ordered log:

~~~text
Client --> current leader --> append log entry to followers
                              |
                              +--> majority acknowledges
Leader marks committed and applies/responds
Followers apply entries in committed order
~~~

Only a leader with authority for a particular term/epoch may commit. A minority partition cannot elect a new valid leader, so it stops accepting strongly consistent writes.

### 10.2 Why this differs from basic async primary–replica

| Basic asynchronous primary–replica | Consensus-replicated log |
|---|---|
| Leader may ACK before a follower has the write | Commit normally requires a quorum |
| Promotion can lose acknowledged writes | Committed entries survive as long as quorum assumptions hold |
| Split-brain prevention may be external/product-specific | Terms/quorums are central to the protocol |
| Often chosen for simpler read scaling/HA | Chosen for safe ordering and leader change |

Consensus still has costs:

- majority coordination latency;
- unavailability without a quorum;
- leader hotspots per shard;
- complex implementation and membership changes.

Google Spanner is a production example of synchronous, leader-based replication where writes are processed by a leader and require votes from a majority of voting replicas; Google documents global strong consistency and the latency/locality implications ([Spanner replication documentation](https://docs.cloud.google.com/spanner/docs/replication?hl=en)).

## 11. Synchronous versus asynchronous is orthogonal to topology

Do not equate:

- single leader with synchronous;
- multi-leader with asynchronous;
- leaderless with eventual consistency.

They commonly correlate, but they are separate decisions.

| Acknowledgement policy | Benefit | Cost/risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Asynchronous** | Low latency; slow replicas do not block | Lag; failover data loss window; stale reads |
| **Synchronous to all** | All required copies current before success | Latency of slowest required replica; poor write availability |
| **Synchronous to quorum/subset** | Survives some replica failures; lower cost than all | Quorum latency; minority cannot progress |
| **Local synchronous + remote asynchronous** | Stronger within region and low user latency; remote DR/active copy | Cross-region staleness and regional-loss RPO |

A very common multi-region compromise is:

~~~text
Within local region:    synchronously replicate across zones
Across regions:         asynchronously replicate for latency/isolation
~~~

That yields good local durability and low latency, but it does not create zero-RPO recovery from total regional loss.

## 12. Replica placement: copies must fail independently

Choosing a replication factor without placement is incomplete.

### 12.1 Failure domains

~~~text
Bad placement:                         Better placement:

Rack 1                                 Zone A       Zone B       Zone C
  - Replica A                            - A          - B          - C
  - Replica B
  - Replica C

One rack failure loses all copies.     One zone failure leaves two copies.
~~~

Consider correlated failure at each level:

- disk;
- machine;
- rack/power/network switch;
- availability zone;
- region;
- cloud/provider;
- software version or operator action.

Placement should match the failure requirement. Cross-region copies help regional resilience but cost more latency, bandwidth, operational complexity, and sometimes regulatory exposure.

### 12.2 Replication factor is not quorum size

With <code>N = 5</code> replicas, the system need not wait for all five. It might require a majority of three, a local quorum, or a leader plus one synchronous follower. State both:

- **how many copies exist**; and
- **how many/current which copies are on the critical path**.

### 12.3 Witness replicas

A witness may vote in consensus without storing a full serving copy of all data. This can help form a geographically sensible quorum with lower storage cost, but it cannot serve as a full replacement data copy. Product-specific semantics matter.

### 12.4 Data residency and locality

Geo-replication can conflict with:

- legal requirements about where personal data is stored;
- tenant-specific residency;
- encryption/key-management boundaries;
- cross-border egress costs;
- deletion obligations.

In an interview, “replicate globally” is not free or universally allowed.

## 13. Replication lag: anomalies and mitigations

### 13.1 Common anomalies

#### Read-your-writes violation

~~~text
User updates profile --> leader
User reloads ---------> lagging follower --> old profile
~~~

#### Monotonic-read violation

~~~text
Read 1 --> up-to-date follower: version 9
Read 2 --> lagging follower:    version 8
~~~

The user appears to go backward in time.

#### Causal-order violation

~~~text
Replica sees: "Great point!" (reply)
Replica has not seen: original post
~~~

#### Stale authorization

A permission is revoked on the leader, but a lagging replica still says “allowed.” This is much more dangerous than a stale like count. Security decisions often need leader/strong reads, short-lived capabilities with deliberate revocation semantics, or fail-closed behavior.

#### Double allocation

Two writers each observe “one item available” on stale state and both allocate it. This is not fixed by eventually making the replicas agree; the physical/business invariant has already been violated.

### 13.2 Mitigation patterns

| Requirement | Useful pattern | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| User must see own update | Leader read, session stickiness, version token | More leader load or routing state |
| Reads must never go backward | Track minimum version per session | Metadata and rerouting/waiting |
| Staleness bounded to a limit | Reject/reroute replicas beyond lag threshold | Lower read availability |
| Hard invariant | Conditional write/CAS/transaction on authoritative path | Coordination latency |
| Approximate display | Serve local stale value with timestamp | Product must tolerate/explain staleness |
| Analytics | Append events and reconcile later | Not suitable for immediate authorization |

### 13.3 Measure lag in a useful way

“Replica lag = 200 MB” does not directly tell the user impact. Observe:

- time since the last applied commit;
- log sequence/offset difference;
- operations waiting to apply;
- oldest unreplicated acknowledged write;
- per-shard and tail-percentile lag;
- whether the replica is eligible for promotion;
- reconciliation backlog after an outage.

Alert thresholds should come from the feature’s staleness/RPO requirement.

## 14. Conflict resolution: preserving intent

### 14.1 Last-write-wins is not neutral

LWW chooses one version, often using a timestamp plus a tie-breaker:

~~~text
Version A: add pen to cart      timestamp 10:00:02
Version B: add mug to cart      timestamp 10:00:03

LWW result: only B, if each version overwrote the full cart
~~~

The replicas converge, but Alice’s pen disappears. **Convergence is not the same as correctness.**

Clock skew can also misorder wall-clock timestamps. Even with perfect ordering, “later” is not necessarily the business rule for “correct.”

### 14.2 Version vectors / logical causality

Version metadata can distinguish:

- A happened before B, so B supersedes A; from
- A and B were concurrent, so neither causally supersedes the other.

The system can then retain both siblings for a domain merge instead of silently overwriting one. Version-vector mechanics are beyond this chapter’s prerequisite depth, but the interview point is simple: **detect concurrency before deciding how to merge it**.

### 14.3 Domain-specific merge

Pick the merge from the business operation:

| Data | Possible merge | Pitfall |
|---|---|---|
| Shopping-cart additions | Union item additions | Remove versus re-add needs explicit semantics |
| Like set | Add/remove-aware set or event dedupe | A plain counter can double count retries |
| Profile fields | Merge independent fields | Same-field concurrent edit still conflicts |
| Document edits | Operational transform/CRDT or user merge | Ordering and intention preservation are hard |
| Bank balance | Do not LWW balances; store ordered ledger entries | Requires invariant-preserving transaction/order |
| Inventory | Reservation/escrow or authoritative conditional decrement | Simple merge can oversell |

### 14.4 Compensation is sometimes the only honest answer

If an AP partition-time action cannot later be merged without breaking an invariant, the system may accept it provisionally and compensate:

- cancel one conflicting reservation;
- refund a payment;
- ask a user to choose;
- put an order into manual review.

Compensation has product and support costs. Mentioning it demonstrates that “eventual consistency” is a user workflow, not only a database setting.

## 15. Replication, partitioning/sharding, and caching together

A large service often looks like:

~~~text
                         shard router
                              |
             +----------------+----------------+
             |                                 |
          Shard 1                           Shard 2
     leader + followers                leader + followers
             |                                 |
      keys A through M                   keys N through Z

Nearby caches sit above the database and have their own staleness rules.
~~~

Important distinctions:

- **Sharding** scales total data/write load by assigning different keys to different groups.
- **Replication** protects and serves each shard through multiple copies.
- **Caching** creates another derived copy optimized for access, often with weaker freshness.

If shard metadata or the shard router is inconsistent, a client can write to the wrong owner or two owners. Therefore the **control plane** that maps shards to leaders often needs stronger coordination than the application data it routes.

## 16. Failure matrix: what actually happens?

Use this matrix to make a replication answer concrete.

| Failure | Async single leader | Majority/consensus leader | Multi-leader async | Leaderless/tunable |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| One follower dies | Leader continues; lag/catch-up later | Continues if quorum remains | Other leaders continue | Continues if required R/W can be met |
| Leader dies | Promote; possible acknowledged-write loss | Elect from quorum; brief unavailability | Other leaders already accept writes | No fixed leader; route around failed replica |
| Network isolates minority | Risk of unsafe manual promotion/split brain | Minority rejects strong writes | Both sides may accept; conflicts later | Depends on consistency level/strict vs sloppy quorum |
| Region-to-region link fails | Primary region may continue; remote copy lags | Side with quorum continues | Both regions continue locally; reconcile | Local levels may continue; global levels may fail |
| Slow replica | Async path mostly unaffected | Quorum can bypass it; tail latency if needed | Replication lag grows | Coordinator chooses enough fast replicas |
| Bad delete/corrupt write | Replicates the bad change | Replicates the bad change consistently | Replicates/conflicts | Replicates/converges |
| Long-offline replica returns | Log catch-up or snapshot | Validate term/log, then catch up | Must reconcile long divergence | Hints + repair/snapshot |

The last two rows are why backups, versioned history, fencing, and repair are not optional operational details.

## 17. Choosing a replication strategy

### 17.1 Start from the invariant, not the database brand

Ask:

1. What must never happen? Examples: negative stock, two owners of one lock, duplicate debit.
2. What staleness can users tolerate, and for how long?
3. During a region/network failure, should this operation fail, queue, serve stale data, or accept conflict?
4. Is traffic read-heavy or write-heavy?
5. Must every region accept writes locally?
6. Can concurrent updates be merged without losing intent?
7. What RPO and RTO are required?
8. What are the latency, cost, residency, and operational constraints?

### 17.2 Compact decision tree

~~~text
Does the operation protect a hard global invariant?
|
+-- yes --> Can ownership be partitioned so each key has one authority?
|           |
|           +-- yes --> leader/consensus per shard; quorum commit as required
|           |
|           +-- no  --> coordinate globally, escrow capacity, or reject during partition
|
+-- no --> Can concurrent updates be deterministically and safely merged?
            |
            +-- yes --> multi-leader or leaderless may fit
            |
            +-- no  --> single leader/home region, or surface conflict to user

Then choose ACK policy:
  required durability/RPO -> sync/quorum scope
  latency and failure isolation -> local ACK / async remote replication

Then choose read path:
  strong/authorization -> leader or quorum/linearizable read
  stale acceptable     -> local follower/cache
  session guarantee    -> version token or stickiness
~~~

### 17.3 Strategy comparison

| Strategy | Main strengths | Main costs | Use when | Avoid when |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Single leader, async followers** | Simple write order, low write latency, read scaling | Lag, possible failover loss, leader bottleneck | Conventional OLTP/read-heavy systems | Every region must write during isolation |
| **Single leader, synchronous/quorum** | Stronger durability/consistency, safer failover | Coordination latency, quorum unavailability | Payments, inventory, metadata, locks | Stale/mergeable workload values latency more |
| **Multi-leader** | Local writes in several regions; partition-time write availability | Conflicts, uniqueness/invariant complexity | Disjoint writers, offline collaboration, mergeable operations | Global non-mergeable invariants |
| **Leaderless/tunable** | High availability/scale; configurable R/W | Stale reads, repair and version complexity | Key-value/wide-column workloads with tolerant data semantics | Rich cross-record constraints/transactions dominate |
| **Local sync + remote async** | Strong local durability and latency; practical DR pattern | Remote staleness; possible regional-loss RPO | Multi-region services that can fail over with bounded loss | Required zero-RPO global failover |
| **Global consensus** | Strong ordered state across failures with quorum | WAN latency, minority unavailability, complexity | Globally correct metadata/transactions | Approximate or locally mergeable data |

### 17.4 Feature-level recommendation examples

| Feature | Reasonable starting point | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Payment ledger | Consensus/single authoritative leader per account shard; quorum durability | Preserve ordered, idempotent money movement |
| Product catalog description | Single leader plus async geo read replicas/cache | A brief stale description is usually tolerable |
| Last item reservation | Linearizable conditional write on authoritative shard | Prevent oversell |
| Social like events | Idempotent events, local acceptance, eventual aggregation | Mergeable and approximate display often acceptable |
| User presence | Local ephemeral state with TTL, AP-style | Freshness matters more than perfect history |
| Profile edit | Home leader or versioned optimistic concurrency | User expects read-your-writes; conflicts are possible |
| Analytics counters | Append/aggregate asynchronously | Throughput and availability dominate immediate exactness |
| Service-discovery ownership | Consensus/quorum | Two owners/routing maps can create widespread corruption |

These are starting points, not universal rules. Clarified product requirements can change any row.

## 18. Worked scenario 1: the last concert ticket

### 18.1 Requirement

Only one seat A-10 exists. Never confirm it for two users.

### 18.2 Unsafe AP design

~~~text
Initial: seat A-10 = available in both regions

Region India                              Region US
Alice reserves A-10                      Bob reserves A-10
returns success             X            returns success

After merge: two promises, one physical seat
~~~

The replicas may eventually converge to one owner, but one customer already received a false confirmation. Eventual convergence does not repair the business promise.

### 18.3 CP/authoritative design

Map A-10 to one shard leader backed by a majority:

~~~text
Alice ----\
           --> Reservation service --> seat-shard leader --> quorum
Bob ------/                                 |
                                      atomic condition:
                                      if available, claim;
                                      else reject
~~~

During a partition, a side unable to reach the authoritative quorum cannot confirm. It can:

- show “temporarily unable to reserve”;
- accept a non-confirming wait-list intent;
- retry with an idempotency key;
- route to the quorum side if latency permits.

### 18.4 PACELC reading

- **P:** choose consistency over accepting reservations on both sides.
- **E:** quorum coordination adds latency, but the invariant justifies it.

### 18.5 Alternative: escrow/pre-allocation

For fungible capacity rather than a named seat, allocate disjoint regional quotas:

~~~text
Global stock 100
India escrow: 40
US escrow:    40
Reserve:      20
~~~

Each region can safely sell within its local allocation without coordination. Rebalancing allocations needs coordination. This does not work for one globally unique seat, but it shows how changing the data model can shrink the CAP-sensitive part.

## 19. Worked scenario 2: social likes and counters

### 19.1 Requirements

- a like tap should feel responsive;
- temporary count error is acceptable;
- the same user should not be counted repeatedly because of retries;
- all replicas should converge.

### 19.2 Availability-oriented design

~~~text
Client --like(post=42, user=7, operation-id=abc)--> local region
                  |
                  +--> durable event/log
                  +--> local count/materialized view
                  +--> async replicate/aggregate globally
~~~

Use an idempotent identity such as <code>(post_id, user_id)</code> or an operation ID so retries do not blindly increment. Regions can merge a set/event stream and derive the count.

### 19.3 CAP/PACELC reading

- **P:** local regions may accept mergeable like intent, preferring availability.
- **E:** local acknowledgement/display favors latency; global counts converge later.
- The API might display “you liked this” immediately from client/session state while the global count lags.

### 19.4 Edge cases

- like then unlike during a partition;
- duplicate/reordered events;
- deleted post receiving delayed likes;
- abuse detection needing a more authoritative view;
- count recomputation after a bug;
- a celebrity post creating a hot key.

The exact CRDT/event model is a later topic, but the key lesson is to store mergeable intent rather than only overwriting a shared integer.

## 20. Worked scenario 3: bank ledger versus balance display

Treat two different operations separately.

### 20.1 Authoritative money movement

~~~text
Transfer request with idempotency key
       |
       v
Authoritative account/ledger shard
       |
atomic debit + credit or durable transaction workflow
       |
quorum/commit before confirmed success
~~~

Prefer consistency. If the client times out, the outcome may be unknown, so it retries with the same idempotency key and queries status; transport timeout does not prove failure.

### 20.2 Derived balance/statement view

A read-optimized statement or analytics view can lag if it is explicitly not used to authorize spending:

~~~text
committed ledger --> async projection --> statement/read replica
~~~

This illustrates feature-level CAP:

- ledger mutation: CP/strong path;
- derived display: possibly highly available stale path;
- UI should label pending transactions when relevant.

Never use the stale projection for the hard “may this account spend?” decision.

### 21.1 Amazon Dynamo — source-era production example (2007)

Amazon’s Dynamo paper describes an internal highly available key-value store for selected core services. - Amazon Dynamo (the 2007 internal system) and the current AWS service DynamoDB must not be treated as identical products.

### 21.2 Netflix active–active — source-era production example (2013)

Netflix publicly described an active–active multi-region architecture in 2013 with:

- local in-region request paths;
- asynchronous cross-region data replication;
- Cassandra multi-datacenter replication;
- split-brain exercises that severed regional connectivity;
- local consistency levels chosen for the workload.

See Netflix’s [“Active-Active for Multi-Regional Resiliency”](https://medium.com/netflix-techblog/active-active-for-multi-regional-resiliency-c47719f6685b).

Users East --> services East --> Cassandra replicas East
                                  |
                              async cross-region
                                  |
Users West --> services West --> Cassandra replicas West
~~~

Interview lesson:

- removing cross-region calls from the serving path lowers latency and isolates failures;
- the price is cross-region staleness and explicit convergence testing;
- chaos testing validates behavior that an architecture diagram only claims.

This is a source-era description, not a claim that every Netflix dataset then or now uses the same policy.

Twitter described Manhattan as an eventually consistent, multi-region distributed database whose default emphasized availability and low latency, with strong consistency available for selected operations. Public material describes reconciliation, read repair, hinted handoff, and an ordered replicated-log path for operations such as compare-and-set:

Selected CAS path:         coordinator --> ordered replicated log
                                             |
                                             +--> replicas apply in order

### 21.4 YouTube/Vitess lineage — public technology example

Vitess originated at YouTube, but these notes do not assert YouTube’s current private topology. Current Vitess documentation says each shard uses normal MySQL primary-to-replica replication; asynchronous replication is the default, while semi-synchronous replication can be configured for stronger failover durability ([Vitess replication FAQ](https://vitess.io/docs/faq/getting-started/overview/how-do-vitess-replicas-stay-in-sync-do-replicas/)).

                  Vitess routing
                 /              \
        shard A primary       shard B primary
          /       \             /       \
      replica   replica      replica   replica

Interview lesson:

- sharding distributes write ownership;

### 21.5 Uber trip service — illustrative design

This is **not** a claim about Uber’s current implementation:

~~~text
Critical trip state:
REQUESTED -> MATCHED -> PICKED_UP -> COMPLETED
        authoritative leader/transaction per trip

Frequent driver locations:
location events -> local ingestion -> latest-location views
        tolerate coalescing/staleness; newer data supersedes old
~~~

- Two drivers must not both become the authoritative match: use a conditional/transactional state transition.
- A late GPS point should not block a newer point: order by event/version and discard/coalesce stale updates.
- Rider map rendering may tolerate slight staleness; fare/payment state should not.

Uber publicly described its Docstore database in 2021 as providing strict serializability at the partition level, which reinforces the general lesson that guarantee scope matters ([Uber Engineering](https://www.uber.com/en-US/blog/schemaless-sql-database/)). It does not verify the illustrative trip flow above.

### 21.6 WhatsApp chat — illustrative design

This is **not** a claim about WhatsApp’s private storage:

~~~text
Send message:
client operation ID --> conversation/home shard --> durable ordered message ID
                                              |
                                              +--> async fan-out/device delivery

Presence/typing:
ephemeral local state + TTL --> best-effort propagation
~~~

- Durable message history needs idempotency, per-conversation ordering rules, offline delivery, and reconciliation.
- Presence and typing indicators can prefer availability/freshness and expire rather than demand a global order.
- Delivery/read receipts are separate state machines; they should not be inferred solely from a network ACK.

Interview lesson: “chat” is not one consistency requirement. Message durability, ordering, presence, group membership, and receipts can each choose differently.

## 22. Common mistakes and corrections

### Mistake 1: “CAP says pick any two of C, A, and P”

**Correction:** During a partition, a partitionable replicated system cannot guarantee both linearizability and CAP availability for every affected operation. While healthy, it can normally provide both; PACELC discusses the normal latency/consistency trade-off.

### Mistake 2: “We choose not to be partition tolerant”

**Correction:** The network can fail anyway. You can assume partitions outside a narrow fault model, but then behavior under a real partition is undefined or unavailable; you have not stopped the partition.

### Mistake 3: “Availability means returning any response, including an error”

**Correction:** CAP availability requires the operation to terminate with a response allowed by the service interface; rejecting every isolated request with a server error is not preserving the intended availability property.

### Mistake 4: “Consistency means every replica has the same bits at the exact same instant”

**Correction:** CAP uses atomic/linearizable behavior: operations appear to act on one copy in a real-time-compatible order. Replication can be in flight internally while the externally observable contract remains linearizable.

### Mistake 5: “Eventual consistency means the data updates in a few seconds”

**Correction:** Eventual convergence has no universal fixed bound. State a bounded-staleness SLO separately if one is required.

### Mistake 6: “SQL is CP and NoSQL is AP”

**Correction:** Data model/query language does not determine CAP behavior. Configuration, operation, topology, read/write concern, and failure mode do.

### Mistake 7: “R + W > N guarantees strong consistency”

**Correction:** It guarantees set intersection under strict fixed-set assumptions. Linearizability additionally needs correct version/order, handling of concurrency and incomplete operations, membership safety, and appropriate read/write algorithms.

### Mistake 8: “Three replicas means no data loss”

**Correction:** Ask when writes are acknowledged, whether copies are durable, where they are placed, whether failure is correlated, and whether corruption/deletion is replicated. Keep backups.

### Mistake 9: “Async replication is eventually consistent, so it is always AP”

**Correction:** A single-leader system can reject writes on an isolated follower and therefore act CP for writes, while still offering stale AP-style follower reads. Topology and ACK timing do not alone assign one CAP label.

### Mistake 10: “Multi-leader removes the single point of failure”

**Correction:** It adds write entry points but introduces conflict, split-brain, uniqueness, ordering, and recovery complexity. Those can be more dangerous than a brief failover.

### Mistake 11: “Read replicas safely scale every read”

**Correction:** Authorization, inventory, locks, and read-after-write paths can be wrong on a lagging replica. Classify reads before routing them.

### Mistake 12: “Last-write-wins resolves the conflict”

**Correction:** It makes replicas converge by discarding versions; it may not preserve user intent or invariants.

### Mistake 13: “A network timeout means the write failed”

**Correction:** The write may have committed and only the response was lost. Retry with an idempotency key or check operation status.

### Mistake 14: “Replication and backup are the same”

**Correction:** Replication copies current changes—including bad ones. A backup preserves recoverable history in an independent fault domain.

### Mistake 15: “PACELC says latency and consistency are always exact opposites”

**Correction:** It highlights a central replication trade-off, not a claim that every optimization weakening latency must weaken consistency. Better protocols, locality, hardware, batching, and data modeling can improve the frontier; distant coordination still has a non-zero cost.

## 24. Answer spines: cues to reconstruct answers live

### 24.1 “Explain CAP theorem”

Use **S-P-C-R**:

1. **S — Scope:** replicated shared data; CAP C is linearizability, not ACID C.
2. **P — Partition:** required replicas cannot exchange messages.
3. **C — Choice:** reject/wait for consistency, or respond/accept divergence for availability.
4. **R — Recovery:** reconcile, repair, compensate, and restore normal behavior.

Answer spine:

> “CAP applies to replicated shared state when the network partitions. Because one side cannot know what happened on the other, the system cannot guarantee both linearizable behavior and a successful response for every request. For an inventory reservation I would preserve consistency and reject the side without quorum; for a mergeable like event I might keep accepting locally and reconcile. The choice is per operation, and the design must include recovery after communication returns.”

### 24.2 “Why is ‘pick two’ misleading?”

Cues:

> P is a failure condition → forced A/C choice only while partitioned → normal system can offer C+A → choices vary per operation/data → properties and mitigations have degrees.

### 24.3 “Explain PACELC”

Use the acronym itself:

- **P:** if partitioned;
- **A/C:** choose availability or consistency;
- **E:** else, during normal operation;
- **L/C:** choose lower latency or stronger consistency.

Answer spine:

> “CAP focuses on the failure window. PACELC adds the everyday replication cost: even while healthy, a write that waits for remote replicas has stronger consistency but pays coordination latency; a local acknowledgement is faster but permits lag. I would state which replicas are on the write/read path instead of merely labeling the database.”

### 24.4 “Choose a replication strategy”

Use **P-L-A-C-E**:

1. **P — Purpose and invariants:** why replicate, and what must never happen?
2. **L — Leadership:** single, multi, leaderless, or consensus per shard?
3. **A — Acknowledgement and placement:** sync/async/quorum; zones/regions.
4. **C — Consistency/read path:** leader, local follower, quorum, session guarantees.
5. **E — Exceptions:** leader loss, partition, lag, conflict, repair, RPO/RTO.

Answer spine:

> “For orders I need durable, unique state transitions, so I would use one consensus-backed leader per order shard, replicate across three failure domains, and acknowledge after a majority durably records the change. Correctness-sensitive reads go to the leader or a linearizable read path; selected history views can use followers. I would define promotion and fencing, use idempotency for uncertain retries, monitor lag, and keep cross-region backups or replication according to the RPO.”

### 24.5 “Single leader versus multi-leader versus leaderless”

Cues:

- single: simple order/constraints; leader bottleneck/failover/lag;
- multi: local writes/partition availability; conflicts/invariants;
- leaderless: tunable quorum/high availability; reconciliation/repair;
- conclude from workload, not fashion.

### 24.6 “How do you handle replication lag?”

Use **S-R-V-M**:

1. **S — Scope:** which reads may be stale?
2. **R — Route:** leader, session-pinned, or acceptable follower?
3. **V — Version:** carry minimum log/version token; wait or reroute.
4. **M — Monitor:** time/offset lag, promotion eligibility, alerting.

### 24.7 “What happens when the leader fails?”

Cues:

> detect/suspect → elect/promote caught-up replica → majority/term → fence old leader → update routing → handle in-flight unknown results with idempotency → catch up recovered node → state possible RPO.

### 24.8 “Explain quorum replication”

Cues:

> N copies → W write ACKs → R read responses → strict-set overlap if W + R > N → newest/version reconciliation → formula is not a complete linearizability proof → sloppy quorum and membership caveats.

### 24.9 “Which side should a rate limiter choose?”

Cues:

- safety/security limiter: fail closed or strong centralized/partitioned counters;
- protective best-effort limiter: local counters and fail open may preserve service;
- global exact limit costs coordination;
- local sub-quotas/escrow can avoid global request-path coordination;
- replication lag permits bounded over-admission; quantify it.

### 24.10 “How would you answer the write-heavy quiz-system follow-up?”

Cues:

1. Separate question delivery, answer submission, live leaderboard, and final score.
2. Durable/idempotent append for answer submissions.
3. Partition by quiz/user to scale writes.
4. Async projection for a slightly stale live leaderboard.
5. Strong/finalized computation for official score and tie rules.
6. State partition behavior: accept submissions locally only if duplicate/order semantics remain safe, otherwise queue with timestamp/sequence policy.

This avoids the weak answer “use Cassandra because it is write-heavy.” The data model and correctness rules determine the choice.

## 25. Interview follow-up drills with model reasoning

### Drill A: “Can a CP system be highly available?”

Yes in normal operation and for failures that leave a quorum. “CP” means it refuses unsafe work during a partition, not that it is usually down. Replication, fast election, multi-zone placement, and graceful degradation can make practical availability high. It still cannot guarantee CAP availability on every side of a partition while preserving linearizability.

### Drill B: “Can a system be both strongly consistent and low latency?”

Within one region or locality, often yes enough for the SLO. Across distant regions, a write that needs remote coordination pays network delay. Shard leadership near the writer, locality groups, batching, read-only snapshots, and data modeling can reduce cost, but they do not eliminate the information-transfer requirement.

### Drill C: “If W = 2 succeeded, is the write durable?”

Ask what an ACK means. Were two independent replicas durably flushed, merely holding data in memory, or only receiving it into a relay buffer? Are both in one failure domain? Can a later election select a replica without that write? Durability follows protocol semantics, not the number alone.

### Drill D: “Why not always read from the leader?”

Leader reads simplify freshness, but:

- concentrate load;
- add geographic latency;
- reduce read availability if the leader is unreachable.

Use them for correctness-sensitive paths; followers/caches can serve explicitly stale or version-bounded views.

### Drill E: “Why not always use global consensus?”

It gives a powerful abstraction but costs cross-region latency, quorum dependency, operational complexity, and often write throughput per leader/shard. Mergeable data and local availability may not need that cost. Use the weakest model that safely satisfies the invariant—while being honest about application complexity.

### Drill F: “What if the client retries after a timeout?”

The original may have committed. Assign an idempotency key, persist the operation/result atomically with the business effect, and return the stored result for duplicates. Consensus or TCP does not itself give exactly-once business execution.

### Drill G: “Does eventual consistency always improve availability?”

Not automatically. A poorly placed cluster, unavailable coordinator, overloaded nodes, or required consistency level can still reject work. Eventual consistency makes certain non-coordinating behaviors possible; the complete architecture determines actual availability.

## 26. Compact review sheet

### 26.1 One-screen mental model

~~~text
Replication gives copies.
Copies create coordination and conflict questions.

CAP:
  During a network partition:
    preserve C -> reject/wait on unsafe side
    preserve A -> respond/accept possible divergence

PACELC:
  If P: A or C
  Else: lower L or stronger C

Write ownership:
  single leader -> simple order, failover/lag
  multi-leader  -> local writes, conflicts
  leaderless    -> tunable quorum, repair/reconcile
  consensus     -> majority-ordered log, minority stops

ACK policy:
  async -> lower latency, lag/RPO risk
  sync/quorum -> stronger durability/consistency, coordination cost

Always state:
  invariant + read path + write ACK + placement + failure + recovery
~~~

### 26.2 Vocabulary corrections

| Instead of saying | Say |
|---|---|
| “Pick two of CAP” | “During a partition, this operation must choose linearizability or CAP availability” |
| “This database is AP” | “With this read/write configuration and failure, this operation keeps serving with possible staleness” |
| “Replica means backup” | “Replica supports live availability; independent versioned backups support recovery” |
| “Quorum guarantees consistency” | “Quorum intersection is one required mechanism; ordering/version/membership semantics complete the guarantee” |
| “Eventual means five seconds” | “Eventual guarantees convergence; bounded staleness needs an explicit SLO” |
| “Multi-master solves HA” | “Multi-leader improves local write availability but requires conflict and invariant design” |
| “Follower ACK means readable” | “Define whether ACK means received, durably flushed, committed, or applied” |

### 26.3 Metrics to mention

- read/write latency, especially p95/p99;
- replication/apply lag;
- quorum/leader availability;
- failover duration;
- rejected operations during partition;
- conflict/sibling rate;
- repair backlog and repair age;
- RPO and RTO;
- stale-read or session-guarantee violations;
- cross-region bandwidth and storage cost.

## 27. Say-out-loud 30-second narrative

> “Replication keeps several copies for availability, durability, read scale, and geographic locality, but those copies must agree. CAP says that when a network partition prevents coordination, an operation cannot guarantee both linearizable consistency and a successful response everywhere: I either reject unsafe work or keep serving and reconcile divergence. PACELC adds that even when healthy, waiting for more or distant replicas trades latency for consistency. I choose single-leader, multi-leader, leaderless, or consensus replication from the business invariant, then specify replica placement, acknowledgement quorum, read path, failover fencing, lag handling, conflict resolution, and recovery. The choice is per operation—for example, consistent inventory but eventually aggregated likes—not one label for the whole product.”
